## Cold opens / hook candidates

**The well-house, March 3, 1887.** Helen Keller at Tuscumbia. Sun on the porch, honeysuckle, Anne Sullivan's hand under the spout, "w-a-t-e-r." Reusable for any chapter on language acquisition, equivalence, the moment a sign attaches to a referent, or scene-vs-summary pedagogy. The Project Gutenberg source is open and citation-clean.

**Mr. Auld's prohibition, Baltimore, c. 1827.** Douglass overhearing his enslaver tell Mrs. Auld why teaching a slave to read is dangerous. The hinge moment is *overhearing*, not learning. Reusable for chapters on rhetorical force, the politics of literacy, ethos through specific dialogue, or any argument about who gets to be a knower.

**Tara Westover's brother Tyler walking off the mountain.** The hinge is someone *else's* hinge becoming yours. Reusable for chapters on community vs. individual literacy, role models, or the slow-rotation kind of turning point.

**Malala's "Tuesday, 9 October 2012."** One eighteen-word sentence doing all five narrative jobs at once. Reusable as the canonical example of compression — a paragraph on compression in any genre chapter could open with this sentence and unpack it.

## Specification moves

**The widening of "literacy."** UNESCO 2021 → NCTE digital-age statement → the example list (text messaging, cooking, code, Discord, TikTok). Reusable any time the workshop needs to demonstrate the move of pulling a vague modern term apart by showing what it *used to mean* and why it had to stretch.

**Idiolect, from Greek *idios* + *lect*.** Etymology + plain-English gloss + permission-slip implication (Anzaldúa). Reusable for any chapter on voice, audience, register, code-switching.

**Rhetorical situation as a seven-element map.** Author / message / audience / purpose / means / context / culture. Reusable as a template for any genre chapter that asks the student to plan before drafting.

## The central distinction

**Scene vs. summary.** *Scene buys you presence at the cost of generalization. Summary buys you generalization at the cost of presence.* The Keller ratio: seven years in fifty words; five seconds in several hundred. Reusable in: Memoir, Profile, Case Study, Narrative Argument, anywhere the writer must decide what to render vs. what to compress.

**The "I have always been a reader" trap.** Six summary sentences as a failed opening, rewritten as one scene with *A Wrinkle in Time*. Reusable as a teaching example in any revision chapter — the same diagnosis applies to most over-summarized first paragraphs across genres.

**The object-anchor technique.** When a scene won't assemble, pick any concrete object that was present, describe it sensorily in three sentences, then drop yourself into that object's vicinity in time. Memory is associative. Reusable for any writing chapter where students struggle to find specificity.

## Scale shifts

**Cosmic ↔ intimate at the well-house.** "The mystery of language was revealed to me" → one six-year-old's hand under a spout. Reusable as the canonical example of intimate-to-universal scale.

**Westover, mountain to PhD.** The Idaho mountain → three children with doctorates. Reusable for any chapter on the long arc of literacy, or on what schooling does *to* family alongside what it does *for* the student.

## Worked techniques

**Sentence combining example pairs.** *1a/1b* and *2a/2b* from the source (m00034), preserved verbatim. Reusable in any chapter that needs to demonstrate sentence-level revision.

**The "Omit needless words" reduction.** 49 words → 16 → 8 → 4. Strunk-derived. Reusable in every revision discussion in the book.

**Define *you* and *this*.** Search-and-resolve technique. Reusable in every chapter's revision section.

## Trade-offs named

- Scene vs. summary (the central trade-off of this chapter).
- Authentic idiolect vs. wide audience reach.
- Rendering in the moment vs. honest acknowledgment of memory's unreliability.
- LLM as interlocutor (productive) vs. LLM as ghostwriter (corrosive in this genre).
