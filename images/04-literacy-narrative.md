# Image briefs — Chapter 4: Literacy Narrative

*Hero image and in-chapter figure briefs. Each brief includes: where it sits, what it shows, what it has to *do* pedagogically (not just decoratively), and a caption draft.*

---

## Hero image (chapter opener)

**Where:** Top of chapter, above title block.

**What it shows:** The well-house at Ivy Green, Tuscumbia, Alabama — the actual well where Helen Keller learned the word *water* on March 3, 1887. Period photograph if available; modern photograph of the preserved site if not.

**What it has to do:** Place the reader *at* the well before reading the first sentence. The chapter opens at this site; the image and the text should both put the reader on that ground. Not a portrait of Keller herself — too biographical, too iconic, too easy to skip past. The well-house is more specific.

**Caption draft:** *The well-house at Ivy Green, Tuscumbia, Alabama. Helen Keller learned the word "water" here on the afternoon of March 3, 1887. The site is preserved as a historic landmark.*

**Source candidate:** Library of Congress, Helen Keller Archive, or the [Ivy Green museum site](https://www.helenkellerbirthplace.org/). Verify rights before publication. `[verify]`

## Figure 4.1 — Frederick Douglass, frontispiece from the 1845 *Narrative*

**Where:** §5, opening the Douglass subsection.

**What it shows:** The original frontispiece engraving from the first edition of *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave* (Anti-Slavery Office, 1845). Douglass aged 27.

**What it has to do:** Anchor the reader in the historical specificity of the 1845 publication. The engraving is a primary visual document; pairing it with the Mr. Auld scene shows the reader the face of the writer who *made the moment public*.

**Caption draft:** *Frederick Douglass, frontispiece from the 1845 first edition of his Narrative. The image was deliberately included to refute the contemporary claim that no enslaved person could have written the book attributed to him.*

**Source candidate:** [Library of Congress, Douglass Papers](https://www.loc.gov/collections/frederick-douglass-papers/). Public domain.

## Figure 4.2 — Story-arc diagram

**Where:** §3, after the five-element discussion.

**What it shows:** A clean diagram of the classic story arc — exposition → rising action → climax → falling action → resolution — with the *hinge moment* visually marked at the climax position, and a secondary line showing where summary compresses (the long sections before the climax) versus where scene expands (around the climax itself).

**What it has to do:** Give the reader a visual handle for the scene-vs-summary distinction. Specifically: show that scene zooms *in* near the hinge, and summary covers the long lead-up.

**Caption draft:** *The literacy narrative's pacing: summary covers the lead-up; scene expands around the hinge moment; falling action and resolution close at a moderate pace.*

**Source candidate:** Original diagram. Create as SVG.

## Figure 4.3 — Scene vs. summary side-by-side

**Where:** §4, after the *A Wrinkle in Time* example.

**What it shows:** Two boxes side by side. Left box: the failed "I have always been a reader" opening. Right box: the rewritten *A Wrinkle in Time* scene. Annotation arrows between them point to specific words that change — *summarized timeframe* on the left becomes *specific moment* on the right; *generic descriptors* becomes *sensory anchors*; etc.

**What it has to do:** Make the revision move visible at a glance. The same writer, the same source memory, two very different paragraphs. The reader should see the moves.

**Caption draft:** *The same writer's literacy narrative opening, before and after a revision pass. The right-hand version anchors in a single moment with sensory detail; the left covers twenty years in summary.*

**Source candidate:** Original layout. HTML or InDesign.

## Figure 4.4 — The seven-element rhetorical-situation map

**Where:** §6, after the introduction to the assignment.

**What it shows:** A clean visual organizer of the seven rhetorical-situation elements (author / message / audience / purpose / means / context / culture) arranged around a central node labeled "your literacy narrative." Each element has 2–3 prompting questions adjacent to it.

**What it has to do:** Replace the awkward seven-row table that the OpenStax source uses. Same information, but visually accessible — a student should be able to scan it once and use it to plan.

**Caption draft:** *Mapping the rhetorical situation before drafting. Each element corresponds to a question you should be able to answer in one or two sentences before you begin writing.*

**Source candidate:** Original. Design as a single-page handout the student can also print.

## Figure 4.5 — Anzaldúa, *Borderlands / La Frontera* book cover

**Where:** §2, near the Anzaldúa quotation.

**What it shows:** Cover of *Borderlands / La Frontera: The New Mestiza* (Aunt Lute Books, 1987 first edition).

**What it has to do:** Make Anzaldúa a real published writer in the student's mind, not a name on a page. The cover is iconic enough to function as a memory anchor.

**Caption draft:** *Gloria Anzaldúa's* Borderlands / La Frontera *(1987) refuses to translate itself between English, Spanish, Tex-Mex, and Nahuatl. The book is a permission slip for the literacy narrative to be written in the writer's own idiolect.*

**Source candidate:** Aunt Lute Books cover image, licensed. `[verify]`

## What we are NOT including

- A portrait of Helen Keller. Too iconic; the chapter wants the well, not the face.
- A portrait of Frederick Douglass as a public figure (post-1850 engravings). The 1845 frontispiece does specific argumentative work — the later images don't.
- Generic stock photography of "people reading." Empty calories. Cut.
- A photograph of a TED stage. The TED Talk reference in the source is a minor footnote in §6; doesn't need an image.
