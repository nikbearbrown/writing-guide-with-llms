# Chapter 13 — Reasoning Strategies

*The Six Moves Underneath Every Argument You'll Ever Make.*

---

Sometime in the fourth century BCE, in the city of Athens, Plato sat down to describe an imaginary cave.

Inside the cave are prisoners who have been chained from childhood, facing a wall. They cannot turn their heads. Behind them, somewhere they cannot see, there is a fire. Between the fire and the prisoners, people walk by carrying objects. The objects cast shadows on the wall the prisoners are facing.

The prisoners take the shadows for reality. They name them. They develop expertise in predicting their movements.

One prisoner is released. He turns, sees the fire, sees the objects casting the shadows. He is forced out of the cave entirely, into sunlight. The sun is painful. He realizes the world he had inhabited was an artifact of the cave — a projection, not the thing itself.

He goes back to tell the others. They do not believe him.[^1]

[^1]: Plato, *The Republic*, Book VII, "The Allegory of the Cave," trans. B. Jowett. Available via [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/1497/1497-h/1497-h.htm#link2H_4_0010).

I want to point at something specific about what Plato is doing here, because it is easy to miss.

He is not writing fiction. He is not telling a story for its own sake. He is making a philosophical argument about the nature of knowledge — about how the unexamined mind mistakes its representations for reality, and about what it costs to leave that comfortable confusion behind. The argument is difficult. The concept it is trying to convey — the distinction between appearances and forms, between the world as we encounter it and the world as it actually is — is genuinely hard to think about directly.

So Plato does something clever. He finds a different situation — prisoners in a cave, shadows on a wall — that shares the relevant *structure* with the philosophical situation he wants you to understand. The prisoners are to the shadows as the unexamined mind is to its representations. The fire is to the real objects as the real objects are to their deeper nature. The man who escapes the cave is to the others as the philosopher is to the multitude.

He is not saying the situations are the same in every respect. They obviously aren't. He is saying they share one specific structural relationship, and that structural relationship carries the argument. Once you feel the analogy operating, you can follow the philosophical point through it. You couldn't follow it as easily any other way.

That move — carrying an argument through structural similarity between two unlike things — is one of six reasoning strategies this chapter is going to take apart. You have used all six of them before, in your writing and in your thinking, without ever naming them. This chapter names them, shows how each one works, and gives you a way to use them deliberately rather than by accident.

---

## What a reasoning strategy is

A *reasoning strategy* is a structural pattern for organizing evidence into an argument. It is not the evidence — the facts, the data, the examples. It is not the conclusion. It is the *shape* of the relationship between the evidence and the conclusion.

The distinction matters because writing problems in argument usually are not evidence problems. You have the evidence. The question is how to organize it so the conclusion follows visibly. That is what the strategies do.

Here is a useful analogy — and I notice I just used one. The reasoning strategies are to writing what scales are to playing an instrument. You do not perform a scale for an audience. You use scales as the underlying structure on which something more complex is built. The audience hears the music. The scales are invisible in the final product. But without them, the music would not be possible.

The six strategies this chapter covers:

- **Analogy** — explaining the unfamiliar through the familiar.
- **Cause and effect** — naming why something happened or what its consequences are.
- **Classification and division** — grouping things into categories or breaking things into parts.
- **Comparison and contrast** — showing similarities and differences to clarify both subjects.
- **Problem and solution** — naming a difficulty and proposing a response.
- **Definition** — specifying what a contested term actually means.

Real arguments rarely use only one. A strong position argument might open with an analogy, use a definition to nail down a key term, build toward a conclusion through a cause-and-effect chain, and use problem-and-solution as the overall frame. The strategies are tools in the same toolbox. You use the one that fits the work you are trying to do.

## Analogy

The mechanism of an analogy: you take a situation the reader already understands and you map it onto a situation the reader does not yet understand, in order to transfer understanding from the first to the second.

The situations are unlike in most respects. That is not a problem. The analogy does not claim they are the same in most respects. It claims they share one specific structural relationship, and that the shared structure is the thing you need the reader to understand.

Plato's cave works because the relationship between a prisoner and their shadow-world — familiarity, expertise, comfort, and total dependence on something that is not the actual thing — is structurally identical to the relationship between an unexamined mind and its representations. The literal details are completely different. The structure is the same. That is the whole game.

When analogies work, they make genuinely difficult ideas tangible by giving the reader something concrete to hold. When they fail, they introduce confusion because the mapping doesn't hold — the reader follows the analogy into a corner where the two situations stop being structurally similar, and now the analogy has made the original concept harder to understand rather than easier.

The practical discipline has four parts.

First, pick an analog the reader already understands. This sounds obvious and is routinely violated. If you explain electrons by comparing them to water flowing through a pipe, you are assuming the reader understands water pressure, flow, and pipe dynamics. If they don't, the analogy fails at the first step. The analog must be more familiar than the subject.

Second, state the mapping specifically. Not *this is like that*. Say what the relationship is. *Just as the prisoners mistake shadows for the objects that cast them, a mind that never examines its own assumptions mistakes its representations of the world for the world itself.* The reader needs to be able to see the specific structural relationship you are claiming.

Third, name where the analogy breaks down. Every analogy has limits. The careful writer names them. The careless writer extends the analogy past the point where it holds, and a hostile reader finds the over-extension and uses it to dismiss the whole argument. Naming the limits preempts the dismissal and earns trust.

Fourth, stop the analogy when the mapping stops holding. The most common failure: a writer finds an analogy that works beautifully for one aspect of the argument and then forces every subsequent aspect through the same analogy, including aspects it was never designed to cover. The analogy becomes a trap rather than a tool.

## Cause and effect

Cause-and-effect reasoning identifies why something happened or what consequences follow from a particular event or condition. It is the most common reasoning strategy in policy writing, science writing, and journalism, because most of what those genres do involves explaining mechanisms.

In 2015, an oil pipeline ruptured near Refugio State Beach on the California coast. The spill released over 100,000 gallons of crude oil into the Pacific. Researchers at UC Davis documented the immediate consequences: 49 coastal birds, 25 sea lions, 6 elephant seals, all rescued and rehabilitated; many more uncounted.[^2] The cause-and-effect chain: a corroded pipeline section failed; oil spread along the coastline; wildlife in the path of the spread was poisoned and oil-coated; some animals died; some were rescued; the rescued animals required months of cleaning and rehabilitation; the broader ecosystem will take years to recover.

[^2]: Kat Kerlin, ["Wildlife Experience High Price of Oil," UC Davis, 2 June 2015](https://www.ucdavis.edu/news/wildlife-experience-high-price-oil/).

Notice what makes that a cause-and-effect chain and not a list of events. Each item causes the next. The structure is a chain of *becauses*. Remove any link and the chain either breaks or the reader cannot follow it.

Three distinctions that matter enormously in this strategy, and that public discourse routinely collapses.

The first is correlation versus causation. Two things can happen together without one causing the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in the summer. The sun causes both, through separate pathways. A student who notices that grades fell the same year phones were banned in class and concludes that the phone ban caused the grade drop is treating correlation as causation. The causal claim requires showing a mechanism, not just co-occurrence.

The second is proximate versus distal cause. The proximate cause of the Refugio spill is the pipeline rupture. A distal cause is the regulatory regime that allowed an aging pipeline to remain uninspected. Both are real causes. Both matter for different arguments. A proximate cause explains what happened. A distal cause often explains why it was allowed to happen. A strong cause-and-effect argument is explicit about which kind of cause it is invoking.

The third is necessary versus sufficient. A necessary cause is one without which the effect could not have occurred. A sufficient cause is one whose presence alone is enough to produce the effect. The pipeline corrosion was necessary for the spill — without it, the pipeline doesn't rupture — but it was not sufficient, because a pipeline can be corroded without rupturing if it is monitored and repaired. Many causes are necessary without being sufficient, and conflating the two produces arguments that look like they demonstrate more than they do.

These three distinctions are important because the genre of cause-and-effect reasoning is the most frequently misused in public discourse. The writer who can specify *which* kind of cause they are invoking is doing reasoning that most of what you read in a newspaper is not.

## Classification and division

Classification groups disparate things into a larger category. Division breaks a single thing into parts. They are inverse moves that share an underlying logic.

Classification is what biology does when it groups species into genera and genera into families. It is what a film critic does when they group movies into genres. It is what this chapter does when it groups six distinct reasoning patterns into one category called *reasoning strategies*.

Division is what an anatomy textbook does when it breaks the human body into systems. It is what a film analysis does when it breaks a movie into acts. It is what a project plan does when it breaks a project into phases.

Here is the thing about these strategies that is easy to miss. The categories are not given by nature. They are choices. And the choices are the argument.

The biological taxonomy — Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species — reveals certain relationships among living things and obscures others. A different classification system, organized around ecological role rather than morphology, would reveal different relationships. Neither is wrong. They are useful for different purposes. But whichever you use, the classification is a claim about which relationships matter.

For your own writing, the classification or division you choose needs to earn its keep. The test: does this grouping produce insight I could not have produced by treating the items separately, or by grouping them differently? If yes, the strategy is doing work. If no, you are just organizing the material, and organization without insight is not argument.

## Comparison and contrast

Comparison examines similarities. Contrast examines differences. Together they produce insight that neither subject examined alone would generate — because seeing what is alike and what is different between two things shows you something about both that you could not see by looking at either one independently.

The strategy is most powerful when the two subjects share a category — they are alike enough that the contrasts are meaningful — but differ within it in ways that illuminate what each is doing.

Consider Shakespeare's *Romeo and Juliet* alongside *Macbeth*. Both are tragedies. Both involve the death of the central characters. Both engage themes of fate, ambition, and human weakness. The similarities establish that comparison is legitimate — they belong in the same conversation. The contrasts are where the insight lives. *Romeo and Juliet*'s tragedy is driven largely by external forces: family feud, plague, a letter that doesn't arrive in time, a chain of misunderstandings. *Macbeth*'s tragedy is driven internally: the protagonist's own ambition and moral collapse. The same genre, the same ending, a completely different mechanism. Setting them side by side makes that difference visible in a way that examining either play alone would not.

Two practical points.

Choose subjects that share a category. Comparing things that have nothing in common produces no insight because the contrasts are obvious and the similarities don't exist. The interesting comparisons are between subjects that are similar enough for the differences to be revealing.

Organize by point or by subject. Point-by-point alternates between the two subjects for each feature under consideration — good for short comparisons. Subject-by-subject fully describes one subject before fully describing the other — sometimes better for long, complex comparisons where the reader needs full context on each subject before the contrasts land. The choice is architectural, not cosmetic. It changes what the reader holds in mind while reading.

## Problem and solution

Problem-and-solution is the structure the proposal genre runs on, but it appears far beyond proposals. Opinion writing, analytical reports, scientific papers, policy documents — wherever someone is arguing that a difficulty exists and that something should be done about it.

The move has four beats: name the difficulty, explain its causes and consequences, propose a response, evaluate whether the response actually addresses the difficulty.

The strategy's main failure mode is rushing to the solution before the problem has been adequately specified. A solution to an under-specified problem is a solution to nothing — it may address a symptom rather than a cause, or it may address the wrong version of the problem entirely. Most bad policy writing makes this error. The proposed solution is specific, detailed, and well-reasoned. The problem it is supposed to solve is stated at a level of generality so high that the solution cannot be evaluated against it.

The practical discipline: before you write the solution section, write one paragraph that specifies the problem precisely enough that the solution can be evaluated against it. What is the problem? Who does it affect, and how? What causes it? What would a successful solution look like — what would change? Once you can answer those questions, you know what the solution has to do. The solution then earns or does not earn the work it claims to be doing.

## Definition

The definition strategy specifies what a contested term means. Most consequential arguments rest on terms whose meanings are not settled, and the argument is often won or lost at the level of the term.

Consider *patriotism*. The dictionary gives you *love of one's country*. That is the denotation — the literal meaning. The connotation — the emotional and political freight the word carries — varies enormously by speaker, by moment, by context. Some use the word to mean support for existing leadership, particularly in times of crisis. Some mean active participation in the country's institutions and processes. Some mean specifically *critical* engagement — the claim that loving a country means demanding better of it, not deferring to it. These are not the same definition. They lead to different arguments. They authorize different actions.

A writer who uses *patriotism* without specifying which sense they mean is writing an argument that will be evaluated differently by every reader depending on which sense they bring to it. The argument will mean different things to different people, and no single reader will have encountered the argument the writer intended to make.

A strong definition argument has four moves.

Distinguish denotation from connotation. Name the literal meaning; then acknowledge the contested freight. The reader needs to know you know the word is contested.

Specify your working definition. *In this essay, I am using "patriotism" to mean...* The reader now knows what you mean. They can disagree with your definition — that is fine — but they cannot misunderstand it.

Defend the definition. Why is your definition the right one to use for this argument? What does it allow you to see that other definitions would obscure? What does it exclude that other definitions would include, and why is that exclusion justified?

Handle competing definitions fairly. Other definitions exist and have their own logic. The strongest writers acknowledge the alternatives and explain why their working definition is more useful for the particular argument at hand.

## Why the strategies work together

Reasoning strategies are most powerful when used in combination. A single argument might use all six, each doing specific structural work.

Here is a concrete example. Suppose you are arguing that high school students should be required to take a course in AI literacy. The argument might work like this.

Open with an analogy: just as twentieth-century high schools required driver's education because cars were becoming pervasive and dangerous to operate without instruction, twenty-first century high schools may need a required course in AI literacy because language models are becoming similarly pervasive in the spaces where students work and think.

Use definition to specify what AI literacy means for this argument — not how to write a prompt, but how to evaluate AI-generated output for accuracy, bias, and appropriate use.

Use cause-and-effect to explain what is currently happening in the absence of such a course: rising rates of students relying on AI-generated content without evaluating it, detectable in assignment quality and survey data, with downstream effects on the skills students are building.

Use comparison to draw on what is already known from other required courses — driver's education, sex education, civics — about what works and what doesn't when schools require instruction on something culturally contested.

Use problem-and-solution as the overall frame: here is the problem, here is the proposed response, here is why the response addresses the problem rather than some adjacent problem.

Use classification to organize what the proposed course would contain: three categories of AI literacy, each requiring different instruction and different assessment.

That is six reasoning strategies in one argument. None of them decorative. Each doing structural work. The argument would be weaker with any one removed. The Plato analogy with which we opened was already doing the same thing — carrying a difficult philosophical point through a structural similarity that makes it followable.

## A note about AI

The reasoning strategies are the dimension of writing where the language model is most likely to simulate the work without doing it.

Three places where the model is genuinely useful.

Identifying which strategies a draft uses: paste a draft and ask the model to identify every instance of each strategy and quote the specific passage. The model is mechanically good at this, and the inventory helps you see what you have done and what you have not.

Stress-testing an analogy: paste the analogy and ask where it breaks down — at what point does the mapping between the subject and the analog stop holding? The model often catches the failure modes you have not noticed because you are too close to the analogy.

Flagging weak causal reasoning: paste a cause-and-effect chain and ask where the chain treats correlation as causation, and where it conflates necessary and sufficient causes. The model is reasonably good at finding the soft spots, though it will occasionally flag reasoning that is actually sound.

Three places where the model does damage.

Asking it to improve the reasoning: the model will produce prose that looks more reasoned. The improvements often introduce qualifications or hedges that flatten the argument's specific commitments. Read every suggested change skeptically.

Asking it to generate analogies: the model produces analogies in abundance. Most are clichéd or generic. Genuine analogies come from the writer's specific knowledge of two domains. The model's analogies feel borrowed because they are.

Asking it to validate the reasoning: the model will sometimes confidently endorse a causal chain that is in fact flawed, particularly when the flaw is in the middle of a long chain where it is easy to overlook. Use the model to *interrogate* your reasoning, not to approve it.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 13

**What you're building:** a reasoning-strategy inventory and audit of a draft you have already written, so you can see your own reasoning patterns and choose more deliberately on the next draft.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm taking a college writing class. The class has just introduced six
reasoning strategies: analogy, cause and effect, classification and
division, comparison and contrast, problem and solution, and
definition.

Below I'm pasting a draft of a longer essay I've already written.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Identify every instance in this draft of each of the six reasoning
   strategies. For each instance, quote the specific passage and
   name which strategy it is. Be specific — don't conflate adjacent
   strategies.

2. For each instance, evaluate whether the strategy is doing real
   work in the argument or whether it is filler. A strategy is doing
   real work if removing the passage would weaken the argument. A
   strategy is filler if the passage could be cut without loss.

3. Identify three places in the draft where I COULD have used a
   reasoning strategy and didn't — where an argument would have been
   stronger if I had explicitly used analogy, classification, etc.

4. Identify any reasoning failures in the draft: places where I
   treated correlation as causation, where I forced an analogy beyond
   its useful range, where I used a term without defining it, where
   I claimed necessity when I had only shown sufficiency.

Do not rewrite the draft. Do not produce a revised version. The
revision is mine to do.

Here is the draft:
[paste your draft]
```

**What this produces:** an inventory of the reasoning strategies you have used, a critique of which are doing real work, suggestions for where you could have used more, and a list of specific reasoning failures. After this, you revise the draft yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

For ChatGPT or Gemini, the prompt works as written. For Cowork: useful if the draft is long — tell Cowork to read the file before the conversation. For Claude Code: not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model is sometimes overconfident about identifying causal failures. It will flag a cause-and-effect passage as treating correlation as causation when in fact you have provided enough evidence elsewhere in the essay to support the causal claim. Read the model's critique carefully and accept only the ones that hold against your full argument.
