# Chapter 13 — Reasoning Strategies

*The Six Moves Underneath Every Argument You'll Ever Make.*

A famous scene to begin.

Sometime in the fourth century BCE, in the city of Athens, Plato — a student of Socrates who is now writing his own philosophical work — sits down to describe an imaginary scene. In his book *The Republic*, he asks the reader to imagine a cave. Inside the cave are prisoners who have been chained from childhood facing a wall. They cannot turn their heads. Behind them, somewhere they cannot see, there is a fire. Between the fire and the prisoners, people walk by carrying objects. The objects cast shadows on the wall the prisoners are facing.

The prisoners, knowing nothing else, take the shadows for reality. They name the shadows. They debate them. They develop expertise in their movements.

One prisoner is released. He turns. He sees the fire and the objects and the people. The shadows are revealed as projections of something else. The prisoner is forced out of the cave entirely, into the sun. The sun is painful. The world he had known was an artifact of the cave.

He goes back into the cave to tell the others. They do not believe him.[^1]

[^1]: Plato, *The Republic*, Book VII, "The Allegory of the Cave," trans. B. Jowett. Available via [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/1497/1497-h/1497-h.htm#link2H_4_0010).

The Allegory of the Cave is one of the foundational texts of Western philosophy. It is also one of the most enduring examples of a single reasoning move — *analogy*. Plato is not just telling a story. He is mapping one situation onto another, where the relationship between the two situations carries the argument. The cave is to the philosopher's intellectual journey as the prison is to ignorance. The sun is to the form of the Good as truth is to comprehension. The reader is supposed to feel the analogy operating and to follow Plato's larger argument through it.

That move — *carrying meaning from one domain to another to clarify an unfamiliar idea* — is one of six reasoning strategies this chapter is going to take apart. The strategies recur in every kind of writing. You have already used several of them in chapters one through twelve without my naming them. This chapter names them, shows how each one works, and gives you a toolkit you can deploy deliberately.

There is no traditional assignment at the end of this chapter. Instead, the chapter asks you to revisit a draft you have already written — a position argument, an analytical report, a proposal — and identify which reasoning strategies you used, which you could have used, and which you missed.

## What "reasoning strategy" means here

A *reasoning strategy* is a structural pattern for organizing evidence into an argument. It is not the evidence. It is not the conclusion. It is the *shape* of how the evidence supports the conclusion.

The six this chapter covers:

1. **Analogy** — explaining the unfamiliar through the familiar.
2. **Cause and effect** — naming why something happened or what its consequences are.
3. **Classification and division** — grouping things into categories or breaking things into parts.
4. **Comparison and contrast** — showing similarities and differences to clarify either subject.
5. **Problem and solution** — naming a difficulty and proposing a response.
6. **Definition** — specifying what a contested term actually means.

Real arguments rarely use only one of these. A strong position argument might use a definition to clarify a key term, an analogy to make a difficult concept tangible, a cause-and-effect chain to explain why a policy is failing, and a problem-and-solution structure to organize the overall piece. The strategies are tools in the same toolbox. You pick the one that fits the work.

A useful frame: reasoning strategies are to writing what musical scales are to playing an instrument. You do not perform a scale for an audience. You use it as the underlying structure on which something more complex is built. The audience hears the music, not the scales. But the music would not be possible without them.

## Analogy

An *analogy* explains an unfamiliar subject by mapping it onto a familiar one. The subject and the analog are dissimilar in most ways. The point is that *in one specific way*, they are similar — and the similarity carries the explanation.

Plato's cave is an analogy because the situation of the prisoners — confused about what is real, taking shadows for substance, resistant to learning that the shadows are projections — maps onto the situation of the unphilosophical mind. The two situations are dissimilar in every literal respect. The mapping holds because the *structural relation* in each case is similar.

When analogies work, they make difficult ideas tangible by giving the reader something concrete to hold. When they fail, they introduce confusion by suggesting similarities that don't hold.

Some practical advice on using analogies.

**Pick an analog the reader already understands.** If your reader doesn't know what a cell membrane does, comparing electrons to people walking through a membrane is not going to help. The analog has to be familiar.

**State where the analogy holds, specifically.** Don't just gesture. Say what the mapping is. *Just as the prisoners in Plato's cave mistake shadows for reality, students who only encounter rhetoric through formal definitions miss the way rhetoric actually operates in their daily reading.* The mapping is named.

**State where the analogy breaks down.** Every analogy has limits. The careful writer names them. *The analogy holds in [these ways]; it does not hold in [these ways]; the limits matter because [reason].* Naming the limits earns trust.

**Avoid forcing the analogy beyond its useful range.** A common failure: the writer extends the analogy too far, applying every feature of the analog to the subject, including features that don't map. The reader, sensing the over-reach, loses trust in the parts of the analogy that did work.

## Cause and effect

*Cause-and-effect* reasoning identifies why something happens or what consequences follow from a particular event or condition. It is the most common reasoning strategy in policy writing, science writing, and journalism — because most of what those genres are doing involves explaining mechanisms.

A worked example, condensed. In 2015, an oil pipeline ruptured along the coast near Refugio State Beach in California. The spill released over 100,000 gallons of crude oil into the Pacific. Coastal wildlife was affected. Researchers at UC Davis documented the immediate consequences: 49 coastal birds, 25 sea lions, 6 elephant seals, all rescued and rehabilitated; many more uncounted.[^2] The cause-and-effect chain: a corroded pipeline section failed; the spill spread along the coast; the wildlife in the path of the spread was poisoned and oil-coated; some animals died; some were rescued; the rescued animals required months of cleaning and rehabilitation; the broader ecosystem will take years to recover.

[^2]: Kat Kerlin, ["Wildlife Experience High Price of Oil," UC Davis, 2 June 2015](https://www.ucdavis.edu/news/wildlife-experience-high-price-oil/).

The reasoning is not just a list of events. It is a chain of *becauses*. Each event causes the next. The argument is the chain.

Some practical advice.

**Distinguish correlation from causation.** Two things can happen together without one causing the other. The student writer most often errs by treating co-occurrence as causation. *The grades in this class are lower than last year, and this is also the year we banned phones in class.* The two facts are correlated. The causal claim requires more.

**Distinguish proximate from distal cause.** The proximate cause of the Refugio spill is the pipeline rupture. A distal cause might be the regulatory regime that allowed an aging pipeline to remain uninspected. Both are causes. Both matter. A strong cause-and-effect argument is clear about which it is invoking and why.

**Distinguish necessary from sufficient.** A necessary cause is one without which the effect could not have occurred. A sufficient cause is one whose presence is enough to produce the effect. Many causes are necessary without being sufficient — the pipeline rupture was necessary for the spill but not sufficient (the corrosion conditions had to exist too).

These distinctions matter because the genre of cause-and-effect reasoning gets misused constantly in public discourse. The writer who can specify *which kind of cause* is doing reasoning the average op-ed isn't.

## Classification and division

*Classification* groups disparate elements into larger categories. *Division* breaks a single subject into smaller parts. They are the inverse moves and they share a logic.

Classification is what biology does when it groups species into genera and genera into families. It is what a sports announcer does when they categorize a play as either offensive or defensive. It is what a textbook chapter does when it groups six reasoning strategies into one chapter.

Division is what an anatomy textbook does when it breaks the human body into systems. It is what a film analysis does when it breaks a movie into acts. It is what a project plan does when it breaks a project into phases.

Both moves require choices, and the choices are the argument. *Which categories you group things into determines what the categories reveal.* The biological taxonomy — Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species — reveals relationships that other classifications would have obscured. A classification of films by genre reveals one set of relationships; a classification by director reveals a different set; a classification by budget reveals a third.

For your own writing, the classification or division you use should be earning its keep. Ask: *does this grouping produce insight I could not have produced by treating the items separately, or by grouping them differently?* If yes, the strategy is doing work. If no, you are just organizing.

## Comparison and contrast

*Comparison* examines similarities. *Contrast* examines differences. Together they are one of the most useful reasoning strategies, especially when the writer has two subjects that are alike enough to make the contrasts meaningful.

A worked example. Comparing Shakespeare's *Romeo and Juliet* to his *Macbeth*. Both are tragedies. Both involve the death of the central characters by the end. Both engage themes of fate, ambition, and human weakness. The contrasts are where the analytical work lives: *Romeo and Juliet*'s tragedy is driven by external forces (family feud, plague, miscommunication); *Macbeth*'s tragedy is driven internally (the protagonist's own ambition and moral collapse). Setting these side by side illuminates what each play is doing in a way that examining either alone would not.

Some advice.

**Choose subjects with something in common.** Comparing things that have nothing in common produces no insight. The interesting comparisons are between subjects that share a category but differ within it.

**Organize point-by-point or subject-by-subject.** Point-by-point: alternate between the two subjects, addressing one feature at a time. Subject-by-subject: fully describe one subject, then fully describe the other. Both work; the choice depends on length and complexity. Point-by-point is better for short comparisons; subject-by-subject is sometimes better for long, complex pieces where the reader needs full context on each before the contrasts land.

**Use signal words deliberately.** *Similarly*, *likewise*, *in contrast*, *however*, *whereas* — these signal which kind of move you are making. Use them when the move would not be clear without them. Avoid them when the structure makes the move obvious.

## Problem and solution

*Problem-and-solution* is the structure I named in Chapter 8 as the proposal genre's spine. It also appears in many other contexts — opinion writing, analytical reports, scientific papers, policy writing.

The move: identify a difficulty, name its causes and consequences, propose a response, evaluate the response's likely effects.

A practical example, current. Social media platforms are circulating misinformation at high speed. The problem: false claims spread faster than corrections; the corrections rarely reach the same audience; the cumulative effect distorts public understanding of contested issues. One proposed solution: combine improved algorithmic flagging of suspect content with increased human moderator capacity to evaluate flagged items. The reasoning evaluates the solution against the problem: does it reduce the time false content remains live? Does it produce false positives that suppress legitimate speech? At what cost? With what unintended consequences?

The structure scales. A short essay might use it for a single problem-solution pair. A long-form policy report might use it for several nested problems and their proposed solutions.

The strategy's main pitfall: rushing to the solution before the problem has been adequately defined. A solution to an under-specified problem is a solution to nothing. Chapter 8 made the same point in the proposal context; the principle generalizes.

## Definition

The *definition* strategy specifies what a contested term means. Most consequential arguments rest on terms whose meanings are not settled. Specifying the term is often where the argument is won or lost.

Consider the word *patriotism*. The dictionary definition will tell you it is love of one's country. The contested meaning is where the work lives. Some argue patriotism means support for the country's leadership, particularly in moments of crisis. Some argue it means active participation in the country's institutions and processes. Some argue it means specifically *critical* engagement — that loving a country means demanding better of it, not deferring to it. These are different definitions. They lead to different arguments. They authorize different actions.

A strong definition argument does several things.

**Distinguishes denotation from connotation.** The denotation is the literal dictionary meaning. The connotation is the emotional or cultural baggage the word carries. *Patriotism* denotatively means love of country; connotatively, in particular times and places, it has carried implications of conformity, of dissent, of nationalism, of inclusion. A definition argument has to engage both layers.

**Specifies the writer's working definition.** Once you have established that the term is contested, commit to the meaning you are using. *In this essay, I am using "patriotism" to mean [specific working definition].* The reader knows what you mean. They can disagree with your definition, but they cannot misunderstand it.

**Defends the definition.** Why is your definition the right one to use for this argument? What does it allow you to see? What does it exclude that other definitions would have included?

**Handles competing definitions fairly.** Other definitions exist. The strongest writers acknowledge them and explain why their working definition is more useful for the particular argument at hand.

## The strategies in combination

Reasoning strategies are most powerful when used together. A specific example.

A position argument on the question of whether high school students should be required to take a course in artificial-intelligence literacy might:

- Open with an **analogy**: just as twentieth-century high school students were required to take a course in driver's education because cars were becoming pervasive, twenty-first century high school students may need a course in AI literacy because language models are becoming similarly pervasive.
- Use **definition** to specify what *AI literacy* means in the writer's argument: not how to write a prompt, but how to evaluate AI-generated output for accuracy, bias, and appropriate use.
- Use **cause-and-effect** reasoning to explain why the current absence of such a course is producing measurable consequences: rising rates of students relying on AI without evaluating outputs, demonstrated through specific surveys and observed classroom patterns.
- Use **comparison** to draw on what we know from existing required courses — driver's education, sex education, civics — for what works and what doesn't in requiring instruction on a culturally consequential topic.
- Use **problem-and-solution** as the overarching frame: here is the problem, here is the proposed response, here is why the response addresses the problem.
- Use **classification** to organize the proposed curriculum: the course covers three categories of AI literacy — evaluating output for factual accuracy, recognizing systemic biases in training data, identifying appropriate versus inappropriate uses.

That is six reasoning strategies in one argument. None of them are decorative. Each is doing structural work.

## A note about AI

The reasoning strategies are the dimension of writing where the language model is most likely to imitate structure without doing the inferential work the structure requires.

Useful moves:

*Identifying which strategies a draft uses.* Paste a draft of yours and ask: *Identify each instance of analogy, cause-and-effect, classification, comparison, problem-solution, and definition reasoning in this draft. For each, quote the specific passage.* The model is mechanically good at this. The inventory helps you see what you have done.

*Stress-testing an analogy.* Paste an analogy and ask: *Where does this analogy break down? At what point does the mapping between the subject and the analog stop holding?* The model often catches the failure modes you have not noticed.

*Spotting weak causal reasoning.* Paste a cause-and-effect chain and ask: *Where in this chain are we treating correlation as causation? Where could a hostile reader argue I have conflated necessary and sufficient causes?* The model is reasonably good at flagging the soft spots.

Damaging moves:

*Asking the model to "improve the reasoning."* The model will produce prose that *looks* more reasoned. The improvements often introduce qualifications or hedges that flatten the argument's specific commitments. Read every change skeptically.

*Asking the model to generate analogies.* The model produces analogies in abundance. Most of them are clichéd, generic, or misleading. Genuine analogies are hard. They come from the writer's specific knowledge of two domains. Borrowed analogies from a model carry less force.

*Asking the model to verify reasoning.* The model will sometimes confidently endorse reasoning that is in fact flawed, particularly when the flaw is in the chain of cause-and-effect. The fix is to use the model to *interrogate* the reasoning, not to validate it.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 13

**What you're building:** a reasoning-strategy inventory and audit of a draft you have already written, so you can see your own reasoning patterns and choose more deliberately on the next draft.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm taking a college writing class. The class has just introduced six
reasoning strategies: analogy, cause and effect, classification and
division, comparison and contrast, problem and solution, and
definition.

Below I'm pasting a draft of a longer essay I've already written.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Identify every instance in this draft of each of the six reasoning
   strategies. For each instance, quote the specific passage and
   name which strategy it is. Be specific — don't conflate adjacent
   strategies.

2. For each instance, evaluate whether the strategy is doing real
   work in the argument or whether it is filler. A strategy is doing
   real work if removing the passage would weaken the argument. A
   strategy is filler if the passage could be cut without loss.

3. Identify three places in the draft where I COULD have used a
   reasoning strategy and didn't — where an argument would have been
   stronger if I had explicitly used analogy, classification, etc.

4. Identify any reasoning failures in the draft: places where I
   treated correlation as causation, where I forced an analogy beyond
   its useful range, where I used a term without defining it, where
   I claimed necessity when I had only shown sufficiency.

Do not rewrite the draft. Do not produce a revised version. The
revision is mine to do.

Here is the draft:
[paste your draft]
```

**What this produces:** an inventory of the reasoning strategies you have used, a critique of which are doing real work, suggestions for where you could have used more, and a list of specific reasoning failures. After this, you revise the draft yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful if the draft is long. Tell Cowork to read the file before the conversation.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model is sometimes overconfident about identifying causal failures. It will flag a cause-and-effect passage as treating correlation as causation when in fact you have provided enough evidence in the broader essay to support the causal claim. Read the model's critique carefully and only accept the ones that hold against your full argument.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter takes the same reasoning strategies and integrates them with research methodology. You move from arguing from what you already know to arguing from what you have discovered through original investigation.

## What would change my mind

If a careful comparison of essays produced with explicit attention to reasoning strategies to essays produced without such attention showed that the explicit version produced *more mechanical* writing — flatter, more formulaic, less alive — I would revise the chapter's central pedagogical claim that naming the strategies improves the writing. The evidence from teaching practice is that the names help most when the writer treats them as diagnostic tools rather than as templates. The pedagogy stays defensible as long as that distinction is made.

## Still puzzling

The line between *useful structure that the writer can fill with their own thinking* and *constraining form that flattens the writer's voice* is hard to teach cleanly. Some of the strongest writers I know would deny that they think in terms of these six strategies. They would say they just write. But when you analyze their work, the strategies are there. Whether the names are useful pedagogically — whether students who learn the names write better — is a question I think the answer is yes to, but it is not as obvious a yes as it should be.
