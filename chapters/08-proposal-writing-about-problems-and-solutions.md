# Chapter 8 — The Proposal

*Making the Problem Specific Enough to Be Solved.*

A small story to start.

In 2001, Peter Pronovost — a critical-care doctor at Johns Hopkins — looked at a particular kind of hospital infection and noticed something that should have been obvious for decades and somehow wasn't. The infections were in patients who had received a central venous catheter, a long tube threaded into a major vein near the heart, used for medications and monitoring in intensive care units. The infections were common: one in twenty patients who received a central line developed one. They killed roughly fifteen thousand people a year in the United States. They were also, on paper, almost entirely preventable. There were five hand-washing-and-sterile-technique steps a doctor was supposed to follow when inserting the line. Following all five virtually eliminated the infection.

Doctors did not follow all five. Not because they didn't know the steps. Because the situations were urgent and the steps felt like ritual.

Pronovost made a checklist. Five items. He gave the list to nurses and gave the nurses permission to stop the procedure if any item was skipped. He ran the protocol in eight Michigan hospital ICUs.

The infection rate dropped 35 percent in three months, and stayed down. The result was published in the *New England Journal of Medicine*. Atul Gawande, writing about it in 2007 in the *New Yorker* and then in his 2009 book *The Checklist Manifesto*, made the broader point: the problem was not knowledge. The problem was that the application of knowledge fails predictably under pressure, and the fix is a piece of paper.[^1]

[^1]: Atul Gawande, *The Checklist Manifesto: How to Get Things Right* (Metropolitan Books, 2009). The original *New Yorker* piece was Atul Gawande, ["The Checklist," *New Yorker*, 10 Dec. 2007](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2007/12/10/the-checklist).

What Pronovost wrote in 2001, before all of that, was a proposal. Not in the formal sense — he was writing inside a medical research and quality-improvement framework. But the move is the move every proposal makes. He named a specific problem. He named who it affected and how. He proposed one specific intervention. He named the evidence that the intervention could work. He named the obstacles to adopting it. The whole structure of the proposal genre is in that sequence.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to write a proposal of your own — on a problem you can actually research, recommending a solution you can defend. The Pronovost example is the kind of clarity to aim for. Not the scale. The specificity.

## What a proposal is

A *proposal* is a written argument that identifies a specific problem, defines it precisely enough that solutions can be evaluated, and recommends a particular intervention based on evidence. Proposals are real-world documents. They exist in government, in business, in academic research, in nonprofit work, in advocacy, in journalism. The genre's conventions are slightly different in each setting, but the underlying moves are the same.

What proposals are *not*:

- *Editorials*. An editorial argues a position. A proposal argues a specific intervention.
- *Lab reports*. A lab report describes what you did. A proposal describes what should be done.
- *Personal essays about something that bothers you*. The personal voice is allowed in a proposal, but the personal grievance is not the structural argument. The structure has to do work that does not depend on your feelings.

The student version of the proposal genre asks for, typically, fifteen hundred to twenty-five hundred words, five or more credible sources, and a recommendation that is feasible — meaning a solution that could actually be implemented by the audience the proposal is addressed to.

## The problem comes first, and most proposals get it wrong

The single most consequential decision in a proposal is the *problem definition*. Most student proposals fail here. They fail in three predictable ways.

**Failure 1: the problem is too big.** *Climate change*, as a problem, is too big for a fifteen-hundred-word proposal. It does not have one solution. It does not have one audience. It cannot be defined precisely enough that the proposal's recommendation can be evaluated.

What you can do at that scale is narrow. *The carbon footprint of one specific industrial process in one specific region* is a problem. *The municipal trash collection schedule in your college town as a contributor to local methane emissions* is a problem. *The state of your university's recycling infrastructure* is a problem. Each is small enough that it can be defined precisely. Each is connected to climate change without pretending to address it whole.

**Failure 2: the problem is fake.** A *fake problem*, in this sense, is one the writer assumes exists without checking — usually because they have read about it somewhere or because they want it to exist for the proposal's sake. Fake problems are easy to spot: the writer has not produced evidence that the problem affects anyone in particular, beyond hand-waving statements like *many students struggle with* or *experts agree*. If the proposal cannot name specifically who is affected, in what numbers, with what consequences, the problem has not been adequately defined.

**Failure 3: the problem is solved.** A real problem may have a real, working solution that has already been adopted, leaving the proposal arguing for something that has already happened or for an unimproved version of an existing fix. The fix is research. Before you commit to a problem, find out what is already being done about it. If the answer is *nothing*, you may have a strong proposal. If the answer is *something*, your proposal has to engage with the existing solution — extend it, critique it, or argue for something different.

A useful diagnostic before drafting. Try to finish this sentence:

*Right now, [specific group] is experiencing [specific consequence] because of [specific cause]. Existing interventions [briefly describe] are [working / not working / partially working] because [reason]. I propose [specific intervention] to [specific outcome].*

If you can finish this sentence with all the brackets filled in concretely, you have a proposal. If you cannot, you have a topic, not a proposal.

## The objective stance, and what it actually means

Proposals are written in what the genre calls an *objective stance*. The phrase is used too loosely. Let me make it specific.

*Objective stance* does not mean *without an opinion*. The whole point of a proposal is to argue an opinion — that a specific intervention should be adopted. *Objective stance* means *the prose is calibrated to be persuasive to a reader who does not yet share your opinion*.

What that calibration looks like in practice:

- *Evidence carries the argument; emotional language does not.* "This policy is destroying lives" is emotional. "This policy increased the rate of [specific consequence] by 23 percent in the population it was applied to, according to [cited source]" does the work emotion was reaching for, with more force.
- *Counterarguments are named, not avoided.* The proposal that pretends no objection exists reads, to a skeptical reader, as either naive or dishonest. The proposal that names the strongest objection and addresses it earns trust.
- *Sources are credible to the reader's standards, not yours.* If your audience is a city council, the credible sources are government data, peer-reviewed studies, and major reporting. If your audience is a campus committee, the credible sources can include campus reporting and student government records. Match the evidence to the audience.
- *The writer's stake is acknowledged, if relevant, and then set aside.* If you are proposing something that affects you personally, name that you are affected — once, briefly — and then make the rest of the argument from evidence. Hidden stakes erode trust.

The objective stance is not the absence of voice. It is a specific kind of voice — measured, evidence-anchored, willing to look skepticism in the face. The strongest proposals have personality. They are also disciplined about which kinds of personality serve the argument and which kinds undermine it.

## Structure — what a proposal contains

The conventional shape, with notes on what each section is doing.

**1. Introduction.** Names the problem. Establishes that the problem is real, current, and serious. Briefly previews the recommendation. Should be specific enough by paragraph two that the reader knows exactly what is being argued.

**2. Problem section.** Develops the problem with evidence. Who is affected. How many. With what consequences. What causes it. What history it has. This is usually where the writer's research labor is most visible.

**3. Solutions section.** Proposes the intervention. Describes how it would work. Names what it would cost — in money, time, political capital, opportunity. Cites comparable interventions that have succeeded or failed elsewhere. If you propose multiple solutions, prioritize them.

**4. Objections section.** Names the strongest objections to the proposal and addresses them. Often two or three. Not boilerplate. The strongest objections, by the strongest opponents.

**5. Conclusion and call to action.** Restates the recommendation. Names what the audience should do next — and what *now* means: this week, this semester, this year. Specific actions are stronger than vague urgings.

The structure is generic enough that it serves business proposals, grant applications, policy recommendations, and the student proposal you are about to write. The specifics vary by genre. The bones are the same.

## A worked spine — climate, at student scale

Shawn Krukowski, a first-year composition student whose proposal appears in the OpenStax source for this chapter, took on climate change. The pitfall I named earlier — *the problem is too big* — was the central craft challenge of his draft. His move was to define the proposal not as *solve climate change* but as *argue for the structure of a U.S. response to climate change*, with mitigation and adaptation as the two halves.

His thesis: *What is needed to slow climate change is unified action in two key areas — mitigation and adaptation — spurred by government leadership in the United States and a global commitment to addressing the problem immediately.*

That sentence is doing several things at once. It narrows from *climate change* to *slow climate change*. It commits to a specific two-part structure (mitigation and adaptation). It specifies an actor (government leadership in the United States). It names a timeline (immediately). The thesis lets the reader know, by sentence two of the proposal, what the rest of the document will argue.

The proposal then develops each half — mitigation evidence and proposed steps; adaptation evidence and proposed steps — and addresses the strongest objections. The student handled denial of the underlying climate science as one set of objections and feasibility/cost objections as a separate set. Whether handling denial of the science was the right rhetorical move for his audience is a real question — for some audiences, treating denial as a serious objection legitimizes a position better treated as fringe. The decision is judgment-dependent. The student's decision was defensible at the audience he targeted.

What you should take from the example: even on a topic that is structurally too big, the proposal can work if the *frame* is specific enough. The student did not propose to solve climate change. He proposed a frame for thinking about responses. The narrowness is what made the proposal workable.

## Choosing your problem

Some practical filters.

*Does it affect a group whose situation I can verify?* The verifiability matters because the problem section depends on evidence. If you cannot find data on who is affected, you cannot write the problem section.

*Is there an actor who could implement my recommendation?* Proposals exist to influence action. A proposal to *change human nature* has no actor; a proposal to *adjust the campus mental health intake form* has a specific actor (the dean of students or the head of counseling services). Pick the latter.

*Can I find five or more credible sources?* Five is the floor for this kind of assignment. If a quick search produces fewer, the problem is either too obscure, too local in the wrong way, or already saturated by other writers — none of which is fatal, but each requires you to do extra work.

*Is the recommendation something I could defend in a five-minute conversation with a hostile reader?* If you cannot defend your recommendation in five minutes, the recommendation is not yet specific enough. The drafting will only make it worse.

Some problems that work well at a campus scale:

- The application process for a specific scholarship at your university and one specific equity issue in how it is administered.
- The food options in the dining hall and one specific dietary need they currently underserve.
- The way the campus shuttle schedule serves (or fails to serve) one specific group of students whose schedule does not match the shuttle's.
- The textbook policy for one specific course or department, and a specific change that would reduce student cost.
- The campus tutoring center's hours, scope, or staffing relative to a specific identifiable demand.

Notice the *specifics* in each. The proposal that succeeds is rarely the one that takes on the biggest topic.

## A note about AI

Two specific moves where the language model is genuinely useful in proposal writing, and two specific moves where it does damage.

Useful:

*Stress-testing the problem definition.* Ask the model: *Here is my problem statement. Is the problem actually as defined? What are the assumptions I am making that I have not yet defended? Where is my evidence weakest?* The model is often good at flagging the assumptions you have built in without noticing.

*Generating the strongest counterarguments.* Ask: *Here is my proposal. Construct the three strongest objections a hostile reader would raise. Be specific. Do not soften them.* This is one of the model's better tasks because it requires position-taking that the model performs comfortably when given permission.

Damage:

*Asking the model to suggest solutions before you have done your research.* The model will produce plausible-sounding solutions drawn from its training data. The solutions will look fine on the page. Some of them will be undefendable in your specific case because they are general-purpose patterns that don't match your specific problem. You will not always be able to tell which is which until you do the research.

*Asking the model to write the evidence.* The model will produce confident-sounding statistics, source citations, and quotations that may or may not be real. In a proposal genre, where evidence is the spine of the argument, this is the single worst failure mode. Cite sources you have read. Use statistics you can trace. The discipline of *not* delegating the evidence is part of the proposal's discipline.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 8

**What you're building:** the problem statement and counterargument map for your proposal, before you begin the drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended for this — you may want to return to it during revision.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word proposal for a college writing class.
The proposal must identify a specific problem and recommend a
feasible solution.

The problem I'm thinking about is: [describe it in one sentence]

The group affected is: [describe the specific group]

The audience I'm writing to is: [name a specific actor who could
implement the recommendation]

The intervention I'm proposing is: [describe it in one sentence]

Do the following, in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. Stress-test my problem definition. Ask me four questions that
   push me toward more specificity. Where is my problem too big?
   Where am I assuming the problem exists without evidence? Who
   specifically is affected, in what numbers, with what consequences?

2. Stress-test my intervention. Ask me three questions about whether
   the intervention is feasible. Who would implement it? What would
   it cost? What would it sacrifice?

3. Construct the three strongest objections a hostile reader would
   raise against my proposal. Do not soften them. Make them as
   pointed as you can. After listing them, tell me which one I am
   least prepared to address, based on what I have told you.

4. Once I've answered, propose a one-sentence thesis statement for
   the proposal. The thesis must contain: the problem named, the
   intervention named, and the outcome the intervention produces.

Do not write the proposal. Do not draft the introduction. The drafting
is mine to do.
```

**What this produces:** a sharpened problem statement, three specific objections you will have to address, and a thesis sentence that commits the rest of the proposal to a defensible structure.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful for proposals where you have several sources to track. Tell Cowork to keep a running file of cited evidence as you work.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when you describe your intervention, the model may immediately suggest improvements to it. Refuse: *"Don't improve my intervention. Ask the next stress-test question."* The discipline of letting the model interrogate rather than redirect is the literacy at stake.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter pivots from arguing for an intervention to evaluating an existing artifact. The judgment-from-evidence move stays. The genre's frame changes from *what should be done* to *what is this thing worth*.

## What would change my mind

If careful comparison of proposals written with explicit problem-definition discipline to proposals written without that discipline showed that the disciplined versions did *not* produce more defensible interventions, I would revise the central emphasis of this chapter on the problem-statement move. The evidence from the proposal-writing literature points the other way — Richard Johnson-Sheehan's *Writing Proposals* (Pearson, 2007) and the broader public-policy and grant-writing pedagogy converge on this — but the claim should remain falsifiable.

## Still puzzling

The line between *appropriate framing for the audience* and *softening the problem to make it easier to argue against* is hard to teach cleanly. The Krukowski climate proposal treats climate-change denial as an objection worth addressing. Some readers will see that as good rhetorical strategy; others will see it as legitimizing a position better treated as not worth engaging. The advice in this chapter — *match the evidence to the audience* — does not fully address that judgment. The judgment may not be teachable in a textbook section.
