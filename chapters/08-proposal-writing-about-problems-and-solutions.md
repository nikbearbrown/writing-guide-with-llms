# Chapter 8 — The Proposal

*Making the Problem Specific Enough to Be Solved.*

---

In 2001, Peter Pronovost — a critical-care doctor at Johns Hopkins — looked at a particular kind of hospital infection and noticed something that should have been obvious for decades and somehow wasn't. The infections were in patients who had received a central venous catheter, a tube threaded into a major vein near the heart, used for medications and monitoring in intensive care units. One in twenty patients who received a central line developed an infection. Roughly fifteen thousand people a year died from these infections in the United States. They were, on paper, almost entirely preventable. There were five hand-washing-and-sterile-technique steps a doctor was supposed to follow when inserting the line. Following all five virtually eliminated the infection.

Doctors did not follow all five. Not because they didn't know the steps. Because the situations were urgent and the steps felt like ritual.

Pronovost made a checklist. Five items. He gave the list to nurses and gave the nurses permission to stop the procedure if any item was skipped. He ran the protocol in eight Michigan hospital ICUs. The infection rate dropped 35 percent in three months and stayed down. The result was published in the *New England Journal of Medicine*. Atul Gawande, writing about it in the *New Yorker* in 2007 and then in *The Checklist Manifesto* in 2009, made the broader point: the problem was not knowledge. The problem was that the application of knowledge fails predictably under pressure, and the fix is a piece of paper.[^1]

[^1]: Atul Gawande, *The Checklist Manifesto: How to Get Things Right* (Metropolitan Books, 2009). The original *New Yorker* piece was Atul Gawande, ["The Checklist," *New Yorker*, 10 Dec. 2007](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2007/12/10/the-checklist).

What Pronovost wrote before all of that was a proposal. Not in the formal sense — he was operating inside a medical research and quality-improvement framework. But the underlying move is the move every proposal makes. He named a specific problem. He named who it affected and how. He proposed one specific intervention. He cited the evidence that the intervention could work. He named the obstacles to adopting it. The entire structure of the proposal genre is in that sequence.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to write a proposal on a problem you can actually research, recommending a solution you can defend. The Pronovost example is the clarity to aim for. Not the scale. The specificity.

## What a proposal is — and what it isn't

A proposal is a written argument that identifies a specific problem, defines it precisely enough that solutions can be evaluated, and recommends a particular intervention based on evidence. Proposals are real-world documents. They exist in government, in business, in academic research, in nonprofit work, in advocacy. The conventions vary by context, but the underlying moves are the same across all of them.

This matters because students often arrive at the proposal genre thinking it is just an essay with a recommendation attached. It isn't. The structural difference is more fundamental than it looks.

An editorial argues a position. A proposal argues a specific intervention. The editorial's question is *what is true* or *what should we believe*. The proposal's question is *what should we do, and who should do it, and by when*. An editorial can end with a lament. A proposal that ends with a lament has failed as a genre.

A lab report describes what you did. A proposal describes what should be done. A personal essay about something that bothers you is organized around your experience of the problem. A proposal is organized around the evidence that the problem exists and the evidence that the intervention can address it. The personal voice is allowed in a proposal. The personal grievance is not the structural argument. The structure has to do work that does not depend on your feelings about it.

The student version of the proposal genre typically runs fifteen hundred to twenty-five hundred words, draws on five or more credible sources, and recommends something feasible — meaning a solution that could actually be implemented by the audience the proposal is addressed to. That last clause is doing a lot of work, and I'll come back to it.

## The problem definition — where most proposals fail

The single most consequential decision in a proposal is not the solution. It is the problem definition. Most student proposals fail here, before they get anywhere near the solution. They fail in three predictable ways, and I want to name each one plainly.

**The problem is too big.** Climate change, as a problem statement, is too big for a fifteen-hundred-word proposal. It does not have one solution. It does not have one audience. It cannot be defined precisely enough that the recommendation can be evaluated against it. The proposal that begins with climate change as its problem has nowhere to land.

What you can do with a large topic is narrow until you find the actual proposal inside it. *The carbon footprint of one specific industrial process in one specific region* is a proposal-scale problem. *The municipal trash collection schedule in your college town as a contributor to local methane emissions* is a proposal-scale problem. *The state of your university's recycling infrastructure* is a proposal-scale problem. Each is specific enough to define. Each is connected to the larger topic without pretending to address it whole. The narrowness is not a failure of ambition — it is the condition that makes a proposal possible.

**The problem is fake.** A fake problem, in this sense, is one the writer assumes exists without checking — usually because they have read about it somewhere, or because they need it to exist for the proposal's sake. Fake problems are identifiable by what they are missing: the writer has not produced evidence that the problem affects anyone in particular. The symptoms are phrases like *many students struggle with* or *experts agree that* or *it is well known that* — phrases that gesture at evidence without producing it. If the proposal cannot name specifically who is affected, in what numbers, with what consequences, the problem has not been adequately defined. The writer has described a worry, not a problem.

**The problem is solved.** A real problem may have a real, working solution that has already been adopted, leaving the proposal arguing for something that has already happened. The fix is research. Before committing to a problem, find out what is already being done about it. If the answer is nothing, you may have a strong proposal. If the answer is something, your proposal has to engage with the existing solution — extend it, critique it, or argue for a different approach. The proposal that ignores existing interventions reads as unprepared.

Here is a diagnostic you can run before you draft. Try to complete this sentence with all the brackets filled in concretely:

*Right now, [specific group] is experiencing [specific consequence] because of [specific cause]. Existing interventions [briefly describe] are [working / not working / partially working] because [reason]. I propose [specific intervention] to [specific outcome].*

If you can complete that sentence, you have a proposal. If you cannot, you have a topic. The drafting will not fix the gap — it will only make it longer.

## What "objective stance" actually means

Proposals are written in what the genre calls an objective stance. The phrase is used loosely enough that it has become misleading. Let me be specific about what it means.

Objective stance does not mean *without an opinion*. The whole point of a proposal is to argue for a specific intervention. The writer has a position and is trying to persuade a reader who does not yet hold it. Objective stance means the prose is calibrated to be persuasive to that skeptical reader — which requires a different set of moves than prose calibrated to express how strongly you feel about something.

What the calibration looks like in practice.

Evidence carries the argument; emotional language does not. "This policy is destroying lives" is emotional. "This policy increased the rate of [specific consequence] by 23 percent in the affected population, according to [cited study]" does the work the emotional language was reaching for, and does it with more persuasive force to a skeptical reader. The emotional version preaches to the converted. The evidence-based version has a chance with the unconvinced.

Counterarguments are named, not avoided. The proposal that pretends no objection exists reads, to a skeptical reader, as either naive or dishonest. The proposal that names the strongest objection and addresses it earns trust. This is counterintuitive to many student writers, who believe that naming objections weakens their position. The opposite is true: the reader who is thinking the objection while reading your proposal will trust you more when they see that you were thinking it too.

Sources are credible to the reader's standards, not yours. If your audience is a city council, the credible sources are government data, peer-reviewed studies, and major-outlet reporting. If your audience is a campus committee, the credible sources can include campus reporting and student government records. The evidence has to match the community of practice you are writing into.

The writer's stake, if relevant, is acknowledged once and then set aside. If you are proposing something that directly affects you, name that you are affected — briefly, explicitly, once — and then build the rest of the argument from evidence. Hidden stakes erode trust when the reader discovers them, and readers usually do.

The objective stance is not the absence of voice. It is a specific kind of voice: measured, evidence-anchored, willing to engage with skepticism rather than avoid it. The strongest proposals have personality. They are also disciplined about which kinds of personality serve the argument and which kinds undermine it.

## The structure — what a proposal contains and why each section exists

The conventional shape runs five sections. I want to explain what each section is doing, not just what it contains, because the sections exist for reasons and understanding the reasons makes them easier to write.

The **introduction** names the problem, establishes that it is real and serious, and briefly previews the recommendation. By paragraph two, the reader should know exactly what is being argued. The introduction that is still describing the general background by paragraph three has lost the thread.

The **problem section** develops the problem with evidence. Who is affected. How many. With what consequences. What causes it. What history it has. This is where the research labor is most visible. A problem section built on vague assertions fails the proposal at its foundation. A problem section built on specific, cited evidence with concrete numbers establishes the credibility that the rest of the proposal borrows against.

The **solutions section** proposes the intervention. Describes how it would work. Names what it would cost — in money, time, political capital, opportunity. Cites comparable interventions that have succeeded or failed elsewhere. This last requirement is important: a proposal that recommends something without evidence that it has worked anywhere is asking the reader to take a larger risk than most audiences will accept. The comparable-case evidence is how you reduce the reader's perceived risk.

The **objections section** names the strongest objections and addresses them. Not the weakest objections, which collapse too easily. The strongest ones, the ones a hostile reader is already thinking. Typically two or three. The discipline here is to actually engage with the objection rather than dismiss it — to say *here is why this objection does not defeat the proposal*, not *this objection is wrong because it misunderstands the problem*.

The **conclusion and call to action** restates the recommendation and names what the audience should do next — specifically, with a timeline. *Advocate for this policy change* is vague. *Present this recommendation to the Dean of Students office at the start of the spring semester* is specific. The call to action that is specific enough to be followed is stronger than the call to action that expresses a general wish.

## A worked spine — what specificity looks like in practice

Shawn Krukowski, a first-year composition student whose proposal appears in the OpenStax source for this chapter, took on climate change — the exact pitfall I named earlier. His move was to define the proposal not as *solve climate change* but as *argue for the structure of a U.S. response to climate change*, with mitigation and adaptation as the two halves.

His thesis: *What is needed to slow climate change is unified action in two key areas — mitigation and adaptation — spurred by government leadership in the United States and a global commitment to addressing the problem immediately.*

That sentence is doing several things at once. It narrows from *climate change* to *slow climate change* — a different and more achievable goal. It commits to a specific two-part structure. It names an actor: government leadership in the United States. It names a timeline: immediately. The reader knows by sentence two what the rest of the document will argue, what structure it will follow, and what the proposal is asking for.

The proposal then develops each half with evidence and proposed steps, and addresses two sets of objections separately: denial of the underlying climate science, and feasibility and cost concerns.

Whether treating denial of the science as a serious objection was the right rhetorical choice is a real question. For some audiences, engaging with denial legitimizes a position better treated as fringe. For other audiences — a state legislature with members who hold that position, for example — ignoring it would render the proposal unpersuasive to the people who need to be persuaded. The decision is audience-dependent. The student made a defensible choice for his stated audience.

What the example demonstrates is this: even on a topic that is structurally too large, the proposal can work if the frame is specific enough. The student did not propose to solve climate change. He proposed a frame for thinking about how to respond to it. The frame is specific. The specificity is what makes the proposal workable.

## Choosing your problem

Several filters that save drafting time by catching the wrong problems before you invest in them.

*Does it affect a group whose situation you can verify?* The verifiability matters because the problem section depends on evidence. If there is no data on who is affected, there is no problem section.

*Is there a specific actor who could implement your recommendation?* Proposals exist to influence action. A proposal to change human nature has no actor. A proposal to adjust the campus mental health intake form has a specific actor — the dean of students, the head of counseling services — and that actor can either adopt the recommendation or explain why not. The latter kind of proposal is worth writing.

*Can you find five or more credible sources?* Five is the floor for this assignment. A quick search that produces fewer means the problem is either too obscure, too locally specific in the wrong way, or already saturated by existing work — none of which is fatal, but each requires extra effort to overcome.

*Can you defend the recommendation in a five-minute conversation with a hostile reader?* If you cannot hold the position under pressure, the recommendation is not yet specific enough. The drafting will only make it longer, not more defensible.

Some problems that work well at campus scale: the application process for a specific scholarship and one specific equity issue in how it is administered. The food options in the dining hall and one specific dietary need they underserve. The campus shuttle schedule and one specific group whose schedule it fails. The textbook policy for one specific course or department and a change that would reduce student cost. The tutoring center's hours relative to a specific identifiable gap in demand.

The pattern in each: one specific thing, one specific affected group, one specific actor. Not *food insecurity on campus*. *The dining hall's policy on meal-swipe donations and one gap in how it reaches students who need it*. That is a proposal.

## Where the language model is useful and where it does damage

Two specific places where the model genuinely helps in proposal writing, and two where it causes problems that look like help until you read the draft carefully.

Useful: stress-testing the problem definition. Ask the model to identify where your problem statement is too vague, where you are assuming the problem exists without evidence, where the affected group is underspecified. The model is reliably good at this because it can apply the diagnostic questions from the outside, where you cannot.

Useful: generating the strongest counterarguments. Ask the model to construct the three strongest objections a hostile reader would raise — and to make them pointed, not soft. This is one of the model's more reliable tasks. It can take positions it doesn't hold when given explicit permission to do so, which is exactly what counterargument generation requires.

Damaging: asking the model to suggest solutions before you have done your research. The model will produce plausible-looking solutions drawn from its training. The solutions will read fine on the page. Some of them will be undefendable in your specific case because they are general-purpose patterns that don't fit your specific problem. You will not always know which is which until you do the research — by which point you may have built a draft around an unworkable intervention.

Damaging: asking the model to write the evidence. The model will produce confident-sounding statistics, source citations, and quotations that may or may not exist. In a proposal, where the evidence is the spine of the argument, this is the single worst failure mode. A cited statistic that cannot be traced to a real source destroys the entire proposal's credibility when discovered. Cite only sources you have read. Use only statistics you can trace. The discipline of not delegating the evidence is part of the proposal's discipline.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 8

**What you're building:** the problem statement and counterargument map for your proposal, before you begin the drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended for this — you may want to return to it during revision.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word proposal for a college writing class.
The proposal must identify a specific problem and recommend a
feasible solution.

The problem I'm thinking about is: [describe it in one sentence]

The group affected is: [describe the specific group]

The audience I'm writing to is: [name a specific actor who could
implement the recommendation]

The intervention I'm proposing is: [describe it in one sentence]

Do the following, in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. Stress-test my problem definition. Ask me four questions that
   push me toward more specificity. Where is my problem too big?
   Where am I assuming the problem exists without evidence? Who
   specifically is affected, in what numbers, with what consequences?

2. Stress-test my intervention. Ask me three questions about whether
   the intervention is feasible. Who would implement it? What would
   it cost? What would it sacrifice?

3. Construct the three strongest objections a hostile reader would
   raise against my proposal. Do not soften them. Make them as
   pointed as you can. After listing them, tell me which one I am
   least prepared to address, based on what I have told you.

4. Once I've answered, propose a one-sentence thesis statement for
   the proposal. The thesis must contain: the problem named, the
   intervention named, and the outcome the intervention produces.

Do not write the proposal. Do not draft the introduction. The drafting
is mine to do.
```

**What this produces:** a sharpened problem statement, three specific objections you will have to address, and a thesis sentence that commits the rest of the proposal to a defensible structure.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful for proposals where you have several sources to track. Tell Cowork to keep a running file of cited evidence as you work.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when you describe your intervention, the model may immediately suggest improvements to it. Refuse: *"Don't improve my intervention. Ask the next stress-test question."* The discipline of letting the model interrogate rather than redirect is the literacy at stake.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter pivots from arguing for an intervention to evaluating an existing artifact. The judgment-from-evidence move stays. The genre's frame changes from *what should be done* to *what is this thing worth*.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Donella Meadows** was working on *Thinking in Systems* and her essay "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System" — the proposal as a structural argument about where to apply effort in a complex problem — decades before most people had encountered the proposal genre. Here's a prompt to find out more, and then make it better.

**Run this:**

```
Who was Donella Meadows, and how does their work on systems thinking
and the structure of effective proposals connect to writing a proposal
that defines a problem and recommends a solution? Keep it to three
paragraphs. End with the single most surprising thing about their
career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"Donella Meadows"** on Wikipedia after you run this. See what the model got right, got wrong, or left out.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it to walk through Meadows's twelve "leverage points" — from constants and parameters to paradigms — with a worked example from a problem you care about.
- Ask: "Meadows famously said the most effective place to intervene in a system is the paradigm. What did she mean — and what does that imply about most proposals that focus on policy or budget?"
- Add the framing: "Answer as if you're writing a memo to the head of an organization that has been applying effort at the wrong leverage point for years."

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. Take any news story about a social problem published in the past six months. Run it through the three failure-mode tests: is the problem too big? Is the problem fake (asserted without specific evidence of who is affected and how)? Is the problem already solved? Write one sentence for each test, naming which failure mode applies or explaining why the problem clears that bar. *(Tests: applying the three failure-mode diagnostics to a real case before applying them to your own draft.)*

2. Find any published proposal — a government policy brief, a nonprofit grant application excerpt, a student op-ed that makes a recommendation, a campus senate resolution. Identify whether it contains all five structural sections: introduction, problem section, solutions section, objections section, conclusion and call to action. For any section that is missing or weak, write one sentence naming what the proposal loses by not having it. *(Tests: recognizing the five-section structure and understanding why each section exists.)*

3. Write the diagnostic sentence for a problem you are considering for your own proposal, with all brackets filled in concretely: *Right now, [specific group] is experiencing [specific consequence] because of [specific cause]. Existing interventions [briefly describe] are [working / not working / partially working] because [reason]. I propose [specific intervention] to [specific outcome].* If you cannot fill in a bracket, name which one stops you and why. *(Tests: applying the diagnostic before drafting; identifying where the problem definition is still a topic rather than a proposal.)*

**Application**

4. Take the Krukowski climate-change thesis sentence and rewrite it for a different, campus-scale problem of your choosing. Your thesis must contain, as his does: a narrowed goal (not the whole problem), a specific two-part or multi-part structure, a named actor, and a timeline. Then write one sentence explaining what the narrowing cost you and whether that cost was worth it. *(Tests: applying the specificity moves from the worked example to a new case; practicing the trade-off between scope and workability.)*

5. Write the objections section for a proposal you have not yet drafted — pick any campus-scale problem from the list in the chapter or one of your own. Name two objections a hostile reader would raise. For each: state the objection as sharply as you can (not softened), and then write two to three sentences addressing it without abandoning the recommendation. *(Tests: the handled-counterargument move in isolation, before the pressure of a full draft.)*

6. Take any claim that uses vague authority — *many students struggle with*, *research has shown*, *experts agree* — from a piece of writing you have encountered. Rewrite the claim in the named-source form: find and cite the actual evidence the vague claim was gesturing at, or, if you cannot find it, explain what specific data you would need to make the claim real. *(Tests: distinguishing the shape of evidence from its substance; practicing what evidence-based argument requires versus what it performs.)*

7. Write the call to action for a proposal you have not yet drafted. The call must name: the specific actor, the specific action, and the specific timeline. Then ask: if this proposal landed on that actor's desk tomorrow, is there anything in the call to action they could not understand or act on without asking a follow-up question? If yes, revise until the answer is no. *(Tests: writing a specific, actionable call to action rather than a general wish; testing the call against the audience it is addressed to.)*

**Synthesis**

8. Draft the problem section of your proposal — the section that develops the problem with evidence, names who is affected and how, and traces causes. It should run 300–500 words and cite at least two sources. Then run it against the three failure-mode tests yourself: is the problem still too big? Is any part of it still fake — asserted without evidence? Does any part of it describe something already addressed by an existing intervention you have not acknowledged? Revise until it clears all three. *(Tests: full integration of problem-definition discipline in original prose; self-application of the diagnostic.)*

9. Write the solutions section of your proposal — the section that proposes the intervention, describes how it works, names its costs, and cites at least one comparable intervention that has succeeded or failed elsewhere. Then write one paragraph identifying the point at which the model-generated shortcut would be most tempting in this section — and most dangerous — and explain why. *(Tests: drafting the solutions section with comparable-case evidence; developing awareness of where delegating to the model introduces undefendable claims.)*

**Challenge**

10. Find a proposal — published or student — in which the writer made a judgment call about how to treat a contested objection: whether to engage with it seriously, dismiss it as fringe, or ignore it entirely. Reconstruct the rhetorical situation: who is the audience, and why might the writer have made that choice? Was the choice defensible? What would a different choice have cost the proposal? Write 300–400 words. There is no single correct answer; the quality of the response depends on the specificity and honesty of the analysis. *(Open-ended; extends the chapter's "still unresolved" problem — the line between appropriate framing and softening — into a real case the student has to evaluate.)*

If careful comparison of proposals written with explicit problem-definition discipline against proposals written without it showed that the disciplined versions did not produce more defensible interventions, I would revise this chapter's central emphasis on the problem-statement move. The evidence from the proposal-writing literature points the other way — Richard Johnson-Sheehan's *Writing Proposals* (Pearson, 2007) and the broader public-policy and grant-writing pedagogy converge on the problem-first claim — but the claim should remain falsifiable.

## Still unresolved

The line between *appropriate framing for the audience* and *softening the problem to make it easier to argue* is hard to teach cleanly. The Krukowski climate proposal treats climate-change denial as an objection worth addressing at length. Some readers will see that as good rhetorical strategy for a politically mixed audience. Others will see it as legitimizing a position that should be treated as outside the serious conversation. The advice in this chapter — match the evidence to the audience — does not fully resolve that judgment call. The judgment may not be teachable in a textbook section.
