# Chapter 17 — The Case Study

*When One Person's Specifics Reveal a General Pattern.*

A scene from a Paris hospital in 1861.

A patient named Louis Victor Leborgne lies in a bed at the Bicêtre asylum, where he has been confined for more than two decades. He is fifty-one years old. He has lost the ability to speak. Whatever the question, whatever the address, he answers with the same single syllable: *tan*. He understands what is said to him. He cannot produce other sounds. His nickname in the asylum, used for years, is now *Tan*.

A physician named Pierre Paul Broca examines Leborgne for the first time on April 11, 1861. Six days later, Leborgne is dead — gangrene; he had been admitted to the asylum decades earlier for unrelated reasons, and he had recently developed a sepsis Broca could not treat. Broca performs the autopsy. He finds, in Leborgne's brain, a specific lesion in the left frontal lobe.

The publication that follows — Broca's report of the case, presented to the Société d'Anthropologie de Paris within days — does something that medical science had not yet done. It identifies a specific brain region with a specific function. Speech production, Broca argues, depends on the left inferior frontal gyrus. The region is now called *Broca's area*. The condition Leborgne suffered from — the inability to produce speech with intact comprehension — is called *Broca's aphasia*.

One patient. One autopsy. The foundation, in 1861, of what would become the field of neurolinguistics.[^1]

[^1]: For a contemporary discussion of the Broca-Leborgne case and its standing in the history of neuroscience, see [the Bibliothèque Henri-Pierre Roché's collection on the Société d'Anthropologie de Paris](https://www.bium.parisdescartes.fr/) and standard neurolinguistics textbooks. Broca's original 1861 paper was published in the *Bulletins de la Société d'Anthropologie de Paris*, vol. 2.

What Broca produced is called a *case study*. The genre is foundational across medicine, neuropsychology, sociology, anthropology, law, business, and education. Its central premise is counterintuitive: by examining one specific case in detail, the researcher can reveal patterns that general statistical analysis would miss.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is a short case study of your own — a study of one person's use of language across different contexts, 1,000 to 1,200 words. Your subject will be one specific person. Your data will be observation and interview. Your conclusion will be modest — what one person's case can show — and your discussion will be specific about what generalizes and what does not.

## What a case study is, and what it isn't

A *case study* is a research method that examines one specific instance — one person, one event, one institution, one community — in sufficient depth to reveal patterns that the case can illuminate. The case is not chosen because it is statistically representative. It is chosen because it can be examined in depth, and because something specific about it can teach.

What case studies are not:

- *Statistical studies in miniature.* Case studies do not produce findings of the form *X percent of the population behaves this way*. They produce findings of the form *here is how this specific case works, and here is what about it might generalize and what might not*.
- *Anecdotes.* An anecdote tells a single story; a case study analyzes it. The analytical move is the difference.
- *Profiles.* A profile (Chapter 7) renders a subject. A case study examines a subject for what it teaches. The two genres overlap, but the case study's purpose is more explicitly analytical.
- *Personal essays.* The case study uses formal research methods (observation, interview, analysis) and writes in a more academic register than a personal essay. Your stake is acknowledged where relevant, then set aside.

The genre has versions across many disciplines. In medicine, case studies report novel patient presentations and treatments. In law, they analyze specific court rulings. In business, they examine specific firms or decisions. In psychology and linguistics, they examine specific individuals to reveal patterns of mind or language. The methodology adapts to the discipline. The underlying move is the same.

## What case studies do that other methods don't

Three specific things.

**Depth.** A case study can examine one subject in detail no large-scale study can match. The 800-person survey can tell you average behaviors and rough correlations. The case study can tell you what happened in one specific Tuesday afternoon, in one specific person's reasoning, in one specific decision. The depth is the genre's currency.

**Hypothesis generation.** Many of the most important findings in the human sciences began as case studies that suggested hypotheses larger studies later confirmed. Broca's case generated the hypothesis that brain function is localized; subsequent work — Wernicke, Lichtheim, twentieth-century imaging — tested and refined the hypothesis. The case study did not prove the general claim. It made the general claim worth testing.

**Counter-evidence.** Case studies can definitively refute general claims, even when they cannot prove them. If you claim *no patient with a left-frontal-lobe lesion can produce speech*, a single case study of a patient who could produce speech with that lesion would refute the claim. The single counter-example does what the average-finding study cannot.

The case study's limit is also clear: it cannot, on its own, establish general patterns. The student case study you will write of one person's language use can show that *this person* behaves in certain ways in certain contexts. It cannot show that *all speakers of this language community* behave that way. The generalization, when it happens, comes from many case studies in concert, or from the case study combined with other methods.

## Case study ethics — the issue you cannot ignore

Case studies involve real people. The ethical obligations are real.

A short list:

**Consent.** Anyone who is the subject of a case study must consent to being studied. They must know what the study is about, how the data will be used, and where the report will be shared. Consent is not just a legal formality; it is the foundation of the relationship the case study depends on.

**Confidentiality.** Subjects are often given pseudonyms in published case studies. The decision to name or anonymize depends on the case. Broca's patient, posthumously, was named — the historical record of the case became more important than the patient's privacy, but the patient was beyond the reach of harm. A living subject's case is different. Default to anonymization unless the subject explicitly prefers to be named.

**Minimization of harm.** The case study should not cause harm to the subject. This sounds obvious; it is sometimes complicated. A case study of one person's struggle with a stigmatized condition could, if not careful, expose them to consequences they did not anticipate. The careful researcher thinks about this in advance.

**Honest representation.** The subject's actions, words, and circumstances should be represented honestly. Composite characters and conflated events are not permitted. The genre's credibility depends on the reader's confidence that the case is real and rendered accurately.

A cautionary case from the history of social psychology research: the Milgram experiments in the early 1960s, in which subjects were instructed to administer what they believed were painful electric shocks to other people. The experiments were not case studies in the genre's narrow sense, but they raised the ethical questions case study research has had to address ever since. The Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) that now oversee human-subject research in U.S. universities exist in part because of the recognition that human research can produce psychological harm even when the subjects are consenting volunteers.

For your student case study, IRB review is usually not required for an informal study of one person you know. Your instructor will tell you what level of review is needed at your institution. Either way, the underlying ethics apply: consent, confidentiality, minimization of harm, honest representation.

## The student assignment — a case study of language use

The genre at the student scale is narrower than the medical or sociological version. Your case study will examine one person's language use across at least two different contexts.

Some specific topics that work well:

**Code-switching.** A bilingual speaker who moves between two languages — when do they switch? With whom? In what contexts? What triggers the switches?

**Register shifting.** A speaker who uses different versions of one language with different audiences — the same person texting friends, emailing professors, talking with grandparents. What changes, and what stays the same?

**Media-specific language use.** How does one person's communication differ across text message, email, voice, video call, and in-person conversation? Where are the consistencies, and where are the breaks?

**Emoji or other paralinguistic features.** How does one person use emoji, gifs, and other non-textual features across contexts? What functions do these serve in their communication?

**Online community language.** A speaker who participates in a specific online community (Discord, a subreddit, a fan forum) and develops a particular register there. How does their language inside the community differ from their language outside it?

Each of these is narrow enough to be a 1,000-1,200 word study and rich enough to produce real findings.

## Research methodology — for your case study

A small repertoire of methods, calibrated to the assignment.

**Observation.** Spend time around your subject when they are using language naturally. Take notes — not on what you think they should be doing, but on what they actually do. Distinguish what you observe (specific utterances, specific behaviors) from what you interpret (what you think they mean).

A practical note on observation: the subject's awareness of being observed will affect their behavior. There is no clean fix; the best you can do is acknowledge it in your analysis. A subject who knows you are taking notes on their speech will speak more carefully. A subject who has known you for years and forgotten you are also studying them will speak more naturally.

**Interview.** Ask the subject about their language use. Open-ended questions. *Describe a time when you noticed yourself switching from one language to another.* *Tell me about a recent text exchange where you chose your wording carefully.* *Walk me through the difference between how you talk with your friends and how you talk with your parents.* Record the answers if you have permission; take careful notes if you don't. Transcribe the parts you might quote.

**Document collection.** With permission, examine specific examples of the subject's writing — text messages, emails, social media posts, anything available. The documents are evidence. Treat them carefully (the permission should specifically cover documents you intend to quote in your study).

**Survey.** For some case studies, a structured short survey adds useful comparable data. *On a scale of 1 to 5, how often do you...* Surveys produce countable responses. They are most useful as a supplement to observation and interview, not as the primary method.

For the assignment, observation and interview together usually produce enough data. Document collection adds depth when feasible. Survey is occasionally useful for narrowing the questions.

## How to structure the case study

A working structure.

**Background and context.** Who is the subject, and why are they an interesting case for the question? What are the cultural and linguistic contexts that shape their language use? This is where the rhetorical situation gets named for the case. Pseudonym if appropriate.

**Research question.** What specifically are you trying to learn? Stated as an open question. *How does Maya, a Korean-American college student who speaks Korean at home and English at school, code-switch between the two languages in different social contexts?*

**Methods and participant.** What did you do? How long? What kind of data did you collect? Number of observations, number of interview sessions, what documents you reviewed. The methods section is brief but specific.

**Findings.** Organized by the dimensions you were studying. *Maya's code-switching across three contexts: at home with parents, with Korean-American friends, with non-Korean classmates.* Each section presents observation, evidence, and analysis. Specific quotes, specific patterns. The findings should be detailed enough that a reader can see what you saw.

**Discussion.** What do the findings suggest about the larger question? What patterns emerged that might generalize, and what is specific to this case? Where did your initial hypotheses hold up, and where did the data complicate them?

**Limitations.** What did the methodology not capture? What more would you need to know to answer the larger question? The limitations section is the discipline of the genre — it is what separates the case study from anecdote.

**Conclusion and further research.** What does the case suggest for further study? What questions remain? Short.

The structure scales. The Broca paper is much longer and uses similar moves. The student case study compresses each section to a few paragraphs.

## The generalization problem — and how to handle it

The central tension in case study writing is between *specificity* — your subject is one specific person — and *generalization* — what about this case teaches about the larger pattern.

The two failures.

**Over-generalization.** The student writes a case study of one person's code-switching and concludes *this is how all bilingual speakers behave*. The conclusion is unsupported by the case. One person's behavior is one person's behavior. You can hypothesize about broader patterns; you cannot prove them from one case.

**Under-generalization.** The student writes a case study of one person's code-switching and concludes *Maya code-switches in these ways*, full stop. No reflection on what about the case might illuminate broader questions. The case study without reflection on its broader stakes is just a detailed report.

The discipline is in the middle. You hypothesize about what might generalize — *Maya's code-switching pattern, where she shifts more readily into Korean in emotional contexts than in informational contexts, is consistent with the pattern documented in Schumann's 1976 study of second-language acquisition under emotional intimacy* — and you flag the limits — *whether this is true beyond Maya's specific case would require larger studies with broader samples*.

The two moves together — hypothesis and limit — are what the case study genre rewards.

## A worked example, condensed

A student case study, written in 2023, of a college student named Mateo (pseudonym) who is bilingual in Spanish and English.

*Background:* Mateo, age 20, born in Texas, native bilingual; Spanish at home with parents (immigrants from Oaxaca), English in school. Lives in dorm; works retail; has Spanish-speaking and English-speaking friends.

*Research question:* How does Mateo's code-switching between Spanish and English vary across contexts of emotional intimacy and informational exchange?

*Methods:* Three weeks of observation (the student attended one shared class with Mateo and had three meals with him); one 45-minute recorded interview; review of approximately 50 text messages Mateo shared (with permission).

*Findings, condensed:* Mateo's text messages with his mother are predominantly Spanish; with his Spanish-speaking friends, mixed roughly evenly; with his English-speaking friends, predominantly English with occasional Spanish phrases for emphasis or humor. In observed conversation: Mateo defaults to Spanish in emotional registers (when discussing his grandmother's recent illness; when describing a fight with his brother) even with friends who would normally hear him in English. He defaults to English in informational registers (describing his job's procedures, recounting class material) even with his Spanish-speaking friends. The pattern is consistent across all observed instances.

*Discussion:* The pattern is consistent with the broader literature on bilingual emotional language (the use of one's first language for emotional content is widely documented). What Mateo's case suggests, beyond what the literature already has, is the very fine granularity of the switching — within a single conversation, within seconds, the language can shift specifically with the topical register. The case does not establish this pattern as general, but it is consistent enough across observed contexts to suggest the hypothesis is worth testing in larger samples.

*Limitations:* One subject. Three weeks. The observer is the subject's friend, and Mateo's awareness of being studied may have affected his usage. Self-report in the interview is limited by Mateo's own metalinguistic awareness.

The case study is doing the genre's work. It hypothesizes specifically. It limits itself honestly. It locates itself in the existing literature without claiming to extend it definitively.

## A note about AI

The case study is the genre where AI assistance is most likely to introduce specific failure modes.

Useful:

*Interview question design.* Ask the model to suggest interview questions on your topic. Use the suggestions as starting points; revise them to fit your specific subject and rhetorical situation.

*Pattern identification in your data.* After you have collected observation notes and interview transcripts, paste them and ask the model to identify patterns. *Where do you see consistent moves across these observations? Where do you see contradictions?* The model is reasonably good at this; the interpretation of the patterns is yours.

*Locating your case in the literature.* Once you have a tentative pattern, ask the model what the existing literature on the topic has documented. Verify everything the model says against actual sources before citing.

Damaging:

*Generating the case study from a topic.* The model can write a plausible-sounding case study of a fictional person. It will not be a case study in the genre's sense, because no real observation has occurred. Do not.

*Generalizing for you.* The model will tend to overgeneralize from your specific findings, because its training data includes many examples of case studies whose conclusions overreached. Read every generalization-shaped sentence in your draft and ask whether your specific case supports it.

*Imitating interview transcripts.* If you ask the model to clean up or fill gaps in an interview transcript, it will produce plausible-sounding speech that the subject did not actually say. Only use direct quotes you can verify against the recording or your notes.

## What would change my mind

If the case study's hypothesis-generation function turned out to be empirically inferior to large-sample correlational analysis for generating real research insights — that the cases that became foundational (Broca's, Genie's, the H.M. amnesia case) were exceptions in a literature whose modal contribution was minor — I would revise the chapter's framing of the genre as analytically powerful. The evidence from the history of the human sciences runs the other way: many of the most important findings began as case studies. But the claim should be evaluable.

## Still puzzling

The relationship between case study and large-N statistical research is uneasy in 2026. Some fields treat the case study as foundational (clinical medicine, neuropsychology); some treat it as inferior to randomized controlled trials and dismiss it as anecdotal (much of contemporary psychology); some are conflicted (sociology, anthropology). The case study's standing depends partly on what the field believes science looks like. Whether the genre is best taught as a method on its own terms or as a stepping stone toward larger-sample work is a pedagogical question I have not resolved.
