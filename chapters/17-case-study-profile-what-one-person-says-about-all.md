# Chapter 17 — The Case Study

*When One Person's Specifics Reveal a General Pattern.*

---

A scene from a Paris hospital in 1861.

A patient named Louis Victor Leborgne lies in a bed at the Bicêtre asylum, where he has been confined for more than two decades. He is fifty-one years old. He has lost the ability to speak. Whatever the question, whatever the address, he answers with the same single syllable: *tan*. He understands what is said to him. He cannot produce other sounds. His nickname in the asylum, used for years, is *Tan*.

A physician named Pierre Paul Broca examines Leborgne for the first time on April 11, 1861. Six days later, Leborgne is dead — gangrene. Broca performs the autopsy. He finds, in Leborgne's brain, a specific lesion in the left frontal lobe.

The publication that follows — Broca's report of the case, presented to the Société d'Anthropologie de Paris within days — does something that medical science had not yet done. It identifies a specific brain region with a specific function. Speech production, Broca argues, depends on the left inferior frontal gyrus. The region is now called *Broca's area*. The condition Leborgne suffered from — the inability to produce speech with intact comprehension — is called *Broca's aphasia*.

One patient. One autopsy. The foundation, in 1861, of what would become the field of neurolinguistics.[^1]

[^1]: For a contemporary discussion of the Broca-Leborgne case and its standing in the history of neuroscience, see standard neurolinguistics textbooks and the Bibliothèque Henri-Pierre Roché's collection on the Société d'Anthropologie de Paris. Broca's original 1861 paper was published in the *Bulletins de la Société d'Anthropologie de Paris*, vol. 2.

What Broca produced is called a case study. The genre is foundational across medicine, neuropsychology, sociology, anthropology, law, business, and education. Its central premise is counterintuitive: by examining one specific case in sufficient depth, the researcher can reveal patterns that general statistical analysis would miss. The average-finding study tells you what most people do. The case study tells you what one person does — and that, sometimes, is what the field needed to know.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is a short case study of your own — a study of one person's language use across different contexts, 1,000 to 1,200 words. Your subject will be one specific person. Your data will be observation and interview. Your conclusion will be modest — what one person's case can show — and your discussion will be specific about what generalizes and what does not.

## What a case study is — and what makes it different

A case study is a research method that examines one specific instance — one person, one event, one institution, one community — in sufficient depth to reveal patterns that the case can illuminate. The case is not chosen because it is statistically representative. It is chosen because it can be examined in depth, and because something specific about it can teach.

The distinction from other genres is worth drawing precisely. A case study is not a statistical study in miniature — it does not produce findings of the form *X percent of the population behaves this way*. It produces findings of the form *here is how this specific case works, and here is what about it might generalize and what might not*. A case study is not an anecdote — an anecdote tells a single story; a case study analyzes it, and the analytical move is the entire difference. A case study is not a profile, which renders a subject. The case study examines a subject for what it teaches. The profile's purpose is evocation; the case study's purpose is inquiry.

The genre has versions across many disciplines. In medicine, case studies report novel patient presentations and treatments. In law, they analyze specific court rulings. In business, they examine specific firms or decisions. In psychology and linguistics, they examine specific individuals to reveal patterns of mind or language. The methodology adapts. The underlying move is the same.

---

## Three things case studies do that other methods cannot

Depth. A case study can examine one subject in detail no large-scale study can match. The 800-person survey can tell you average behaviors and rough correlations. The case study can tell you what happened in one specific afternoon, in one specific person's reasoning, in one specific decision. The survey's aggregate loses exactly this. The depth is the genre's currency.

Hypothesis generation. Many of the most important findings in the human sciences began as case studies that suggested hypotheses larger studies later confirmed or refined. Broca's case generated the hypothesis that brain function is localized; subsequent work — Wernicke, Lichtheim, twentieth-century imaging — tested and refined the hypothesis across larger samples. The case study did not prove the general claim. It made the general claim worth testing. This is not a small contribution. Before Broca's case, the hypothesis had no compelling warrant. Afterward, it did.

Counter-evidence. A case study can definitively refute a general claim even when it cannot prove one. If someone claims no patient with a left-frontal-lobe lesion can produce speech, a single case study of a patient who could would refute the claim. The single counter-example does what the average-finding study cannot. Statistical analysis establishes tendencies; one well-documented exception can demolish a universal claim.

The case study's limit is equally clear. It cannot, on its own, establish general patterns. The student case study you will write of one person's language use can show that *this person* behaves in certain ways in certain contexts. It cannot show that *all speakers of this language community* behave that way. The generalization, when it comes, comes from many case studies in concert, or from the case study combined with other methods.

## Ethics — the issue you cannot set aside

Case studies involve real people. The ethical obligations are not formalities.

Consent. Anyone who is the subject of a case study must know what the study is about, how the data will be used, and where the report will be shared. They must agree to being studied. Consent is the foundation of the relationship the case study depends on — and beyond that, it is what makes the research human-subject research rather than surveillance.

Confidentiality. Subjects are typically given pseudonyms in published case studies. The decision to name or anonymize depends on the case and the subject's preference. A living subject's case defaults to anonymization unless they explicitly prefer otherwise. Broca's patient was named posthumously because the historical record of the case became more important than his privacy — and he was beyond the reach of harm. The living are not.

Minimization of harm. A case study of one person's struggle with a stigmatized condition, language use, or personal history could expose them to consequences they did not anticipate when they consented. The careful researcher thinks about this in advance, not after.

Honest representation. The subject's words and circumstances should be represented accurately. Composite characters and conflated events are not permitted. The genre's credibility depends on the reader's confidence that the case is real and rendered faithfully.

For your student case study, IRB review is usually not required for an informal study of one person you know. Your instructor will tell you what level of review is needed at your institution. The institutional ethics apply regardless: consent, confidentiality, minimization of harm, accurate representation.

## Research methods for the student case study

A small repertoire of methods, calibrated to 1,000-1,200 words and a few weeks of work.

Observation means spending time around your subject when they are using language naturally and taking detailed notes on what you see. The notes must distinguish observation — what you actually saw and heard — from interpretation — what you think it meant. That discipline is what makes observation a research method rather than a personal impression. Your subject's awareness of being observed will affect their behavior; there is no clean fix. Acknowledge it in your analysis.

Interview means asking open-ended questions and recording the answers. Open-ended, specifically: *describe a time when you noticed yourself switching from one language to another*, not *do you code-switch?*. *Walk me through the difference between how you talk with your friends and how you talk with your parents*, not *do you use different language in different contexts?* Yes/no questions produce yes/no data. The interview is most useful when the subject is narrating and explaining, not confirming. Record with permission, or take careful notes; transcribe the passages you might quote before the context fades.

Document collection means examining specific examples of your subject's writing — text messages, emails, posts — with their permission. The documents are evidence. The permission should specifically cover documents you intend to quote.

For most student case studies, observation and interview together produce sufficient data. Document collection adds useful depth when accessible. The combination of what you saw, what the subject said, and what they wrote across contexts is where the patterns become visible.

## Structure — the case study assembled

A working structure that serves the genre at student scale.

**Background and context.** Who is the subject, and why is their case an interesting one for the question you are asking? What cultural and linguistic contexts shape their language use? The pseudonym, if applicable, is introduced here.

**Research question.** What specifically are you trying to learn, stated as an open question. *How does Maya, a Korean-American college student who speaks Korean at home and English at school, code-switch between the two languages in different social contexts?* The question should be answerable by the data you can collect and narrow enough that the 1,000-1,200 word report can actually address it.

**Methods and participant.** What did you do? How long? What kind of data did you collect? Number of observations, length of interview sessions, what documents you reviewed. Brief but specific.

**Findings.** Organized by the dimensions you were studying. *Maya's code-switching across three contexts: at home with parents, with Korean-American friends, with non-Korean classmates.* Each section presents observation, evidence, and analysis together — specific quotes, specific patterns, specific claims about what the data shows.

**Discussion.** What do the findings suggest? What patterns emerged that might generalize, and what is specific to this case? Where did your initial hypotheses hold, and where did the data complicate them?

**Limitations.** What did your methodology not capture? What more would you need to know to answer the larger question? The limitations section is the discipline that separates case study from anecdote. Without it, the study claims more than it can support.

**Conclusion and further research.** What does the case suggest for further study? Short.

## The generalization problem — the central tension

The tension every case study writer faces is between specificity — your subject is one specific person — and generalization — what about this case teaches about a broader pattern.

The two failures.

Over-generalization: the student writes a case study of one person's code-switching and concludes *this is how all bilingual speakers behave*. One person's behavior cannot establish a general pattern. You can hypothesize about broader patterns; you cannot prove them from a single case.

Under-generalization: the student writes a case study of one person's code-switching and concludes *Maya code-switches in these ways*, full stop. No reflection on what about the case might illuminate larger questions. The case study without any reach toward the general is just a detailed report with no analytical stakes.

The discipline is in the middle. You hypothesize about what might generalize — *Maya's pattern, where she shifts more readily into Korean in emotional contexts than in informational ones, is consistent with the pattern Schumann documented in second-language acquisition under conditions of emotional intimacy* — and you flag the limits — *whether this pattern holds beyond Maya's specific case would require larger samples*. The hypothesis and the limit together are what the genre rewards.

## A worked example, condensed

A student case study, written in 2023, of a college student named Mateo (pseudonym), native bilingual in Spanish and English, born and raised in Texas.

*Background:* Age 20. Spanish at home with immigrant parents from Oaxaca; English in school and at work. Lives in a dorm; has Spanish-speaking and English-speaking friends.

*Research question:* How does Mateo's code-switching between Spanish and English vary across contexts of emotional intimacy versus informational exchange?

*Methods:* Three weeks of observation (one shared class; three shared meals); one 45-minute recorded interview; review of approximately 50 text messages shared with permission.

*Findings, condensed:* Texts with his mother: predominantly Spanish. With Spanish-speaking friends: mixed roughly evenly. With English-speaking friends: predominantly English with occasional Spanish phrases for emphasis or humor. In observed conversation: Mateo defaults to Spanish in emotional registers — when discussing his grandmother's recent illness, when describing a fight with his brother — even with friends who would normally hear him in English. He defaults to English in informational registers — describing his job's procedures, recounting class content — even with Spanish-speaking friends. The pattern is consistent across all observed instances.

*Discussion:* The pattern is consistent with the broader literature on bilingual emotional language — use of the first language for emotional content is widely documented. What Mateo's case contributes is the fine granularity of the switching: within a single conversation, within seconds, the language shifts specifically with the topical register. The case does not establish this as a general pattern; it is consistent enough to suggest the hypothesis is worth testing in larger samples.

*Limitations:* One subject. Three weeks. The observer is the subject's friend, and Mateo's awareness of being studied may have affected his usage. Self-report in the interview is limited by Mateo's own metalinguistic awareness.

The case study is doing the genre's work. It hypothesizes specifically. It limits itself honestly. It locates itself in the existing literature without claiming to extend it definitively.

## A note about AI

A case study has the same vulnerability as a profile, plus one more.

The vulnerability the case study shares with the profile is that the substance requires irreplaceable work the model cannot have done — the interview, the observation, the actual specific person at the center of the study. A case study built without that work is not a case study; it is a generic essay disguised in case-study clothing.

The case study's distinct vulnerability is the generalization move — the careful claim that this one subject's specifics speak to a broader pattern. The model is trained on patterns, which means it will generalize too quickly, too smoothly, and from too little. Asked to discuss what Mateo's code-switching means about bilingual emotional language in general, the model will produce a confident discussion that treats one case as definitive when the case is only suggestive. The discipline of the genre is the discipline of not overclaiming, and the model has not internalized that discipline; if anything, it has internalized the opposite.

Where the model genuinely helps: after you have collected your data, you can paste your findings and ask the model to draft three possible interpretations of the same pattern — and then ask which interpretation the data does and does not distinguish among. You can ask it to identify limitations you have not yet named. You can ask it to push back on your generalization and require you to defend the scope of your claim.

Where the model does damage: producing the methods section as if research had happened, generating the discussion before the data is in, and softening the limitations into reassurance. Each substitutes the model's pattern-confidence for the writer's empirical honesty.

The rule: the case study lives in the gap between what one subject can support and what the writer might want to claim. The model does not feel that gap. You have to.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,000–1,200 word case study of one person's language use
for a college writing class.

My subject (pseudonymized): [briefly describe]
My research question: [state it as an open question]
My methods: observation over [N] weeks; interview of [N] minutes; document
review of [N] items.

Below I'm pasting my raw observation notes and the relevant portions of
the interview transcript.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Identify the three or four most specific patterns in my data. For
   each, quote the observations or transcript passages that support
   it. Patterns should be specific (not "subject uses both languages"
   but "subject uses Spanish for emotional content even with
   English-speaking listeners").

2. For each pattern, ask me whether the pattern is consistent across
   all observed contexts or whether there are exceptions. Quote any
   exceptions in my notes.

3. Once I've confirmed which patterns are consistent and which have
   exceptions, suggest where these patterns connect to existing
   research literature on language and bilingualism. Suggest specific
   research traditions to look at; do not invent citations.

4. Help me draft a research-question-to-findings outline. NOT the
   prose of the case study itself — the structural outline of which
   patterns go in which section, with the supporting evidence noted.

Do not draft the case study. Do not write the discussion section.
The writing is mine.

Here are my notes and transcript:
[paste]
```

**What this produces:** a sharpened pattern list, connections to the literature, and a structural outline. After this, you draft the case study yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For Cowork:* useful if your notes and transcript are in separate files. Have Cowork read them before the conversation.
- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model will sometimes invent patterns that are not in your data, particularly when the data is sparse. *Where in my notes did you see this pattern?* The verification keeps the analysis grounded.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. State what the Broca/Leborgne case contributed to the field of neurolinguistics in one sentence. Then name which of the three case-study functions it primarily demonstrates — depth, hypothesis generation, or counter-evidence — and defend the choice in two sentences. *(Tests: applying the three-function framework to a concrete example before applying it to your own case.)*

2. Write a research question for a hypothetical case study of a person you know who uses language in an interesting way. The question must be: open-ended (not answerable yes or no); specific enough to be addressed in 1,000–1,200 words; and anchored to a named, specific dimension of their language use (code-switching, register shifting, emoji use, online community language, etc.). *(Tests: the question-specification discipline before the research begins; distinguishing a research question from a topic.)*

3. Take any case study finding — from the Mateo example, from a published case, or from your own early observations — and write the over-generalization version and the under-generalization version of the same conclusion. Then write the middle version: the hypothesis with its limit. *(Tests: recognizing both failure modes and practicing the middle discipline that the genre rewards.)*

**Application**

4. Conduct a fifteen-minute observation of one person's natural language use — in person or in a digital context (a text exchange, a social media feed you can observe in real time with permission). Take notes during and immediately after. When you are done, go through your notes and mark every item as either O (observation — what you actually saw or heard) or I (interpretation — what you think it meant). How many items need to be re-labeled? *(Tests: the observation/interpretation discipline applied in real time; developing awareness of how readily interpretation enters what we think we are observing.)*

5. Draft three interview questions for the subject of your case study. Each must be open-ended, each must be designed to elicit narration rather than confirmation, and each must be specifically calibrated to the dimension of language use you are studying. For each question, write one sentence explaining what you expect the answer to tell you that observation alone cannot. *(Tests: interview question design as an analytical move; understanding what each method contributes that the others cannot.)*

6. Write the limitations section of a case study you have not yet completed — your own study or the Mateo example. List every methodological constraint that would prevent the findings from generalizing: the single subject, the observer effect, the timeframe, the self-report limits, the specific cultural context. Then write one sentence stating what further research would be needed to test whether the pattern generalizes. *(Tests: the limitations discipline as a pre-draft analytical habit rather than a post-hoc acknowledgment.)*

7. Write the background and context section for your case study subject: who they are (pseudonymized if appropriate), what cultural and linguistic contexts shape their language use, and why they are an interesting case for the specific question you are asking. The section should run 150–200 words. After writing it, ask: does the background establish *why this specific person* is worth studying, or does it describe a generic member of a demographic category? Revise until the answer is the former. *(Tests: the specificity of case selection — distinguishing a case chosen for what it can teach from a case chosen for convenience.)*

**Synthesis**

8. Collect at least two observation sessions and one interview segment on your case study subject. Write the findings section — organized by the dimensions you are studying, with specific quotes or observations as evidence for each claim. Then test every claim in your findings against the observation/interpretation distinction: is each claim reporting what you saw, or what you inferred from what you saw? Label which is which in your draft. *(Tests: full application of the findings-writing discipline to original data; the observation/interpretation distinction under the pressure of actual analysis.)*

9. Write the discussion section of your case study. It must: name the pattern that emerged; hypothesize what about it might generalize (citing at least one piece of existing literature where the pattern has been documented in other cases); and name what is specific to this case that would not generalize without further study. The hypothesis and the limit must both be present. After writing it, read the discussion against the Mateo discussion in this chapter: where is yours stronger, and where does it overgeneralize or under-generalize? *(Tests: integrating hypothesis and limit in original analytical prose; developing the self-diagnostic ability to see the generalization problem in your own writing.)*

**Challenge**

10. Find a published case study — in medicine, psychology, linguistics, law, business, or education — in a field adjacent to but not identical to your own case study topic. Read the limitations section. Then write 300–400 words: what does this researcher's acknowledgment of limitations tell you about what the case can and cannot teach? Does the limitations section strengthen or weaken your confidence in the study's findings, and why? There is no single correct answer; the quality of the response depends on the specificity of what you found in the limitations and the honesty of the analysis. *(Open-ended; extends the limitations discipline into a published case the student has to evaluate; develops awareness of how the genre's self-critical move functions across fields.)*

If the case study's hypothesis-generation function turned out to be empirically inferior to large-sample correlational analysis for generating real research insights — that the foundational cases (Broca's, Genie's, H.M.) were exceptions in a literature whose modal contribution was minor — I would revise the chapter's framing of the genre as analytically powerful. The evidence from the history of the human sciences runs the other way: many of the most important findings began as case studies. But the claim should be evaluable.

## Still unresolved

The case study's standing in research varies dramatically by field. Clinical medicine and neuropsychology treat it as foundational. Much of contemporary psychology treats it as inferior to randomized controlled trials and dismisses it as anecdotal. Sociology and anthropology are conflicted. Whether the genre is best taught as a method on its own terms or as a stepping stone toward larger-sample work is a pedagogical question I have not resolved. The chapter teaches it on its own terms. That is a choice, and it should remain contestable.
