# Chapter 19 — Textual Analysis

*Reading Closely Enough to Argue from Inside the Text.*

---

Here is the first sentence of a short story written in 1898:

> The leaves were so still that even Bibi thought it was going to rain.[^1]

[^1]: Kate Chopin, "The Storm" (written 1898, published 1969 in *The Complete Works of Kate Chopin*). Available via [The Kate Chopin International Society](https://www.katechopin.org/the-storm/).

I want to stay with that sentence for a while before we go anywhere else, because it is a better object for showing what textual analysis does than any description of the genre would be.

A casual reader sees weather. The sky is going to open. There is going to be a storm. The sentence is preparatory, functional, and unremarkable.

A close reader sees something different.

The sentence is built in passive construction: *the leaves were*. Agency belongs to the leaves — which have no agency, because leaves are vegetable. The construction signals from the first word that something is going to happen *to* the world of the story rather than *from* the human characters in it.

The word *still* is doing specific work. It is a state of stopped motion. The expectation of motion is implicit — leaves move when wind moves them. *Still* foregrounds the absence, which is the thing you notice about a thing that is unexpectedly not happening. The opening word is about an absence that predicts what is coming.

Then: *even Bibi thought it was going to rain*. The word *even* signals that Bibi is someone whose perceptions are normally unreliable or limited — someone whose noticing is not the default evidence for anything. The reader does not yet know who Bibi is. But the construction has already characterized the household: there is a child in it, and the child's perception is limited but real, and the narrator respects it enough to invoke it.

And then: *going to rain* is colloquial. It is household speech, not the speech of a meteorologist or a nineteenth-century narrator being formal. The narrator has slid into the household's register in the first sentence before we know the household.

All of that from twenty words. Not all of it is equally important to every reading. But none of it is accidental. Chopin is making choices, and the choices are doing work. The question that textual analysis asks is: *what work is this choice doing, and what does the work reveal?*

## The story and what it does

"The Storm" runs about 2,500 words. A married woman named Calixta has an extramarital encounter with a former lover while a thunderstorm keeps her husband and son sheltered at a nearby store. The story does not condemn her. The story does not punish her. The marriage continues intact afterward; she is more at peace, not less. Chopin wrote the story in 1898 and it was not published until 1969, partly because its frank treatment of female desire made it unpublishable in her lifetime.

The story has been read many ways: as feminist allegory, as a meditation on desire and social constraint, as a portrait of post-Reconstruction Louisiana, as a study in figurative language where the storm functions as a meteorological metaphor for the human encounter it makes possible. Each reading is defensible. Each is supported by specific evidence in the text.

What I want you to notice is how those readings were produced. Each one emerged from someone attending to the specific choices of the text — the language, the structure, the images — and asking what those choices add up to. The storm-as-metaphor reading comes from a reader who noticed that the storm's intensification and the encounter's intensification track each other, sentence by sentence. The social-constraint reading comes from a reader who noticed that the story's permissions and prohibitions are inscribed in the language used to describe them. The feminist reading comes from a reader who noticed that the woman's experience is rendered in a vocabulary that nineteenth-century writing typically reserved for moral condemnation, deployed here without the condemnation.

Different readers, attending to different things, producing different interpretations. All of them defensible. All of them working from the text outward, not from a thesis down into the text.

That outward movement — from specific textual choices to an interpretive claim — is the mechanism of textual analysis.

## What close reading actually means

The phrase *close reading* is used loosely. Let me make it specific.

Close reading is the practice of attending to what the text does at the level of word, sentence, paragraph, and structure — not just at the level of plot, theme, or topic. It is attending to the *exact* choices a writer makes and asking what those choices are accomplishing.

The opposite of close reading is reading for plot or summary — attending to what happens and to whom, tracking the events, following the arc. This is not a lesser kind of reading. It is necessary reading. But it is not the reading that produces textual analysis, because it does not attend to the choices that make the story what it is rather than something else.

Here is the difference demonstrated. A plot-level reading of the storm in "The Storm" says: there is a thunderstorm; the characters are separated by it; the encounter takes place during it. A close reading says: the storm's language and the encounter's language are doing identical work. Consider the passage when Calixta and the former lover Alcée reach the height of the storm:

The rain was coming down in sheets obscuring the view of far-off cabins and enveloping the distant wood in a gray mist. The playing of the lightning was incessant. A bolt struck a tall chinaberry tree at the edge of the field. It filled all visible space with a blinding light and the crash seemed to invade the very boards they stood upon.

And the passage that immediately follows, describing the encounter: the same vocabulary — sheets, blinding, invading — applied to both the physical and the human. The storm is not a frame for the encounter. It *is* the encounter, in parallel language. Chopin has made the meteorological and the erotic the same event, happening in the same sentences.

That is a close-reading observation. And from that observation, an interpretive claim follows: the story is suggesting that the desire is as natural, as irresistible, and as amoral as the weather. The storm does not have an opinion about itself. The story does not have an opinion about Calixta's desire. The parallel construction is the argument.

## The interpretive community

Here is the question I have not yet answered: if the story is doing a specific thing, why do different readers see different things in it?

The literary theorist Stanley Fish proposed an answer in the 1970s: *interpretive communities*. The idea is that readers are not neutral. Every reader brings to a text a set of assumptions, training, cultural background, prior reading, and political commitments that shape what they see. These assumptions are not individual quirks — they are shared by communities of readers who have been trained in the same frameworks, are asking the same kinds of questions, and have been formed by the same conversations.

A feminist critic reading "The Storm" in 2024 will attend to different choices than a Catholic moral theologian reading it in 1898, not because the text is different but because the readers are asking different questions of it. Both are doing real reading. Both will find real things. They are not finding the same things.

For your textual analysis essay, three implications follow from this.

The first: your reading of the text will be shaped by what you bring to it. You should acknowledge that framing, at least implicitly. Not in a confessional way — you do not need to begin your essay with a paragraph about your personal history. But the essay should name the kind of question it is asking, the interpretive frame it is operating in. *I am reading this as a text about social constraints on desire* is an implicit announcement of the interpretive community you are working from. It allows the reader to know what kind of reading they are getting.

The second: other interpretive communities have read your text before you. The strongest textual analyses engage with those other readings — not necessarily to refute them, but to locate your reading in relation to them. You are not reading in isolation. You are reading in a conversation. The essay should show that you know what the conversation has already said.

The third: the discipline of textual analysis is the discipline of *specific textual evidence*. Your reading is a claim. The claim has to be supported. The support has to come from the text — from what the text actually does at the level of specific language. A reading that cannot be anchored in specific passages, with close-reading work on each passage, is not a textual analysis. It is an impression.

## The literary present tense

A mechanical point with real implications.

When you write about a literary text, use the present tense: *Chopin's narrator observes* rather than *Chopin's narrator observed*. The convention has a reason: the text continues to do what it does every time it is read. The story is, in a real sense, always happening on its pages. Past tense would suggest it stopped happening.

The convention applies even when the events are clearly historical in setting. *In* Hamlet, *the prince hesitates*, not *the prince hesitated*. The action is in the text, not in some historical Denmark.

The exception is when you are discussing the text's composition or publication history: *Chopin wrote "The Storm" in 1898; the story was not published until 1969*. Here the past tense is correct because you are describing historical events that have a before and after.

The working rule: past tense for what happened to the text in the world; present tense for what the text does on the page.

## What the thesis looks like

The thesis of a textual analysis is the interpretive claim your reading is going to defend. It is the answer to the question: *what is this text doing, and what does its doing reveal?*

Several different kinds of claim are available.

A formal claim attends to the text's structural or stylistic choices. *Chopin's storm is not a frame around the affair but a mechanism for it — the meteorological and the human are doing the same work in the same passages.* This claim commits the writer to showing the parallel construction sentence by sentence.

A thematic claim attends to what the text is about, argued through what it does. *"The Storm" is best read not as a story about adultery but as a story about the social structures that construct the category of adultery — and about what happens when those structures are temporarily suspended.* This claim requires showing specifically where the story's language encodes those structures.

A historical claim attends to what the text is doing in its specific moment. *"The Storm" reads differently in 1898, in 1969, and in 2024, and the history of its reception is itself an analytical object.* This claim requires historical context and evidence of reception history.

A comparative claim attends to how the text does what a tradition does, by setting it beside another text. *Chopin's treatment of female desire in "The Storm" differs from Edith Wharton's treatment in specific ways that illuminate what each writer is making possible.* This claim requires close reading of both texts.

All of these are legitimate. The choice depends on what the text and your reading actually support. The wrong choice is the thesis that sounds analytical but makes no specific claim: *Chopin uses the storm as a symbol for the emotions of the characters.* That is the grammar of an interpretive claim without the interpretive content. It could be said about almost any story with weather in it.

## How the structure works

A textual analysis has five structural moments.

The introduction names the text, the author, and the date. It contextualizes the text briefly — what kind of work it is, what its publication or reception history is, what interpretive questions it has raised. It names the interpretive question you are pursuing and often ends with a tentative thesis.

The brief summary covers the text's basic content in a paragraph or two, enough that a reader who has not read the text can follow the analysis. Keep it short. The summary is not the analysis. It is the minimum scaffolding for the analysis to be legible.

The body is the close reading organized around your interpretive claim. Specific passages are quoted. The analysis works on each passage specifically — at the level of words and sentences — building evidence for the interpretation. The body is where the work lives.

The engagement with other readings names the critical conversation your essay is entering. Not exhaustively. The relevant question is: what would a reader who is doing a *different* kind of reading of this text see? Where would they agree with you, and where would the evidence pull them somewhere else? You locate your reading in relation to those others without needing to refute them all.

The conclusion restates the interpretation with the weight of the evidence behind it, and opens outward: what does this reading reveal about the text, the author's work, the genre, the cultural moment? The conclusion is not the thesis again. It is the thesis after the evidence has earned it.

## A practical note on choosing your text

For a 2,000 to 3,000 word analysis, you want a text short enough to engage closely and rich enough to repay sustained attention. A short story works well. A poem can work even better — poems are often short enough to close-read word by word. A literary essay, a memoir chapter, a speech: all workable. A whole novel or a poetry collection is too much; you would end up summarizing rather than analyzing.

The practical test for richness: can you find two or three passages in the text that are doing something more than what a surface reading sees? If yes, the text has enough interior complexity to sustain analysis. If every sentence is doing exactly what it appears to be doing, the text is transparent — and transparent texts are hard to analyze.

The practical test for length: can you read the whole thing? For a short story or a poem, yes. For a novel, you are choosing a chapter or a section, not the whole. Name that choice and defend it in the essay.

## A note about AI

The textual analysis is the genre where the model is most likely to produce interpretation that looks like analysis and is not.

The reason is structural. The model has been trained on many readings of many canonical texts. When you ask it to analyze a text, it produces an average of those readings — a synthesis of what others have said, rendered in the vocabulary of textual analysis. The result has the grammar of analysis, the vocabulary of close reading, the structure of an interpretive claim. It does not have the one thing that gives textual analysis its value: a reading produced by your specific encounter with the specific text.

Three places where the model is genuinely useful. After you have done your own close reading, you can paste a passage and ask the model to identify word repetitions, image patterns, or structural moves you may have missed. The model is mechanically good at spotting patterns. You then interpret them. You can ask the model to construct the strongest counter-interpretation to the reading you are developing — the case for why a reader attending to the same text might arrive somewhere different. The pushback sharpens the analysis. You can ask the model where your text sits in the broader critical conversation — what major interpretive frames have been applied to it — and then verify whatever it tells you, because the model sometimes invents critics or misattributes claims.

Three places where the model does damage. Asking it to produce a reading — the reading will be competent and averaged and not yours. Asking it to supply quotations — the model sometimes fabricates passages that do not appear in the text, and every quotation you use must be verified against the source. Asking it to revise your prose toward greater analytical fluency — this reliably converts specific observations into vaguer sentences that sound like analysis without doing the close-reading work. *The author uses imagery to suggest* is the model's default. That sentence is the death of textual analysis. Every time you find yourself writing it, go back to the specific passage and start again.

## Exercises

The chapter's inferred learning objectives: (1) perform close reading at the level of word and sentence — identifying specific choices and naming what work those choices are doing; (2) distinguish close reading from plot summary and thematic impression, and explain what each kind of reading produces; (3) apply the interpretive community concept to a text — naming the frame the reading is operating from and locating it in relation to other possible readings; (4) construct a defensible textual analysis thesis of one of the four types (formal, thematic, historical, comparative) and distinguish it from the empty-claim thesis; (5) analyze a text's structural moments and evaluate whether the body's close reading earns the conclusion.

---

**Warm-up**

*1. Close reading in one sentence.* The chapter close-reads the opening sentence of "The Storm" — twenty words — and produces five distinct observations. Apply the same discipline to one sentence from any text you have read. Quote the sentence. Then write five observations about specific choices in it: word selection, syntax, register, what is foregrounded, what is implied. Each observation should name a specific feature of the sentence and say what it is doing. Do not interpret the whole story. Work only on the sentence.
*(Tests objective 1 — close reading at the sentence level.)*
*Difficulty: Low.*

*2. Summary vs. close reading.* The chapter distinguishes plot-level reading from close reading by demonstrating both on the storm passage. Apply the same distinction to a passage of your choice — any prose passage of three to five sentences. Write two versions: a plot-level summary (what happens) and a close-reading account (what choices are being made and what they are doing). Then write one sentence explaining the difference in what each version reveals.
*(Tests objective 2 — distinguishing the two kinds of reading and articulating what each produces.)*
*Difficulty: Low.*

---

**Application**

*3. Interpretive frame.* The chapter describes three readings of "The Storm" (storm-as-metaphor, social-constraint, feminist) and explains that each emerged from a different interpretive community asking different questions of the same text. Choose any text you know well and identify two different interpretive communities that have read it or could read it. For each: name the community's framework, name the question it would ask of the text, and identify one specific passage in the text that each community would attend to and why. Write 200–300 words.
*(Tests objective 3 — applying the interpretive community concept to a specific text.)*
*Difficulty: Medium.*

*4. Thesis construction.* The chapter gives four types of thesis (formal, thematic, historical, comparative) and one example of an empty thesis. For the text you are working with, write one thesis of each type — four total. Then evaluate which is best supported by the specific textual evidence you have found in your close reading, and write 100 words explaining why.
*(Tests objective 4 — constructing and evaluating four thesis types.)*
*Difficulty: Medium.*

*5. Counter-interpretation.* Take the working thesis you developed in Exercise 4. Write the strongest possible counter-interpretation: a one-paragraph reading of the same text that attends to the same evidence and arrives at a different conclusion. Quote at least one passage to support the counter-reading. Then write 100 words explaining what you would have to do in your analysis to address this counter-reading — not refute it necessarily, but show that your reading accounts for the same evidence more fully.
*(Tests objectives 3 and 4 — locating a reading in relation to other possible readings and anticipating the challenge.)*
*Difficulty: Medium.*

---

**Synthesis**

*6. Five-moment structure audit.* Take any published textual analysis — from a literary journal, a critical anthology, a course packet, or a textbook. Map it to the chapter's five structural moments: introduction, brief summary, body/close reading, engagement with other readings, conclusion. For each moment, write two sentences: one describing what the essay does at that moment and one evaluating whether it does what the chapter says it should do. Then identify the one moment where the essay is weakest and explain what it would need to do differently.
*(Tests objective 5 — analyzing structure and evaluating whether the close reading earns the conclusion.)*
*Difficulty: Medium-high.*

*7. Full draft of one body paragraph.* Write one body paragraph of your own textual analysis — the first paragraph of the close-reading body, not the introduction. The paragraph must: quote a specific passage from your text; perform close reading on at least two features of the quoted passage (word choice, syntax, image, structural position); and draw an inference from the close reading that advances your thesis. After the paragraph, write three sentences naming the close-reading moves you made and evaluating whether each one earns the inference it supports.
*(Tests all five objectives together — the student is now doing the work the chapter describes.)*
*Difficulty: High.*

---

**Challenge**

*8. The mechanism question.* The chapter opens by arguing that textual analysis moves outward from specific choices to interpretive claims — not downward from a thesis into the text. It also acknowledges that different interpretive communities produce different defensible readings of the same text. These two claims are in tension: if the reading is supposed to come from the text, why do different readers trained in different frameworks find different things? Write a 400–600 word argument addressing this tension directly. Is the tension a problem for the chapter's account of how textual analysis works, or is it resolved by the chapter? If you think it is unresolved, propose a resolution. Use at least one specific textual example to anchor your argument.
*(Open-ended. Tests all five objectives and asks the student to argue about the theory of the genre, not just practice it.)*
*Difficulty: High.*

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 19

**What you're building:** the analytical scaffold for your textual analysis — patterns observed, interpretive thesis, engagement with other readings — before drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 2,000–3,000 word textual analysis of [text title] by
[author], for a college writing class.

I have read the text closely. Below I'm pasting the text (or a link
to it).

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Identify three to four patterns in the text — patterns of word
   repetition, image repetition, structural choice, or rhythmic move
   that recur across the text. Quote the specific passages where each
   pattern appears. Do not yet interpret.

2. Ask me four diagnostic questions about which patterns I think
   matter most and what interpretive claim each one would support.
   Push back if my interpretation seems thin.

3. After I respond, propose a one-sentence thesis statement that
   commits to a specific reading of the text supported by the
   patterns I identified.

4. Once I have a working thesis, suggest where my reading sits in
   the broader critical conversation about this text. Where would
   feminist critics agree or push back? Formalists? Historicists?
   Suggest specific kinds of reading, not specific scholars I have
   not verified.

5. Construct the strongest counter-interpretation to my reading.
   Where could a reader, looking at the same text, reach a different
   conclusion? Quote the textual evidence that would support that
   counter-reading.

Do not draft the analysis. Do not write the introduction. The reading
is mine.

Here is the text:
[paste or link]
```

**What this produces:** a pattern inventory, a working thesis, a position in the critical conversation, and a counter-interpretation you will have to address. After this, you draft the analysis yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

For ChatGPT or Gemini, the prompt works as written. For Cowork, useful if the text is in a file you want to keep accessible across sessions. For Claude Code, not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the model proposes interpretations, it will sometimes invent textual evidence that does not exist or attribute patterns to passages that do not actually contain them. Verify every quoted passage against the text. *Where in the text does this appear?* The verification is part of the discipline.
