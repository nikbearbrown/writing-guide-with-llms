# Chapter 19 — Textual Analysis

*Reading Closely Enough to Argue from Inside the Text.*

A specific opening to a piece of writing, from 1898.

> The leaves were so still that even Bibi thought it was going to rain. Bobinôt, who was accustomed to converse on terms of perfect equality with his little son, called the child's attention to certain sombre clouds that were rolling with sinister intention from the west, accompanied by a sullen, threatening roar.[^1]

[^1]: Kate Chopin, "The Storm" (written 1898, published 1969 in *The Complete Works of Kate Chopin*). Available via [The Kate Chopin International Society](https://www.katechopin.org/the-storm/).

That is the opening of Kate Chopin's short story "The Storm," written in 1898 and not published until 1969 — partly because the story's frank treatment of female sexuality made it unpublishable in Chopin's lifetime. The story runs about 2,500 words. In those words, a married woman named Calixta has an extramarital encounter with a former lover during a thunderstorm. The story does not condemn her. The story does not punish her. The marriage continues, intact, after the encounter; the woman is more at peace afterward, not less.

The story has been read in many ways. As a feminist allegory. As a meditation on desire and constraint. As a portrait of the social conventions of post-Reconstruction Louisiana. As a study in figurative language — the storm functions, in the story's mechanism, as a meteorological metaphor for the human encounter it makes possible. As a commentary on language reclamation — the story takes a vocabulary that nineteenth-century readers would have associated with sin and uses it to render something the story refuses to call sinful.

Each reading is defensible. Each reading is supported by specific evidence in the text. Each reading was produced by a different *interpretive community* — a different group of readers, with different assumptions, asking different questions of the text.

The genre that produces such readings is called *textual analysis*. The assignment at the end of this chapter is your own textual analysis of a literary text of your choice. Approximately 2,000 to 3,000 words. A specific thesis you defend through close reading.

## What textual analysis is, and what it isn't

*Textual analysis* is an essay that examines a literary or non-fiction text closely enough to produce an interpretation defensible from specific evidence in the text itself.

What it is not:

- *A book report.* The book report summarizes what the text says. The textual analysis argues for what the text *does* or *means*.
- *A review.* The review evaluates the text. The textual analysis interprets it. The two genres overlap, but textual analysis is more focused on *what* the text is doing and *how* than on *whether the text is good*.
- *A summary with literary terms sprinkled in.* Some student textual analyses default to plot summary plus phrases like *the author uses imagery to* without doing real interpretive work. The interpretation is the work.
- *A guess at what the author meant.* Some textual analysis traditions are deliberately *anti-intentionalist* — they argue that what matters is what the text does, not what the author intended. You do not need to know what Kate Chopin intended in 1898 to analyze what "The Storm" achieves. The text is the evidence.

The genre treats the text as something with a structure, a set of moves, a configuration of choices, and asks: *how does this text work, and what does its working reveal*?

## What it means to read closely

The phrase *close reading* gets used loosely. Let me make it specific.

Close reading is the practice of attending to *exactly what the text does* at the level of word, sentence, paragraph, and structure — not just at the level of plot, theme, or topic.

A short demonstration. Look again at the opening sentence of "The Storm":

> The leaves were so still that even Bibi thought it was going to rain.

A casual reader sees a description of weather. A close reader sees the following:

The sentence is in passive construction: *the leaves were*. The agency in the sentence is in the leaves — which is no agency at all, since leaves are vegetable. The construction signals that something is going to happen *to* the world of the story, not *from* the human characters.

The word *still* carries specific weight. It is a state of stopped motion. The expectation of motion is implicit — leaves move when wind moves them. *Still* signals their unnaturalness; the absence is what is foregrounded.

The clause *even Bibi thought it was going to rain* tells you something about Bibi without telling you who Bibi is yet. *Even Bibi*: Bibi is someone whose perception is normally unreliable or limited. The reader can infer (and learns in the next sentence) that Bibi is a young child. The construction has done the work of characterization in three words.

The phrase *going to rain* is colloquial. It is the speech of a household, not the language of a meteorologist. The narrator is sliding into the household's register from the first sentence.

Each of these observations is doing close-reading work. The sentence is doing *more* than describing weather. It is establishing tone, characterizing a household, signaling agency, and creating a particular atmospheric weight. The interpretation of the story can be built from these specific observations.

A textual analysis is sustained close reading. The analyst moves through the text — not necessarily linearly, but with attention to specific passages — and builds an interpretation from what the close reading reveals.

## The interpretive community — and what it means for your essay

The phrase *interpretive community* was popularized by the literary theorist Stanley Fish in the 1970s. The idea: readers are not neutral. Every reader brings to a text a set of assumptions, training, cultural background, prior reading, and political commitments that shape what they see in the text.

This is not a bug. It is the genre's actual condition.

A feminist literary critic reading "The Storm" in 2024 will see different things than a Catholic moral theologian reading it in 1898 will see. Both will be right about *something*. They will not see the same things. They are members of different interpretive communities.

For your textual analysis essay, three implications.

**Name your interpretive frame, at least implicitly.** Your reading of the text is going to be shaped by what you bring to it. Acknowledge what you are bringing. The acknowledgment can be subtle — a paragraph that names the kind of question you are asking, the kind of reading you are doing — but it should be present.

**Engage with other interpretations seriously.** Your reading is one of several. The strongest textual analyses engage with the other major readings — not to refute them, necessarily, but to locate the analyst's own reading in relation to them.

**Defend your reading specifically.** What separates a textual analysis from a personal opinion is the discipline of *specific textual evidence*. Every interpretive claim needs to be anchored in what the text actually does. The reader who disagrees with your interpretation should be able to follow your reasoning even if they do not accept the conclusion.

## The literary present tense

A small mechanical point with substantive implications.

When you write about a literary text, use the *present tense*: "Chopin's narrator observes" rather than "Chopin's narrator observed." The convention has a reason: the text continues to do what it does every time it is read. The story is, in a real sense, always happening on its pages. Past tense would suggest it stopped.

The convention applies even when the events of the text are clearly past. *In* Hamlet, *the prince hesitates*. Not *the prince hesitated*. The action is in the text, not in the historical Denmark of the play.

The exception is when you are discussing the text's historical situation — Chopin wrote the story in 1898, the story was not published until 1969 — where the past tense is correct. The distinction:

- *Chopin wrote* "The Storm" in 1898 — past tense, about a historical event.
- *The narrator describes* the leaves as still — present tense, about what the text does.

Get this right consistently. Mixing the two tenses confuses the reader about what register your analysis is operating in.

## Choosing your text

For a 2,000 to 3,000 word essay, you want a text that is short enough to engage with closely and rich enough to repay sustained attention.

Some categories that work well.

**A short story.** Chopin's "The Storm," Jamaica Kincaid's "Girl," Flannery O'Connor's "A Good Man Is Hard to Find," Lorrie Moore's "How to Become a Writer," Ted Chiang's "Story of Your Life," James Baldwin's "Sonny's Blues." Short fiction is structurally bounded; you can engage with the whole text.

**A poem.** Brooks's "We Real Cool" (eight lines; you can analyze every word). Whitman's "When I Heard the Learn'd Astronomer." Anything by Lucille Clifton, Robert Frost, Tracy K. Smith, Ocean Vuong. Poems are usually short enough to close-read entirely.

**A short essay.** Joan Didion's "The Year of Magical Thinking." James Baldwin's "Notes of a Native Son." Susan Sontag's "Notes on Camp" (selected sections). Eula Biss's "Time and Distance Overcome." Short literary essays can be analyzed for argument structure, voice, evidence patterns, and rhetorical moves.

**A chapter from a longer work.** One specific chapter of a novel or memoir, treated as a self-contained unit. This is harder than the short story but workable if the chapter is structurally bounded.

What does not work well: a whole novel, a whole poetry collection, a whole memoir. Too much text for 2,000 to 3,000 words of analysis to engage closely. You would end up summarizing more than analyzing.

## Structure — what a textual analysis contains

A working shape.

**Introduction.** Names the text, the author, and the date. Briefly contextualizes the text — what kind of work it is, what its publication or composition history is, what readers have made of it. Names the interpretive question you are pursuing. Often ends with a tentative thesis.

**Brief summary.** A paragraph that summarizes the text's basic content, for readers who may not have read it. Keep it short — not the focus of the essay. Just enough so the rest of the analysis is legible.

**The body — the close reading.** Organized around your interpretive claim. Specific passages quoted, with close-reading work on each. The analysis should accumulate evidence that supports your interpretation.

**Engagement with other readings.** Where do other interpretations of this text exist? Where do they agree with yours and where do they diverge? You do not have to refute them; you have to locate your reading in relation.

**Conclusion.** The interpretation restated with the weight of the evidence behind it. What the reading reveals about the text, the author's work, the genre, or the broader question your reading was about.

The structure scales. A short story can be analyzed in 2,000 words. A single chapter of a novel might need 2,500. A short essay or a poem might be done in 1,800.

## Choosing your thesis

The thesis is the analytical claim your reading is going to defend. Some specific kinds.

**A formal claim.** The text's specific structural choice is doing specific interpretive work. *Chopin's storm is not a frame around the affair but a mechanism for it — the meteorological and the human are doing the same work in the same passages.*

**A thematic claim.** The text is engaging a specific theme in a specific way. *"The Storm" is best read not as a story about adultery but as a story about the social structures that produce the conditions for what the period called adultery, and that produce different conditions when those structures are temporarily suspended.*

**A historical claim.** The text is doing specific work in its historical context. *"The Storm" reads in 1898 as transgressive in ways it cannot read in 1969 (when it was first published) or 2024, and the changing reception of the story is itself an analytical object.*

**A comparative claim.** The text does something that another text does not, or does what a tradition does in a specific way. *Chopin's treatment of female desire in "The Storm" differs from her contemporary Edith Wharton's treatment in specific ways that illuminate both writers.*

Each of these is defensible. Each commits the writer to specific kinds of evidence. The choice depends on what the text and your reading actually support.

## A note about AI

The textual analysis is the genre where the LLM is most likely to produce competent-looking interpretation that does not have a specific reading underneath it.

Useful:

*Identifying patterns in the text.* Paste a passage and ask: *What patterns of word repetition, image repetition, or rhythmic recurrence do you see in this passage?* The model is mechanically good at spotting these. You then interpret what they mean.

*Locating your text in critical conversations.* Ask: *What are the major published readings of this text? What interpretive frames have been applied?* The model will sometimes produce useful surveys. Verify everything against actual sources before citing.

*Stress-testing your interpretation.* Paste your thesis and ask: *What is the strongest case against this reading? What evidence in the text complicates it?* The pushback often sharpens the analysis.

Damaging:

*Producing a reading.* The model will produce an interpretation when asked. The interpretation will average the readings the model has seen in its training data. It will not be a reading produced by your specific encounter with the text.

*Quoting the text.* Models sometimes invent passages that don't exist in the actual text. Only use quotations you have verified against the source.

*Generic claims.* The model tends toward *this passage uses imagery to suggest...* — sentences that sound like analysis without doing the close-reading work. Read every analytical sentence the model produces against the specific text. The specificity is what distinguishes analysis from the appearance of analysis.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 19

**What you're building:** the analytical scaffold for your textual analysis — patterns observed, interpretive thesis, engagement with other readings — before drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 2,000–3,000 word textual analysis of [text title] by
[author], for a college writing class.

I have read the text closely. Below I'm pasting the text (or a link
to it).

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Identify three to four patterns in the text — patterns of word
   repetition, image repetition, structural choice, or rhythmic move
   that recur across the text. Quote the specific passages where each
   pattern appears. Do not yet interpret.

2. Ask me four diagnostic questions about which patterns I think
   matter most and what interpretive claim each one would support.
   Push back if my interpretation seems thin.

3. After I respond, propose a one-sentence thesis statement that
   commits to a specific reading of the text supported by the
   patterns I identified.

4. Once I have a working thesis, suggest where my reading sits in
   the broader critical conversation about this text. Where would
   feminist critics agree or push back? Formalists? Historicists?
   Suggest specific kinds of reading, not specific scholars I have
   not verified.

5. Construct the strongest counter-interpretation to my reading.
   Where could a reader, looking at the same text, reach a different
   conclusion? Quote the textual evidence that would support that
   counter-reading.

Do not draft the analysis. Do not write the introduction. The reading
is mine.

Here is the text:
[paste or link]
```

**What this produces:** a pattern inventory, a working thesis, a position in the critical conversation, and a counter-interpretation you will have to address. After this, you draft the analysis yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful if the text is in a file you want to keep accessible across sessions.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the model proposes interpretations, it will sometimes invent textual evidence that does not exist or attribute patterns to passages that do not actually contain them. Verify every quoted passage against the text. *Where in the text does this appear?* The verification is part of the discipline.

## What would change my mind

If careful comparison showed that students who learned textual analysis through close-reading discipline produced no better interpretive work than students who learned it through pattern-spotting and theme identification, I would revise the chapter's central emphasis on close reading at the sentence-and-word level. The evidence from the discipline is that close reading produces better analysis — but the claim is not unfalsifiable.

## Still puzzling

The relationship between *what the text does* and *what the author intended* is more contested in literary studies than this chapter pretends. Anti-intentionalist criticism (the New Criticism, much later structuralism and post-structuralism) holds that author intention is unknowable and irrelevant. Other traditions (much of historical criticism, much of post-colonial criticism) treat author intention and biographical context as part of the analytical object. I have taught this chapter from the anti-intentionalist position, but the position is not the only defensible one. Students working in different traditions will need different advice than this chapter gives.
