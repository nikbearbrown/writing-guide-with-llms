# Chapter 23 — Portfolio Reflection

*Looking Back at What You Now Know.*

---

In 1983, a thirty-year-old writer named Sandra Cisneros published a novel-in-vignettes called *The House on Mango Street*. The book sold modestly for years. It became, slowly, one of the most assigned books in American secondary and college education. In 2009, twenty-five years after the original publication, the publisher released an anniversary edition with a new introduction. Cisneros — now in her mid-fifties — wrote that introduction in 2008.

She did not summarize the book. She did not explain it. She wrote, in the third person, about *the young woman who wrote it*. About what that young woman had not yet understood. About the specific apartment in Chicago where the book was written. About the chair she had bought with her first publishing advance. About the cat. About her father — whose immigrant aspirations had shaped her childhood and shaped the book.[^1]

[^1]: Sandra Cisneros, "A House of My Own," in *A House of My Own: Stories from My Life* (Knopf, 2015). The introduction was originally written for the 25th anniversary edition of *The House on Mango Street*.

What Cisneros did in that introduction is reflection. Not summary. Not analysis. Not argument. The deliberate act of looking back at the work, the writer, the moment, the shape of how one became the other — and articulating something that the moment itself could not have articulated.

That is the move this final chapter teaches.

You have spent a semester writing in a range of genres. Essays, narratives, analyses, arguments, proposals, reviews. You have used LLMs as research partners, interrogators, drafting assistants. You have been graded. You have revised. You have failed and succeeded in specific ways.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to look back at all of it and articulate, in a piece of reflective writing of your own, what you now know about yourself as a writer that you did not know on the first day of the semester. The piece runs between 1,500 and 2,500 words. The form is a letter to a reader — usually your instructor, but written in a register that is honest rather than performative.

---

## What reflective writing is — and why the genre is harder than it looks

Reflective writing is the genre that emerges from looking back at experience and articulating what the experience taught — not what was supposed to be learned, but what specifically was. The form has many versions; Montaigne in the 1580s was writing reflectively, Augustine in 397 was writing reflectively, Cisneros in 2008 was writing reflectively. The move is consistent across all of them: turn experience into articulated understanding.

What makes the genre harder than it looks is the same thing that makes it valuable. The reflection asks for honesty plus specificity — two qualities that are individually attainable but consistently harder to sustain together.

The failure modes are symmetrical. The reflection that is all praise of one's own effort — *I worked hard, I learned a lot, I grew as a writer* — is filler. These sentences have been written so many times in reflective writing that they have ceased to carry information. The reader cannot tell, from them, what specifically changed. On the other side, excessive self-criticism does the same thing from the opposite direction. Both dodge the specific work the genre requires.

The reflection that is mostly summary — listing the assignments, describing the semester — is not yet reflective. Summary names what happened. Reflection asks what the happening changed.

The reflection that evaluates against the rubric — *I met the learning objectives for rhetoric; I need to work on citation discipline* — is useful for one purpose and not for what the genre is asking. The rubric assesses against external standards. Reflection asks what *you* now understand about your own writing.

The reflection that was produced through heavy LLM-smoothing reads as if someone other than you is reporting on your semester. The reader can tell. The genre's currency is the writer's specific honesty. The LLM's currency is generic plausibility. The two are not the same currency, and passing one off as the other is visible in the prose.

---

## What the reflection is actually for

Some honesty about the pedagogy.

The reflective assignment is not just for the instructor's assessment. It is for you — to make explicit, in writing, things about your own writing practice that you would otherwise carry only as unarticulated habit.

The thing about a habit you cannot articulate is that you cannot change it. You may continue to overuse passive voice, or to write opening paragraphs that delay your real argument, or to undercite, or to lean on adjectives for force, or to produce competent prose that is somehow missing your specific voice — without ever noticing. The act of writing a reflection forces the noticing. The noticing is what makes change possible.

This is the real purpose of the genre. The grade is a useful structural device for getting you to do the work. The work itself is the value.

Cisneros writing about the young woman who wrote *The House on Mango Street* was not producing a reflection for an instructor. She was producing an account that she herself needed — of who that person was, what she did not yet know, what the making of the book cost her. The reflection served the writer. It then served the reader who wanted to understand both.

---

## Three moves — and why each is necessary

Reflection is not a single act. It moves through three phases, usually recursively rather than in strict sequence.

Description names what happened. Specific assignments, specific moments of difficulty, specific revisions, specific surprises. The description is the foundation; without it, nothing else has ground to stand on.

Reflection articulates what the moments meant. What they revealed about you. What they revealed about writing. Where they pushed against your prior assumptions. This is the layer most students underwrite — they describe the assignment but stop short of naming what it taught.

Forward projection names what you will carry. Not generic plans — *I will write more carefully* is not a forward projection, it is a wish. A forward projection sounds like: *I now know that my first drafts almost always need their opening paragraphs cut, because I write to find out what I'm writing about and then need to discard the finding-out*. The projection is specific enough to be actionable.

The reflection that has all three moves present and doing their work is doing the genre's work. The reflection that has only one is missing something real.

## Where to look for what changed

The honest reflection asks: *what did I not know that I now know*? Some specific places where the evidence of change tends to accumulate.

Your prose at the sentence level. Compare a paragraph from your first assignment to a paragraph from your most recent one. What is different? Sentence length? Verb choice? Use of subordinate clauses? Use of passive voice? The differences are the evidence. The reflection names them and asks why they changed.

Your approach to argument. Did your sense of what *argument* means shift over the semester? Did you come in believing argument meant heat and find that it can mean measured exposition of a contested position? Did you discover that you have actual positions on contested questions, and that writing the position argument changed your relationship to those questions?

Your relationship to research. Did the research process change? Did you discover kinds of sources you did not know existed? Did your sense of what *credibility* means shift? Did the research log discipline change how much work you kept versus lost?

Your relationship to revision. Did you learn to approach revision differently? Did you find specific revision moves — opening-cuts, sentence-combining, evidence-strengthening — that you now reach for? Or did revision remain something you did reluctantly to meet requirements?

Your relationship to LLMs. This semester is one of the first college writing semesters in which the LLM question was genuinely central to the course. Did your sense of when to use the tools and when not to shift? Did a specific experience — a prompt that went well, a prompt that produced something hollow — change your approach? Where do you stand now on the question, in your own practice?

Your voice. Did your voice on the page change? In what direction? Did you become more or less recognizably *yourself* in your prose? Where in your portfolio do you sound most like yourself, and where do you sound like someone else?

The reflection that engages with two or three of these specifically, with evidence, is doing better work than the reflection that gestures at all of them.

## Evidence — what to quote from your portfolio

The reflection needs evidence. Not page-counts of your portfolio; specific quoted passages from your own work.

Pick two or three short passages from across the semester — one from early, one from middle, one from late. Read them as if they were written by someone else. What do you notice?

A worked example, condensed.

A student's first-assignment opening:

> Throughout my life, I have always loved to read. Reading is very important to me, and books have been an important part of my growth. In this paper, I will be discussing my favorite books.

A student's late-semester opening, same writer:

> The book my grandmother handed me when I was eight had a cover held together with tape, and the back third smelled like the inside of her coat closet — wool, cedar, the small jar of Vicks she kept in there for headaches. I was eight. I asked if I could borrow it. She said no. She said I could have it.

The two openings are by the same person. The reflection on these passages writes itself — but only because the writer is willing to look at them honestly.

Some specifics of what changed between them. The first defaults to summary (*throughout my life*); the second locates a specific moment. The first uses abstract vocabulary (*important*, *growth*); the second uses sensory specifics (*wool, cedar, Vicks*). The first announces its subject (*in this paper, I will be discussing*); the second begins inside the scene. The first opens with the writer's general claim; the second opens with the world.

These are real shifts. The reflection can name them, point to the evidence, and articulate — this is the hard part — what made them possible.

## Voice — the specific problem of reflective prose

A practical caution.

The vocabulary of self-improvement — *growth*, *journey*, *transformation*, *grown as a writer* — has been written into so many reflective assignments that the words have lost specific meaning. A reflection that uses these words without specific content underneath them reads as filler, regardless of how much effort went into producing it.

A simple discipline: avoid the abstract reflective vocabulary unless the specific content is present. *I grew as a writer* is empty. *I learned to cut my opening paragraphs because I write to find out what I'm writing about, and the finding-out doesn't belong in the finished piece* is full.

The voice of your reflection should be the voice of *you specifically* — the voice your text messages would have, the voice you would use describing this semester to a friend you trusted. Not the voice of *a student writing a reflection*. The reader can tell the difference.

This is where the LLM is specifically dangerous for this assignment. Whatever the model produces will be in the model's voice — fluent, organized, wise-sounding, and not quite yours. A reflection produced through heavy LLM drafting reads as performatively reflective rather than actually reflective. The genre's value is the writer's specific honesty. Do not trade it for the model's generic plausibility.

## The letter format — and why it helps

The assignment proposes the reflection as a letter to a reader — usually addressed to the instructor or to a future student in the same course.

The format is useful because it imposes conventions that serve the genre. A letter has a salutation. A letter has a stance — the writer is addressing a specific recipient and shaping the prose for that recipient. A letter has a tone that is conversational rather than academic. A letter can be honest in ways an essay sometimes struggles to be.

The opening *Dear...* is a permission slip for the writer to use their actual voice.

A working structure for a 1,500–2,500 word reflective letter, as loose bones rather than as strict prescription.

A salutation and brief framing — 100 words — establishing who you are writing to and what the letter will examine.

A specific opening anecdote — 300 words — a scene from the semester that anchors the rest. The first essay you turned in. A moment of failure you remember clearly. A moment of unexpected success. A scene from peer review where something shifted. Use it as a concrete entry point.

The body — 1,200 words — three to five specific things that changed. Each treated with specific evidence from the portfolio. Each articulated honestly. Each ending in what you now understand.

Where you still struggle — 200 words — not a confession; a specific naming. The patterns that have not shifted. The skills still to be developed. The honest naming of unfinished work.

The closing — 200 words — what you will carry. Specific moves, not general intentions.

## The end of the book

This is the last chapter of the book.

A few things to leave with, since the occasion is unusual.

The writing genres you have practiced this semester will keep evolving. They have been evolving for thousands of years. The next twenty years will bring specific shifts you cannot now predict. The skills you have practiced — close reading, evidence handling, voice calibration, awareness of rhetorical situation — are durable. They will apply to genres that do not yet exist.

The LLM question will not stabilize quickly. The norms around when and how to use these tools in writing will keep moving for years. Your job is not to learn a fixed set of rules; it is to keep thinking about the question as it changes. The discipline of asking *what is my actual relationship to this tool, and what do I want my reader to be able to assume about how this piece was made* will serve you longer than any specific current policy.

Your voice — the voice that emerged through this semester's work, the voice that is now somewhat different from the one you started with — is a real thing. Trust it more than you currently do. Adjust it for different rhetorical situations. Bring it into work you do for the rest of your life. The voice is the one thing the LLM cannot produce for you. Use it.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 23

**What you're building:** the structural and evidential scaffold for your reflective letter, before you draft it in your own voice.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word reflective letter for a college writing
class. The letter should articulate what I have learned about myself
as a writer over the course of one semester.

Below I'm pasting three short passages from my work — one from early
in the semester, one from the middle, one from late in the semester.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Read all three passages. Without flattery or generic praise,
   identify what is specifically different about the late passage
   compared to the early passage. Be specific at the level of
   sentence structure, word choice, opening move, evidence handling,
   and tone.

2. Ask me five questions about my experience this semester, one at
   a time. Cover at least: what assignment was hardest and why; what
   revision strategy did I discover; how did peer review or
   instructor feedback change my work; where do I still struggle;
   how do I now think about LLMs in my writing practice. Push back
   on vague answers.

3. Based on my answers, identify three specific changes in my writing
   practice this semester that the letter could be built around.
   Each should have specific evidence from my passages or my
   answers.

4. Propose two structural options for the letter — one chronological
   (early, middle, late), one thematic (three changes treated as
   separate sections). Name the trade-offs.

5. Suggest one specific opening anecdote that could anchor the
   letter, drawn from what I have told you. Do not write the
   anecdote; identify what to write about.

Do not draft the letter. Do not write paragraphs of reflective prose.
The writing is mine. The reflection is mine. The voice is mine.

Here are the three passages:
[paste passages with brief context for each]
```

**What this produces:** specific differences identified in your own work, specific changes named with evidence, two structural options, and one opening anecdote candidate. After this, you write the letter yourself, in your voice.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful for keeping your portfolio passages accessible across multiple drafting sessions.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model will sometimes produce generic reflective wisdom when asked to identify changes — *you have grown more confident in your voice* — without specific evidence underneath. Push back: *That's generic. Quote the specific evidence. What changed at the level of word choice or sentence structure?* The specificity is the genre's currency.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Janet Emig** was working on *The Composing Processes of Twelfth Graders* (1971) — the study that founded writing-process research by paying close attention to what young writers actually do, using talked-aloud protocols — decades before most people had heard of the portfolio reflection. Here's a prompt to find out more, and then make it better.

**Run this:**

```
Who was Janet Emig, and how does their work on the writing process and
what longitudinal study reveals about a writer's growth connect to the
portfolio reflection as a way of seeing your own growth as a writer?
Keep it to three paragraphs. End with the single most surprising thing
about their career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"Janet Emig"** on Wikipedia after you run this. See what the model got right, got wrong, or left out.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it to walk through Emig's case-study method — how she had high schoolers compose aloud — and what the talked-aloud transcripts revealed that essay drafts alone would not have shown.
- Ask: "Emig's study used N=8. What does the case-study form let you claim about the writing process that a larger-N quantitative study couldn't?"
- Add the framing: "Answer as if you're writing the conclusion of Emig's own study, but with one of your drafts from this course as the case-study subject."

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. Pull any two sentences from your first assignment of the semester. Now pull any two sentences on the same topic from your most recent assignment. Read all four. Write three sentences: one naming what is the same between the early and late sentences; one naming what is different; one naming which difference you did not notice until you looked carefully. *(Tests: the evidence-finding discipline — looking at your own prose the way you would look at a source.)*

2. Write the abstract-vocabulary version of one thing you learned this semester — using the words *growth*, *journey*, *transformation*, or any similar vocabulary. Then write the specific version of the same claim, without those words. The specific version must name a particular assignment, a particular revision, or a particular moment. *(Tests: the distinction between generic reflective vocabulary and specific content; practicing the substitution before the full draft.)*

3. Complete this sentence as specifically as you can, without using any form of the word *learn* or *grow*: *Before this semester, I thought writing was ___. Now I know it is more like ___*. The two blanks must be genuinely different, and the difference must be one you could defend with evidence from your portfolio. *(Tests: articulating a genuine shift in understanding — the core move of the reflection — before the full draft is written.)*

**Application**

4. Write the opening anecdote for your reflective letter — 250–350 words. Choose a single specific moment from the semester: the first essay you submitted, a peer review session where something shifted, a revision that surprised you, a moment of failure you remember clearly. The anecdote must be specific enough that the reader can see the scene. It must connect, in the final sentence, to what the letter will examine. *(Tests: the concrete opening — the genre's entry move — before the full letter is drafted; the specificity discipline applied to autobiographical material.)*

5. Choose one assignment from the semester that you found difficult and one you found relatively easy. Write two paragraphs — one for each — using the three-move structure: describe what happened, reflect on what it revealed, project what you will carry. Each paragraph must name at least one specific sentence-level or structural choice in that piece. *(Tests: the three-move structure applied twice in small scale, so the writer has practiced it before attempting the full letter.)*

6. Write the "where I still struggle" section of your reflective letter — 150–250 words. This section names the patterns in your writing that have not yet shifted: the habits that remain from your first draft, the skills still underdeveloped, the tendencies that revision has not yet corrected. The honesty of this section must be specific enough that a reader could identify the pattern in your actual portfolio. *(Tests: the honest-unresolved move — the reflection's equivalent of the "still unresolved" sections throughout the book — applied to your own practice.)*

7. Write the closing of your reflective letter — 150–200 words. Name three specific things you will carry forward. Each must be a specific writing move, not a general intention. *I will close-read my sources before citing them* is specific. *I will be a better researcher* is not. Each specific move should trace back to something that happened in your portfolio this semester. *(Tests: forward projection as a specific, evidence-grounded claim rather than a wish; closing the letter on the register of actionable self-knowledge.)*

**Synthesis**

8. Write the complete body of your reflective letter — the 1,200-word middle section that names three to five specific things that changed. Each change must have: a specific assignment or moment as its anchor; a quoted passage (from your own work) as evidence; an articulation of what the change means for your future practice. When finished, read the body against the three-move structure: does description, reflection, and forward projection each appear in every section? If not, revise until they do. *(Tests: full integration of the genre's moves in original prose at the letter's center; the self-diagnostic applied to the complete draft.)*

9. Assemble the complete reflective letter: salutation and framing, opening anecdote, body, where-you-still-struggle section, closing. Then read the complete letter and answer honestly: is this the voice you would use to describe this semester to a friend you trusted? If not, identify specifically where the voice shifted away — where you started performing reflection rather than doing it — and revise those passages. *(Tests: the complete genre assembled; the voice-authenticity check as a final discipline.)*

**Challenge**

10. Cisneros wrote her 2008 introduction about the young woman who wrote the book in 1983 — in the third person, with twenty-five years of distance. Write 300–400 words about the writer who submitted your first assignment of this semester, in the third person, with the distance of a full semester. What did that writer not yet know? What assumptions were they carrying that have since been complicated? What would you, from here, want to tell them? This is the reflective move at its most demanding — honest observation of your own past self. *(Open-ended; the Cisneros move applied directly to the student's own experience; the quality of the response depends on the honesty and specificity of the third-person distance.)*

If a careful longitudinal study followed students who completed reflective writing assignments versus students who did not, and the reflective writers showed no better long-term writing development, I would revise this chapter's central claim that articulated reflection improves practice. The evidence from writing pedagogy is that articulation aids transfer — students who can say what they have learned tend to apply it more reliably. But the claim is empirical, and a careful test could surprise me.

## Still unresolved

The relationship between *honest reflection* and *performance of reflection* is harder than this chapter pretends. Some students produce reflective letters that are technically excellent — specific, well-evidenced, well-structured — and still slightly performative, because the genre rewards self-articulation and the student has learned to perform self-articulation convincingly. The most genuinely reflective writing I have read is sometimes scrappier than the best technical examples. I do not have a clean way to teach students to be reflective rather than to write a reflective letter. The two are related. They are not identical.
