# Chapter 23 — Portfolio Reflection

*Looking Back at What You Now Know.*

A specific scene from forty years ago.

In 1983, a thirty-year-old writer named Sandra Cisneros published a novel-in-vignettes called *The House on Mango Street*. The book sold modestly for years. It became, slowly, one of the most assigned books in American secondary and college education. In 2009, twenty-five years after the original publication, the publisher released an anniversary edition with a new introduction. Cisneros — now in her mid-fifties — wrote that introduction in 2008.

She did not summarize the book. She did not explain it. She wrote, in the third person, about *the young woman who wrote it*. About what that young woman had not yet understood. About the specific apartment in Chicago where the book was written. About the chair she had bought herself with her first publishing advance. About the cat. About the parents — about her father, especially, whose immigrant aspirations had shaped her childhood and the book.[^1]

[^1]: Sandra Cisneros, "A House of My Own" in *A House of My Own: Stories from My Life* (Knopf, 2015). The introduction was originally written for the 25th anniversary edition of *The House on Mango Street*.

What Cisneros did in that introduction is reflection. Not summary. Not analysis. Not argument. The deliberate act of looking back at the work, the writer, the moment, the shape of how one became the other, and articulating something that the moment itself could not have articulated.

That is the move this final chapter is teaching.

You have spent a semester writing in a range of genres. You have produced essays, narratives, analyses, arguments, proposals, reviews, scripts. You have used LLMs as research partners, as interrogators, as drafting assistants. You have been graded. You have revised. You have failed and succeeded in specific ways.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to look back at all of it and articulate, in a piece of reflective writing of your own, what you now know about yourself as a writer that you did not know on the first day of the semester. The piece is between 1,500 and 2,500 words. The form is a letter to a reader — usually your instructor, but written in a register that is honest rather than performative.

## What reflective writing is, and what it isn't

*Reflective writing* is the genre that emerges from looking back at experience and articulating what the experience taught — not what was supposed to be learned, but what specifically was. The genre is older than its current pedagogical role; Montaigne in the 1580s was writing reflectively, Augustine in 397 was writing reflectively, Cisneros in 2008 was writing reflectively. The form has many versions. The move is consistent: turn experience into articulated understanding.

What reflective writing is not:

- *A summary of the semester.* The summary lists the work. The reflection asks what the work changed.
- *A self-evaluation against the rubric.* The rubric assesses against external standards. The reflection asks what *you* now understand about your own writing.
- *Generic praise of your own effort.* *I worked hard. I learned a lot. I grew as a writer.* These are filler sentences in a reflection. The reflection asks for specifics.
- *Excessive self-criticism.* The mirror image of the above. Both dodge the specific work the genre requires.
- *A piece in someone else's voice about your own work.* A reflection produced through heavy LLM-smoothing reads as if someone other than you is reporting on your semester. The reader can tell.

The genre's currency is honesty plus specificity. The student who can name specifically what changed, with specific evidence from specific assignments, is doing what the genre rewards.

## What the reflection is doing for *you*

Some pedagogy honesty.

The reflective assignment is not just for the instructor. It is for *you* — to make explicit, in writing, things about your own writing practice that you would otherwise carry only as unarticulated habit.

The thing about a habit you cannot articulate is that you cannot change it. You may continue to overuse passive voice, or to write opening paragraphs that delay your real argument, or to undercite, or to lean on adjectives for force, or to produce competent prose that is missing your specific voice — without ever noticing. The act of writing a reflection forces the noticing. The noticing makes the change possible.

This is the real purpose of the genre. The grade is a useful structural device for getting you to do the work. The work itself is the value.

## The three moves of reflection

A useful frame.

**Description.** Name what happened. Specific assignments, specific moments of difficulty, specific revisions, specific surprises. The description is the foundation; without it, the rest cannot work.

**Reflection.** Articulate what the moments meant. What they revealed about you. What they revealed about writing. Where they pushed against your prior assumptions.

**Forward projection.** What you will carry forward. Not generic plans (*I will write more*); specific moves (*I now know that my first drafts almost always need their opening paragraphs cut, because I write to find what I am writing about and then have to discard the finding-out*). The forward projection is what makes the reflection useful beyond this assignment.

Most strong reflective writing moves recursively through these — not in a strict sequence, but with each move present.

## What changed for you — and where to find it

The honest reflection asks: *what did you not know that you now know*? Some specific places to look.

**Your prose habits at sentence level.** Compare a paragraph from your first assignment to a paragraph from your most recent one. What is different? Sentence length? Verb choice? Use of subordinate clauses? Word choice? The differences are evidence of growth.

**Your approach to argument.** Did your sense of what an *argument* is shift? Did you come in believing argument meant heat and find that it can mean measured exposition? Did you come in believing argument was something other people did and find you have your own positions on contested questions?

**Your relationship to research.** Did the research process change for you? Did you discover specific sources or kinds of sources you did not know existed? Did your sense of what *credibility* means shift?

**Your relationship to feedback.** Did getting peer reviews change how you draft? Did instructor comments shift how you see your own writing? Did you develop the ability to receive critical feedback without taking it personally?

**Your relationship to revision.** Did you learn to enjoy revising rather than fear it? Did you discover specific kinds of revision that you reach for habitually now — opening-cuts, sentence-combining, paragraph-rearranging, evidence-strengthening?

**Your relationship to LLMs.** This semester is one of the first college writing semesters in which the LLM question was central. Did your sense of when to use the tools and when not to shift? Where do you stand now? What would you teach a younger sibling about this question?

**Your voice.** Did your voice on the page change? In what direction? Did you become more or less recognizably *yourself* in your prose? Where do you read most like yourself, and where do you read most like someone else?

Each of these is a place to look. The reflection that engages with two or three of them specifically is doing better work than the reflection that gestures at all of them.

## Evidence — what to quote from your portfolio

The reflection needs evidence. Not page-counts of your portfolio; specific quoted passages.

Pick two or three short passages from your own work across the semester — one from early, one from middle, one from late. Read them as if they were written by someone else. What do you notice about them?

A worked example, condensed.

A student's first-assignment opening:

> Throughout my life, I have always loved to read. Reading is very important to me, and books have been an important part of my growth. In this paper, I will be discussing my favorite books.

A student's late-semester opening, same author:

> The book my grandmother handed me when I was eight had a cover held together with tape, and the back third smelled like the inside of her coat closet — wool, cedar, the small jar of Vicks she kept in there for headaches. I was eight. I asked if I could borrow it. She said no. She said I could have it.

The two openings are by the same person. The reflection on these passages writes itself, but only because the writer is willing to look at them honestly.

Some specifics about what changed.

The first opening defaults to summary (*throughout my life*); the second locates a specific moment (*the book my grandmother handed me when I was eight*). The first uses abstract vocabulary (*important*, *growth*); the second uses sensory specifics (*wool, cedar, Vicks*). The first announces its subject (*in this paper, I will be discussing*); the second begins inside the scene. The first opens with the writer's general claim; the second opens with the world.

These are real shifts in writing technique. The reflection can name them, point to the evidence, and articulate what made them possible.

## Voice — your specific voice, on this particular page

A practical caution.

Reflective writing is the genre in which generic prose is most easily mistaken for competent prose. The vocabulary of self-improvement — *growth*, *journey*, *transformation*, *grown as a writer* — has been written into so much reflective work that the words have lost their meaning. A reflection that uses these words without specific content underneath them reads as filler. A reflection that uses specific content without these words reads as fresh.

A simple discipline: avoid the abstract reflective vocabulary unless the specific content is present. *I grew as a writer* is empty. *I learned to cut my opening paragraphs because I write to find out what I'm writing about, and the finding-out doesn't belong in the finished piece* is full.

The voice of your reflection should be the voice of *you specifically* — the voice your text messages would have, the voice you would use describing this semester to a friend you trusted. Not the voice of a *student writing a reflection*. The two are different. The reader can tell.

## A note about LLM use, specifically for this assignment

The reflection is the genre most vulnerable to a specific failure mode: the LLM-polished reflection that sounds wise without being yours.

I want to be direct about this. If you use an LLM to draft your reflection, the result will read as performatively reflective rather than actually reflective. The genre's currency is the writer's specific honesty. The LLM's currency is generic plausibility. The two are not compatible.

The places the LLM can help with the reflection:

*Interview you toward the reflection.* The LLM can ask you specific questions about your semester, your work, what changed, where you struggled. The act of answering the questions out loud (typing) produces the raw material for the reflection. The LLM is the interviewer; you are the writer.

*Identify specific contrasts in your portfolio.* Paste two paragraphs from different points in the semester and ask the model: *What specifically is different about these two passages at the level of sentence structure, word choice, and rhetorical move?* The model is reasonably good at this kind of close-comparison work.

*Catch generic prose.* Once you have a draft, paste it and ask: *Where in this reflection am I being generic instead of specific? Where am I using abstract reflective vocabulary without underlying content?* The model can flag the moments that need sharpening.

The places the LLM will damage the reflection:

*Drafting the prose.* Whatever the model produces will be in the model's voice. The reader can tell. The reflection is supposed to be in your voice. Do not.

*"Improving" your specific observations into generalizations.* The model will tend to soften specific claims into reflective-essay clichés. Read every revision specifically for whether the specificity survived.

*Generating evidence from your portfolio that does not exist.* The model has not read your portfolio. Quotes the model produces are fabricated. Only quote from actual passages you can verify.

## A specific structural option — the letter format

The OpenStax source assignment proposes the reflection as a *letter to a reader* — usually addressed to the instructor or to a future student. The format is useful because it imposes specific conventions.

A letter has a salutation. *Dear Reader.* *Dear Professor.* *Dear next semester's first-year writing student.*

A letter has a stance. The writer is addressing a specific recipient and shaping the prose for that recipient.

A letter has a tone that is conversational rather than academic. The vocabulary, the sentence structures, the pacing are all calibrated to address rather than to perform.

A letter can be honest in ways an essay sometimes cannot.

The letter format is not required; some reflections work better in essay form. But the format is a useful default. The opening *Dear...* is a permission slip for the writer to use their actual voice.

A worked structure for a 1,500–2,500 word reflective letter:

*Salutation and brief framing.* (100 words) *Dear reader. This letter is about a semester of writing.* Establishes who you are writing to and what the letter is about.

*A specific opening anecdote.* (300 words) A specific moment from the semester. The first essay you turned in. A moment of failure. A moment of unexpected success. A scene from peer review. Use it as an anchor for what the rest of the letter will examine.

*The body — three to five specific things that changed.* (1,200 words) Each is treated with specific evidence (a quoted passage, a specific assignment, a specific moment of revision). Each is articulated honestly. Each ends with what you now understand.

*Where you still struggle.* (200 words) Not a confession; a specific naming. The patterns in your writing that have not yet shifted. The skills you still want to develop. The honest naming of unfinished work.

*Closing — what you will carry forward.* (200 words) Specific moves you will take into your next semester, your next writing job, your next public piece.

The proportions are loose. The bones are what matter.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 23

**What you're building:** the structural and evidential scaffold for your reflective letter, before you draft it in your own voice.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word reflective letter for a college writing
class. The letter should articulate what I have learned about myself
as a writer over the course of one semester.

Below I'm pasting three short passages from my work — one from early
in the semester, one from the middle, one from late in the semester.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Read all three passages. Without flattery or generic praise,
   identify what is specifically different about the late passage
   compared to the early passage. Be specific at the level of
   sentence structure, word choice, opening move, evidence handling,
   and tone.

2. Ask me five questions about my experience this semester, one at
   a time. Cover at least: what assignment was hardest and why; what
   revision strategy did I discover; how did peer review or
   instructor feedback change my work; where do I still struggle;
   how do I now think about LLMs in my writing practice. Push back
   on vague answers.

3. Based on my answers, identify three specific changes in my writing
   practice this semester that the letter could be built around.
   Each should have specific evidence from my passages or my
   answers.

4. Propose two structural options for the letter — one chronological
   (early, middle, late), one thematic (three changes treated as
   separate sections). Name the trade-offs.

5. Suggest one specific opening anecdote that could anchor the
   letter, drawn from what I have told you. Do not write the
   anecdote; identify what to write about.

Do not draft the letter. Do not write paragraphs of reflective prose.
The writing is mine. The reflection is mine. The voice is mine.

Here are the three passages:
[paste passages with brief context for each]
```

**What this produces:** specific differences identified in your own work, specific changes named with evidence, two structural options, and one opening anecdote candidate. After this, you write the letter yourself, in your voice.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful for keeping your portfolio passages accessible across multiple drafting sessions.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model will sometimes produce *generic reflective wisdom* when asked to identify changes — *you have grown more confident in your voice* — that does not have specific evidence underneath. Push back: *That's generic. Quote the specific evidence. What changed at the level of word choice or sentence structure?* The specificity is the genre's currency.

## The end of the book — and what's next

This is the last chapter of *The Writing Guide with LLMs*. The book has been an unusual one. It has asked you to learn the classical craft of writing — rhetoric, evidence, argument, voice, revision — while also navigating a tool that did not exist in stable form when you started college. The intersection of those two trajectories is where most of the book has lived.

A few things to leave with.

The writing genres you have learned will keep evolving. They have been evolving for thousands of years. The next twenty years will see specific shifts you cannot now predict. The skills you have practiced — close reading, evidence handling, voice calibration, awareness of rhetorical situation — are durable. They will apply to genres that do not yet exist.

The LLM question will not stabilize quickly. The norms around when and how to use these tools in writing will keep moving. Your job is not to learn a fixed set of rules; it is to keep thinking about the question as it changes. The discipline of asking *what is my actual relationship to this tool, and what do I want my reader to be able to assume* will serve you longer than any specific policy.

Your voice — the specific voice that emerged through this semester's work, the voice that is now somewhat different from the voice you started with — is a real thing. Trust it more than you currently do. Adjust it for different rhetorical situations. Bring it into work you do for the rest of your life. The voice is the one thing the LLM cannot produce for you. Use it.

## What would change my mind

If a careful longitudinal study followed students who completed reflective writing assignments versus students who did not, and the reflective writers showed no better long-term writing development, I would revise this chapter's central claim that articulated reflection improves practice. The evidence from writing pedagogy is that articulation aids transfer — students who can articulate what they have learned tend to apply it more reliably. But the claim is empirical, and a careful test could surprise me.

## Still puzzling

The relationship between *honest reflection* and *performance of reflection* is harder than the chapter pretends. Some students produce reflective letters that are technically excellent — specific, well-evidenced, well-structured — and still slightly performative, because the genre rewards self-articulation and the student has learned to perform self-articulation. The most genuinely reflective writing I have read is sometimes scrappier than the best technical examples. I do not have a clean way to teach students to be reflective rather than to *write a reflective letter*. The two are related; they are not identical.
