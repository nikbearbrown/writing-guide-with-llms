# Chapter 3 — Language, Identity, and Culture

*The Voice You Were Born Into and the Voice You're Asked to Borrow.*

---

In the early 1820s, somewhere in the hills of what is now eastern Tennessee or northern Georgia — the exact location is uncertain — a Cherokee silversmith named Sequoyah sat down and tried something no one in the history of his nation had done.

He invented an alphabet.[^1]

[^1]: ["Sequoyah and the Creation of the Cherokee Syllabary," National Geographic](https://www.nationalgeographic.org/article/sequoyah-and-creation-cherokee-syllabary/).

<!-- → [IMAGE: portrait or engraving of Sequoyah holding the syllabary chart, widely reproduced from the 1828 lithograph — caption should note the syllabary chart he holds is the actual script, not a prop] -->

Not a code. Not an adaptation. A working syllabary — eighty-six characters, each representing a syllable of spoken Cherokee — built from scratch by a man who could neither read nor write any other language. He had seen white settlers reading and writing English. He had figured out, from watching, that the marks on paper corresponded to sounds. He had not learned the marks. He had *inferred the principle*.

Within a few years, the Cherokee Nation adopted his script. Literacy rates shot up. A newspaper launched in 1828 — the *Cherokee Phoenix* — printing in both English and Cherokee. A nation that had been entirely oral became, within a single generation, a nation whose laws, sermons, letters, and journalism were written down in its own language.

<!-- → [IMAGE: front page of the Cherokee Phoenix, 1828 — bilingual layout visible, Cherokee syllabary column alongside English column; reader should see that both languages occupy equal real estate on the page] -->

Here is the thing that matters for this chapter: Sequoyah did not do this because he wanted to write. He did it because he understood — clearly, without anyone telling him — that being literate in someone else's language is not the same as being literate in your own. The settlers had used writing to make treaties they then violated. They had used it to make laws that did not apply to him. The technology was being used against his people, and the only real defense was to take the technology and write in *Cherokee*.

That decision — to refuse to translate yourself into someone else's grammar — is what this chapter is about.

## What "culture" stops meaning

Start with the word itself, because it causes problems.

*Culture* gets used in at least three ways that have almost nothing in common. There is *culture* in the high-arts sense — opera, museums, the person who reads novels in the evening and calls it cultivation. There is *culture* in the pop sense — fan culture, the latest thing on the internet. And then there is *culture* in the anthropological sense: the shared beliefs, practices, references, foods, fears, jokes, gestures, holidays, ways of mourning, ways of celebrating, that a group of people inhabit *together*. The last one is what this chapter means.

The crucial thing about culture in this sense is that it is not optional and not chosen. You are inside several of them simultaneously, before you can name any of them. The culture of your family. The culture of your region. The culture of your generation. The culture of any school or religion you grew up in or near. The culture of any community you joined online and stayed in. The culture of any sport or art that took you serious time.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: concentric or overlapping circles showing a single person at the center, surrounded by labeled rings — family culture, regional culture, generational culture, religious/school culture, online community, hobby/sport culture — student should see that these overlap and that the overlaps are where interesting friction lives] -->

A student once asked me: *which one counts as my culture?* All of them, simultaneously, and the more interesting question is how they interact.

W. E. B. Du Bois named one specific kind of interaction in 1903.[^2] He called it *double consciousness* — the experience, for Black Americans, of always seeing yourself through the eyes of the dominant white culture *and* through the eyes of your own community at the same time. Two sets of standards. Two ways of being judged. The same person held responsible for satisfying both.

[^2]: W. E. B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903). [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/408/408-h/408-h.htm).

Du Bois was describing something that, once named, becomes impossible to unsee in any society where two cultures of unequal power overlap. The Cherokee speaker learning English under federal pressure. The first-generation college student whose family did not have English books on the shelves. The queer teenager in a family church. The neurodivergent kid in a school designed for neurotypical behavior. Every one of these is a version of double consciousness, with different specifics. The structure is the same: *you must satisfy two audiences who do not share standards, and neither audience fully sees the work the other one requires*.

For writing, this matters immediately. Every time you sit down to write for a college class, you are writing into at least two rooms at once: the room of the assignment, with its conventions, and the room of the *you* who walked in carrying everything else.

## What Standard English actually is

Here is a specification I want to make carefully, because it is widely misunderstood.

*Standard English* is the dialect of English used in formal U.S. publishing, mainstream broadcast journalism, official government documents, and most of academia. It has rules — about syntax, grammar, and usage — that other dialects of English do not share. It is widely treated as *correct* English, with other dialects positioned as deviations from it.

That framing is wrong.

Standard English is not the original form. It is one particular dialect that got institutionally promoted over others because the people who spoke it had the social, economic, and political power to make their version the published one. The other dialects — Southern white, Appalachian, African American Vernacular English, Cajun, Hawaiian Pidgin, and dozens more — are not corrupted Standard English. They are parallel English dialects with their own rules, their own internal grammar, their own history.

This is not a political opinion. It is a descriptive linguistic fact, the same kind of fact as saying that French and Spanish are both derived from Latin rather than one being a degraded version of the other.

African American Vernacular English has been studied carefully enough that linguists can specify the rules with precision.[^3] It distinguishes *habitual* aspect from *current* aspect in ways Standard English has no clean mechanism for: *he be working* — habitually employed, a stable condition — is grammatically distinct from *he working* — doing it right now, at this moment. The distinction exists in AAVE and not in Standard English, which means AAVE can make a grammatical statement that Standard English requires a whole clause to approximate. That is not broken English. It is a richer apparatus for one particular distinction.

[^3]: Conference on College Composition and Communication, ["Students' Right to Their Own Language" (1974, revised 2014)](https://prod-ncte-cdn.azureedge.net/nctefiles/groups/cccc/newsrtol.pdf) and ["This Ain't Another Statement! This Is a DEMAND for Black Linguistic Justice!" (July 2020)](https://cccc.ncte.org/cccc/demand-for-black-linguistic-justice).

AAVE speakers — particularly Black students — have nonetheless been told for decades that their English is wrong. Told by teachers who had no professional training in linguistics and who had simply absorbed the position that their own dialect was the standard. The harm this causes is documented in research. The 1974 "Students' Right to Their Own Language" statement, and its 2020 update demanding Black linguistic justice, name it directly.

I want to be honest about the complication here, because pretending it doesn't exist would be doing you a disservice. Different rhetorical situations have different conventions. A cover letter to most U.S. employers will be expected to read in Standard English. So will a grant proposal, a legal brief, most academic journal submissions. The skill of moving between dialects deliberately — sometimes called *code-switching* — is a genuine literacy, and students who grew up speaking nonstandard English at home often have this skill in deeper supply than students who grew up speaking Standard English.

The point is not that Standard English is wrong. The point is that knowing only one dialect, in a country with many, is a limitation — not a credential.

## Writing from the inside

Charles Dickens visited the United States in 1842 and wrote *American Notes for General Circulation*.[^4] His description of Broadway in New York City is technically accomplished — the pavement polished to a sheen, the red brick walls like kilns in the afternoon sun, the omnibuses, the carriages, the vivid clothing of the crowds. The sentences move. The observation is precise.

[^4]: Charles Dickens, *American Notes for General Circulation* (1842). [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/675/675-h/675-h.htm).

It is also entirely from the outside. Every American on the street is a *type* — a Black porter, a Bowery dandy, a young woman in vivid silk. None of them have interiority. None of them are inside their own cultures the way Dickens is inside his. What he produced is a Broadway as it would look to a sufficiently observant European tourist: well-rendered, entirely external, and therefore missing everything that mattered to the people who actually lived there.

That is the mode this chapter asks you to avoid.

The opposite move is what Cathy Park Hong does in *Minor Feelings: An Asian American Reckoning*.[^5] She will name something specific inside Korean American experience — an object, a phrase, a quality of attention a parent gives a child — and explain it with one sentence of context that does not flatten it but lets it land for a reader who doesn't share the cultural origin. Not a tour guide. Not a translator. Someone writing from the inside, with enough care for the outside reader to open a door without taking the door off its hinges.

[^5]: Cathy Park Hong, *Minor Feelings: An Asian American Reckoning* (One World, 2020).

Du Bois's *veil* — his metaphor for the color line, an invisible but real barrier between Black and white American experience — works this way too. It is not a literal object. It is a way of seeing that people inside the culture recognize immediately. An outsider cannot produce the meaning by guessing. But Du Bois writes it in a way that an outsider can approach, can feel the edges of, can understand as something real even if they cannot inhabit it from the inside.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: two-axis diagram — horizontal axis: insider knowledge required (low to high); vertical axis: outsider accessibility (low to high) — plot four quadrants with labeled examples: Dickens on Broadway (low insider, high accessibility = "tourist writing"), unexplained in-group slang (high insider, low accessibility = "closed door"), Du Bois's veil (high insider, high accessibility = "the goal"), generic cultural overview (low insider, low accessibility = "encyclopedia entry"); student should see that the assignment targets the upper-right quadrant] -->

The key test, when you are not sure whether you are writing from the inside or from the outside, is this: would someone else from your culture read this and recognize themselves? Or would they recognize *a tour guide's version* of themselves? The first is the goal. The second is what most first drafts deliver.

## What "cultural artifact" means in this course

The assignment this chapter is working toward asks you to write 350 to 700 words about a *cultural artifact* — an object, expression, symbol, or practice that does specific work inside a culture you belong to.

What it is *not*: a cliché of your culture. The dish that comes up first when somebody Googles your background. The holiday everyone has heard of. The pop-cultural figure your culture has agreed to be proud of in mixed company. Those have their place, but they are not what this assignment is designed to surface.

What it *is*: something specific, sometimes small, that you understand from inside the culture in a way an outsider could not pick up by searching. A particular phrase your grandmother used to signal that someone was being foolish without being cruel about it. A kind of song that gets played at one specific family occasion and never at another. A piece of jewelry that signals one precise affiliation. The way someone you know does or does not get up when an elder enters a room. A specific object that, in your family or community, carries meaning that would be invisible to anyone looking from the outside.

The essay needs three things.

First, *the thing itself*, rendered with enough sensory specificity that a reader can see or hear or smell it. This is the same move as the scene work in Chapter 4 — the *Wrinkle in Time* book held together with tape, the cedar-and-Vicks smell. Pick something you actually know. Render it precisely.

Second, *what it means inside the culture*. The interior meaning. The thing the outsider cannot infer.

Third, *enough context that the outsider can enter*. Not so much context that the artifact gets translated into a sociology textbook. Just enough that the meaning can land. The trade-off between these two is the most interesting craft decision in the essay. Too much context for the outsider and you strip the artifact of its specificity. Too little and the outsider cannot enter at all.

Where exactly to land on that line is not a rule I can give you in advance. It is a judgment you develop by drafting, reading the draft back, and asking: *does the outsider have what they need, and did I preserve the thing in the process of giving it to them*?

## The language model in the room

There is a new problem this assignment runs into that did not exist ten years ago.

Large language models are trained predominantly on Standard English text scraped from the open internet. When you ask one to help you write, its defaults pull toward Standard English. If you draft in AAVE, or in a Spanish-English code-switch, or in a Cantonese-inflected English the model has seen relatively little of, and you ask it to *polish* the draft — the model will tend to translate your prose toward the Standard English center.

This is not malice. It is a consequence of training-distribution statistics. The model has seen more Standard English than any other dialect, by orders of magnitude. When you say *polish*, it moves toward what it has seen most. That is what its objective function rewards.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: bar chart or proportional area chart showing rough estimated distribution of English dialect representation in large LLM training corpora — Standard American/British English dominant, with AAVE, Appalachian, Hawaiian Pidgin, Chicano English, etc. shown as much smaller slices; caption: "When the model says 'polish,' it moves toward the largest bar"] -->

The implication for this assignment is direct. If your cultural-artifact essay is written in your authentic voice — including dialect, code-switching, or linguistic moves your culture uses and Standard English does not — and you run the draft through an LLM for editing without thinking about this, the LLM will systematically erase the very thing the assignment asks you to put on the page. Not all at once. Gradually. A centimeter at a time, in places small enough that you might miss the drift.

There is no perfect fix for this in 2026. The honest workflow is: *use the model lightly on these drafts, and read every suggestion it makes as if it might be doing the thing you told it not to do*.

Two practices help.

*First, tell the model explicitly what you are doing.* If you are writing a passage in AAVE on purpose, say so before sharing the draft. "I am writing this in African American Vernacular English. Do not standardize the grammar. Suggest edits only at the level of clarity and flow, and leave the dialect intact." Models will follow that instruction, mostly, in the current generation of tools.

*Second, when the model proposes a revision, ask why.* If it says a sentence should be changed, ask: *is this making the sentence clearer, or is it making it more Standard English?* Those are different. The model will tell you, if you ask. Then you decide whether the revision serves your essay or serves the model's training distribution.

These are not workarounds. They are part of the literacy this course is teaching — the literacy of working with a tool that has biases you have to know about in order to use the tool well.

## What would change my mind

If research over the next decade found that students who were given full permission to write in their own dialects in college composition classes did *worse* in subsequent professional and academic settings than students trained exclusively in Standard English from the start, I would revise the central argument of this chapter. The current evidence — the CCCC position statement and the linguistic-justice scholarship that has built on it — points the other way: students do better, not worse, when their authentic linguistic identities are treated as assets to develop alongside Standard English rather than as deficits to replace it with. But that is a falsifiable claim, and I want to be clear that it is falsifiable.

---

## Still puzzling

The mechanics of using a language model on a draft written in a low-resource dialect remain genuinely unsolved. Even when you instruct the model explicitly to preserve the dialect, it will sometimes suggest an edit that standardizes the prose in a place small enough to miss. I do not have a clean fix for this. The workflow I described above — use it lightly, read every suggestion critically — is the best I can offer right now.

The deeper question I keep coming back to: when a model trained on a particular dialect's dominant corpus becomes the primary writing tool for people whose home dialect is underrepresented in that corpus, what happens to the dialects over time? That is not a question this chapter can answer. It is one worth keeping.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. *Identify your rooms.* List every cultural community you are inside simultaneously — family, region, generation, religion or school, online community, hobby. For each one, name one word, phrase, gesture, or object that people inside that community would recognize and an outsider would not. You are not writing the essay yet. You are taking inventory. (Tests: ability to distinguish the anthropological sense of culture from the pop-arts sense; ability to locate multiple simultaneous cultural memberships.)

2. *Dialect audit.* Write three sentences the way your mouth would actually say them — not the way you think a college essay should sound. Then write the same three sentences in Standard English. Identify what changed. What was gained? What was lost? (Tests: awareness of the gap between authentic register and academic register; the starting material for the code-switching section.)

3. *Inside or outside?* Find a passage — from a published essay, a news article, a personal blog, anywhere — that describes a cultural practice. Diagnose it: is it writing from the inside or from the outside? What specific sentences tell you? (Tests: ability to apply the Dickens/Hong distinction to real text before applying it to your own.)

**Application**

4. *The three-things test.* Pick an artifact you are considering for the essay. Write one paragraph that delivers all three requirements: the thing rendered sensorially, its interior meaning, and just enough context for the outsider to enter. Then read it back and ask: did the context swallow the artifact? (Tests: ability to hold the insider/outsider balance in a single piece of prose.)

5. *Du Bois in your own terms.* In 150 words or fewer, describe a specific moment when you had to satisfy two cultural audiences simultaneously — and what the cost of satisfying both was. Do not use the phrase "double consciousness." Demonstrate the concept without naming it. (Tests: internalization of Du Bois's concept as a lived observation, not just a term to define.)

6. *Dialect preservation prompt.* Draft two paragraphs of your cultural artifact essay in your authentic voice. Then submit them to an LLM with the dialect-preservation instruction from the LLM Exercise section. Copy every suggestion the model makes. For each one, write one sentence identifying whether the suggestion improves clarity or standardizes dialect. (Tests: ability to work deliberately with an LLM tool without surrendering dialect to it.)

**Synthesis**

7. *The subtitle problem.* The subtitle of this chapter is *The Voice You Were Born Into and the Voice You're Asked to Borrow.* In one paragraph, explain why that subtitle fits — or argue that it doesn't, and propose a better one. Use specific evidence from the chapter. (Tests: whether the student has grasped the chapter's central tension, not just its individual sections.)

8. *Sequoyah and the LLM.* In 200–300 words, draw the connection between Sequoyah's decision to build a syllabary rather than learn to write in English, and the problem of using a Standard-English-dominant LLM to edit a draft written in a nonstandard dialect. What is structurally similar? What is different? (Tests: ability to connect the historical opening to the contemporary problem — the chapter's spine.)

**Challenge**

9. *Write the essay.* 350–700 words. One cultural artifact. From the inside. In your voice. Use the LLM Exercise as scaffolding if you want the sensory details and interior meanings worked out before you draft. The essay itself is yours. (The culminating assignment this chapter has been building toward.)
