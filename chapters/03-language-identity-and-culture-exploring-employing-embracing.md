# Chapter 3 — Language, Identity, and Culture

*The Voice You Were Born Into and the Voice You're Asked to Borrow.*

In the early 1820s, somewhere in the hills of what is now eastern Tennessee or northern Georgia — the exact location is uncertain — a Cherokee silversmith named Sequoyah sat down with a pen and tried something nobody in the history of the Cherokee Nation had done.

He invented an alphabet.[^1]

[^1]: ["Sequoyah and the Creation of the Cherokee Syllabary," National Geographic](https://www.nationalgeographic.org/article/sequoyah-and-creation-cherokee-syllabary/).

Not a code. Not an adaptation of an existing one. A working syllabary — eighty-six characters, each representing a syllable of spoken Cherokee — built from scratch by a man who could neither read nor write any other language. He had seen white settlers reading and writing English. He had figured out, from watching, that the marks on paper represented sounds. He had not learned the marks. He had inferred the principle.

What he produced, somewhere around 1821, was a script the Cherokee Nation adopted within a few years. Literacy rates among Cherokee speakers shot up. A newspaper was launched in 1828 — the *Cherokee Phoenix* — printing in both English and Cherokee. A nation that had been entirely oral became, within a single generation, a nation whose laws, sermons, letters, and journalism were written down in its own language.

The interesting thing for this chapter is not that Sequoyah did it. The interesting thing is *why*. Sequoyah saw, correctly, that being literate in someone else's language was not the same as being literate in your own. The settlers had a written language. They had used it to make treaties they then violated. They had used it to make laws that did not apply to him. The technology of writing was being used against his people, and the only defense was to take the technology and write in *Cherokee*.

That decision — to refuse to translate yourself into the colonizer's grammar — is the spine of this chapter.

## The assignment, briefly

Before we go further: by the end of this chapter you will write what is called a *Cultural Artifact* essay. Three hundred fifty to seven hundred words. You choose one object, expression, or symbol that represents something larger about a culture you belong to, and you write about it with enough specificity that a reader who shares none of your cultural background can understand both what the thing is and what it does inside the culture.

The point of the assignment is not to perform diversity. The point is to practice the move Sequoyah made — refusing to translate yourself away from yourself while still writing for a reader who is not you.

## What "culture" stops meaning

Start with the word. The word "culture" gets used in three or four different ways that have almost nothing to do with each other.

*Culture* in the high-arts sense — opera, museums, the cultivated person who reads novels in the evening. This sense is rare in writing courses and is not what the chapter is about.

*Culture* in the consumer sense — pop culture, fan culture, the latest thing. Closer, but still not what we mean here.

*Culture* in the anthropological sense — the shared beliefs, practices, references, foods, fears, jokes, gestures, holidays, ways of mourning, ways of celebrating, that a group of people inhabit together. *That* is what we mean. Culture in this sense is not optional and not chosen. You are inside several of them, simultaneously, before you can name any of them.

A practical move: stop saying *culture* as if it were one thing. You are inside the culture of your family. The culture of your region, neighborhood, or city. The culture of your generation. The culture of any school or religion you grew up in or near. The culture of any community you joined online and stayed in. The culture of any sport, art, or hobby that took you serious time. Each of these is distinct. Each has its own rules. The way you move through the world is determined by *how many of them you are inside at once* and *how they interact*.

W. E. B. Du Bois named one specific interaction in 1903.[^2] He called it *double consciousness* — the experience, for Black Americans, of always seeing oneself through the eyes of the dominant white culture *and* through the eyes of one's own community. Two sets of standards. Two ways of being judged. The same person held responsible for satisfying both. Du Bois was naming something that, once named, became impossible to unsee in any plural society. The concept generalizes — every person inside two cultures with unequal power lives with some version of it. The Cherokee speaker learning English under federal pressure. The first-generation college student raised in a household where books in English were not on the shelves. The queer kid in a family church. The neurodivergent teenager in a school designed around neurotypical norms. All are versions of double consciousness, with different specifics.

[^2]: W. E. B. Du Bois, *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903). Excerpt in [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/408/408-h/408-h.htm).

The relevance to writing: every time you sit down to write for a college class, you are writing into at least two cultural rooms at once. The room of the assignment — academic, generally Standard English, with its own conventions. And the room of the *you* who walked into the assignment carrying everything else. Du Bois's word for what that is like, in 1903, still works.

## What "Standard English" really is

Now a specification I want to make carefully.

*Standard English* is the dialect of English used in formal U.S. publishing, mainstream broadcast journalism, official government documents, and most of academia. It has rules about syntax and grammar that other dialects of English do not share. It is widely perceived — including by many of its native speakers — as *correct* English, with other dialects positioned as deviations from it.

The framing is wrong. *Standard* English is not the original English. It is one particular dialect that got institutionally promoted over the others because the people who spoke it had the social, economic, and political power to make their version the published one. The other dialects — Southern white, Appalachian, African American Vernacular English (AAVE), Cajun, Hawaiian Pidgin, and dozens of others — are not corrupted Standard English. They are parallel English dialects with their own rules, their own systematic grammar, their own history.

AAVE specifically has been studied carefully enough that linguists can specify the rules.[^3] It distinguishes habitual aspect from current aspect in ways Standard English cannot ("he be working" — habitually employed — is grammatically distinct from "he working" — currently doing it, right now). It has a precisely-rule-governed system for verb agreement, for negation, for stress, for intonation. None of this is broken anything. It is just *different*.

[^3]: Conference on College Composition and Communication, ["Students' Right to Their Own Language" (1974, revised 2014)](https://prod-ncte-cdn.azureedge.net/nctefiles/groups/cccc/newsrtol.pdf). See also CCCC's ["This Ain't Another Statement! This Is a DEMAND for Black Linguistic Justice!" (July 2020)](https://cccc.ncte.org/cccc/demand-for-black-linguistic-justice).

That linguistic fact has not been politically neutral. AAVE speakers — particularly Black students — have been told for decades that their English is wrong. They have been told this by teachers who themselves did not speak any other regional dialect of English, who had simply absorbed the position that *theirs* was right. The harm is documented. The 1974 "Students' Right to Their Own Language" statement from the Conference on College Composition and Communication and its 2020 update name the harm directly.

This matters for the cultural-artifact assignment because the assignment is one of the rare college writing assignments where a student is given explicit permission to write in their own dialect. Not as a stunt. As the appropriate register for the genre.

There is a real complication I want to name honestly. Different audiences in different rhetorical situations have different conventions. A cover letter to most U.S. employers will be expected to read in Standard English. A grant proposal will. A legal brief will. The skill of *moving between dialects deliberately* — sometimes called *code-switching* — is itself a literacy, and it is one that students who grew up speaking nonstandard English often have in deeper supply than students who grew up speaking Standard English at home.

But the cultural-artifact assignment is not the cover letter. It is the place where the writer's actual voice is the asset.

## The cultural artifact — what we're really looking for

A *cultural artifact*, in this assignment's sense, is an object, expression, symbol, or practice that does specific work inside a culture you belong to.

What it is *not*: a cliché of your culture. The dish that comes up first when somebody Googles your background. The holiday everyone has heard of. The pop-cultural figure your culture has agreed to be proud of in mixed company.

What it *is*: something specific, sometimes small, that you understand from inside the culture in a way an outsider could not pick up by Googling. A particular phrase your grandmother used to indicate that someone was being foolish without being mean about it. A particular kind of song that gets played at one kind of family event and never at another. A piece of jewelry that signals one specific affiliation. The way someone you know does or doesn't get up when an elder enters the room. A specific object that, in your family or community, means something that other families wouldn't recognize as meaning anything.

Du Bois's *veil* — his metaphor for the color line, an invisible but real barrier between Black and white American experience — is a cultural artifact in the second sense. It is not a literal object. It is a way of seeing that the people inside the culture recognize, and that an outsider could not produce by guessing.

For your artifact, you will need three things on the page.

First, *the thing*, rendered with enough specificity that a reader can see, hear, smell, or otherwise sense it. This is the move you started practicing in Chapter 4 (scene work) — the *Wrinkle in Time* book held together with tape, the cedar-and-Vicks smell. Pick something you actually know. Render it.

Second, *what it means inside the culture*. The interior meaning. The thing the outsider can't infer.

Third, *enough context that the outsider can understand*. Not so much that the artifact gets translated into a sociology textbook. Just enough that the meaning lands. Cathy Park Hong, in her 2020 book *Minor Feelings: An Asian American Reckoning*,[^4] does this constantly — she will name a specific thing inside Korean American experience and explain it with one sentence of context that does not flatten the thing but lets it land.

[^4]: Cathy Park Hong, *Minor Feelings: An Asian American Reckoning* (One World, 2020).

The trade-off here is real. Too much context for the outsider, and you strip the artifact of its cultural specificity. Too little, and the outsider cannot enter at all. Where exactly to land is a craft decision. It is the most interesting decision in the essay.

## A scene from Dickens — the move you're avoiding

For contrast, look at one historical example of how *not* to write about another culture, and what it shows you about your own essay.

In 1842 Charles Dickens — then thirty years old, already famous for *The Pickwick Papers* — toured the United States and wrote *American Notes for General Circulation*.[^5] The book describes American cities and customs through the eyes of a British observer who finds most of them strange.

[^5]: Charles Dickens, *American Notes for General Circulation* (1842). Available via [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/675/675-h/675-h.htm).

His section on Broadway in New York City describes the pavement polished to a sheen, the red brick walls like kilns in the afternoon sun, the omnibuses, the carriages, the colorful clothing of the crowds. The writing is technically accomplished. The sentences move. The observation is specific.

It is also entirely from the outside. Every American on the street, for Dickens, is a *type* — a Black porter, a Bowery dandy, a young woman in vivid silk. None of them have interiority. None of them are inside their own cultures the way Dickens is inside his. The Broadway he describes is a Broadway *the way it would look to a sufficiently observant European tourist*.

This is not your essay's mode. Your essay's mode is the opposite — *from the inside*. The artifact has to mean something to you that it could not mean to a Dickens-style observer. The point is not to perform your culture for an outsider. The point is to write about something that lives inside you, and let the outsider come to it on its terms.

The honest move, when you are not sure whether you are writing from the inside or from the outside, is to ask: would someone else from my culture read this and recognize themselves? Or would they recognize *a tour guide's version* of themselves? The first is what you want. The second is what most first drafts do.

## The 2026 wrinkle — the language model in the room

A new problem.

Large language models are trained, predominantly, on Standard English text scraped from the open internet. When you ask one of them to help you write, the model's defaults are pulled toward Standard English. If you write a draft in AAVE, or in a Spanish-English code-switch, or in a Cantonese-inflected English the model has seen relatively little of, and you ask the model to *polish* the draft, the model will tend to translate your prose toward the Standard English center.

This is not malice. It is a function of training-distribution statistics. The model has seen more Standard English than any other dialect, by orders of magnitude, and "polish" toward what it has seen most of is what its objective function rewards.

The implication for this assignment is direct. If your cultural artifact essay is written in your authentic voice — including dialect, code-switching, or linguistic moves your culture uses and Standard English does not — and you run the draft through an LLM for editing without thinking about this, the LLM will systematically erase the very thing the assignment is asking you to put on the page.

The solution is not to avoid the LLM. The solution is to use it deliberately. Two specific practices.

*First, tell the model what you are doing.* If you are writing a passage in AAVE on purpose, say so. "I'm writing this in African American Vernacular English. Do not standardize the grammar. Suggest edits only at the level of clarity and flow, and leave the dialect intact." Models will follow that instruction in 2026, mostly.

*Second, when the model proposes a "fix," ask why.* If the model says a sentence should be revised, ask: *is the revision making the sentence clearer or is it making it more Standard English*? Those are different things. Sometimes the first; sometimes the second. The model will tell you, if you ask.

These are not workarounds. They are part of the literacy this textbook is teaching — the literacy of working with a tool that has biases you have to know about to use the tool well.

## What this chapter is actually asking of you

A cultural artifact essay, 350–700 words, on something specific you understand from inside that an outsider could not infer.

A voice that is yours, not a borrowed academic one. If your idiolect — the personal way of speaking we named in Chapter 4 — is built from Standard English, fine. If it is built from anything else, also fine. Either way: write the sentences your mouth would actually say.

Enough context that an outsider can enter, not so much that the artifact disappears.

And, if you use a language model, the discipline of asking it what it is doing to your voice and saying no when the answer is *flattening it*.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 3

**What you're building:** a draft of your cultural artifact essay, with Claude as a research and context partner — but with the writing voice yours alone.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended — you may want to come back to this material across multiple work sessions.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 350–700 word essay for a college writing class about a
cultural artifact — an object, expression, or symbol that represents
something about a culture I belong to.

The artifact I'm writing about is: [name the artifact in one phrase]

The culture this artifact lives inside is: [name the culture in one
phrase]

The dialect or register I'm writing in is: [name the dialect — could
be Standard English, AAVE, Spanish-English code-switch, a specific
regional variety, anything]. Do not "correct" my dialect when I share
drafts with you. If you suggest edits, suggest them at the level of
clarity, flow, or structural argument — not grammar normalization.

I want you to help me, in this order:

1. First, ask me three questions about the artifact, one at a time,
   that push me to render it with more sensory specificity. (What
   does it look like, sound like, smell like, feel like? Where did
   I first encounter it? Who else inside the culture would also
   recognize it?)

2. Then, ask me three questions about what the artifact MEANS inside
   the culture — meanings an outsider would not be able to guess.

3. Then, ask me one question about what context an outsider would
   need to be given so that the artifact can land on the page without
   me having to translate it away from its specificity.

4. Finally, summarize what I've told you in a paragraph in your own
   voice — not the essay's voice, just a research note I can carry
   into the drafting. Do not write the essay. The essay is mine.
```

**What this produces:** a clean set of sensory details, interior meanings, and outsider-context notes, all of them yours. After this, you draft the essay yourself, in your voice, using the notes as scaffolding.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written. Watch closely for the model trying to suggest a draft anyway — push back if it does.
- *For Cowork:* useful if you want to keep your answers in a file you can come back to. Tell Cowork to write each answer to `artifact-notes.md` as you go.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool — this is a conversation.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when you tell Claude to leave your dialect intact, it will sometimes still slip a Standard English suggestion into a follow-up. When you notice: *"That suggestion is replacing my dialect with Standard English. Drop it. Suggest something at the level of clarity instead."* The pushback itself is part of the literacy.

**Preview of next chapter:** Chapter 4 (which you may have already done) takes a related but different move — writing the moment you became literate in something specific. Chapter 5 is a short bridge into the divide between personal voice and academic writing, where the question of when to translate yourself and when not to gets sharper.

## What would change my mind

If linguistic research over the next decade found that students who were given full permission to write in their own dialects in college composition classes did *worse* in subsequent professional and academic settings than students taught to write in Standard English from the start, I would revise the central permission of this chapter. The current evidence — see the CCCC "Students' Right to Their Own Language" position and the linguistic-justice work that has built on it — points the other way: students do better, not worse, when their authentic linguistic identities are treated as assets to develop alongside Standard English rather than deficits to replace it with. But this is a claim that should remain falsifiable.

## Still puzzling

The mechanics of using a language model on a draft written in a low-resource dialect are not yet good. Even when you instruct the model explicitly to preserve the dialect, you will sometimes catch it suggesting an edit that would standardize the prose by a centimeter at a time, in places small enough that you might miss the drift. I do not have a clean fix for this. The honest workflow right now is *use the model lightly on these drafts, and read every suggestion as if it might be doing the thing you told it not to*.
