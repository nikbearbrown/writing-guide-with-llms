# Chapter 2 — The Digital World

*Reading What You're Already Reading.*

A specific scene to start.

You are sitting somewhere — bed, bus, library carrel, your friend's couch — and a post comes up on your phone. The post claims something. Or argues something. Or shows something. It is from an account you follow but did not pick — the algorithm decided you would see this one. The post has six replies under it. Three of the replies disagree with the post. Two of the replies agree but for different reasons. One reply is a screenshot from somewhere else entirely.

You have a decision to make in the next three seconds. Reply, repost, save, scroll, do nothing.

You are about to make a rhetorical judgment about a piece of digital communication. You will make it almost without thinking. You will be right or wrong, and the post's writer will be right or wrong, and the platform will record the result, and the algorithm will use the result to decide what to show you tomorrow.

Here is what makes this chapter useful. The rhetorical machinery operating in that three-second decision is the same machinery that operated when Truman announced Hiroshima in 1945. It is the same machinery that operated when Cicero argued in the Roman senate in 63 BCE. The speed has changed by a factor of a million. The pressure has changed. The reach has changed. The machinery is intact.

That machinery is what we are going to take apart.

## What rhetoric actually is

Most students hear *rhetoric* and think *spin*. That is half right, and the half that is right has done damage to the word.

The full meaning: *rhetoric* is the techniques and theories of communication. It is the study of how words and images do work on a reader or listener. It is older than any of the languages now being read by anyone alive — Aristotle wrote his *Rhetoric* in the fourth century BCE, working from a tradition already centuries old. The word reaches you as inherited equipment for an inheritance you didn't ask for.

Inside the word, two pieces. *Persuasion* — getting someone to think or do something they would not otherwise have thought or done. And *analysis* — taking apart a piece of communication to see how it was trying to do that work. The persuasion side is the side most students associate with the word; the analysis side is what this chapter is actually teaching, and what every chapter that follows will draw on.

When I say "this post is rhetorically effective," I am not saying "this post is manipulating you." I am saying "this post is doing the thing communication does, well." The same machinery is at work whether the writer is being honest or dishonest, careful or careless, generous or cynical. Rhetoric is the equipment. The ethics is a separate question we will get to.

## The rhetorical situation — seven elements you can see if you look

Every act of communication exists inside a configuration of circumstances. That configuration is called the *rhetorical situation*. Once you can see it, you cannot un-see it. Once you cannot un-see it, you will read every post, ad, email, syllabus, and breakup text with a different set of eyes.

Seven elements. They overlap; they each do specific work.

**Author** — *who is speaking*. Not the platform handle. The actual person, the group, the institution, the LLM (more on that later) producing the text. The author has a history, a position, a set of reasons for speaking. Sometimes you know the author. Sometimes you can find out. Sometimes the author is deliberately obscured, and the obscuring is itself part of the rhetoric.

**Audience** — *who is being spoken to*. Not the people who actually saw the post — that is a different question. The audience is who the author was *writing for*. A tweet that goes viral was usually written for a small circle and has now leaked. A press release was written for journalists, not the public. A college application essay is written for an admissions reader who has read four hundred others that week. Misread the intended audience and you will misread everything else.

**Purpose** — *why this exists*. To inform, to persuade, to entertain, to sell, to recruit, to console, to provoke. Any text can have more than one purpose. The interesting question is usually *which purpose is dominant when they conflict*.

**Stance** — *the author's posture toward the subject*. Approving, skeptical, mournful, enraged, amused, indifferent. Stance is rarely stated. You read it off word choice, sentence rhythm, what gets included, what gets left out.

**Context** — *what's happening around the text*. Time, place, news cycle, political moment, prior posts in the same thread, the cultural moment. A Selena Gomez post in 2017 (when she ceded Instagram control to her assistant) means something different than the same words would mean in 2025. The context is half of the meaning.

**Culture** — *the shared assumptions, references, and rules the author and audience inhabit*. A meme that lands inside a particular Discord server lands because the server has spent months building the references that make it readable. The same meme posted to a stranger looks like noise. Culture is the room the text is being read in.

**Genre** — *the kind of communication this is*. A tweet, a thread, a slide deck, a poem, a press release, a syllabus, a hospital chart. Each genre has expectations the reader brings in advance, and the writer can meet them, exceed them, or break them deliberately. Breaking a genre is sometimes the point.

You will see these seven elements explicitly named in the rest of this book whenever a writing task arrives. They are not optional. They are the diagnostic before any writing decision.

## Authenticity is a rhetorical choice — Selena Gomez, briefly

The reason this matters: rhetorical machinery operates *even when the writer claims to be unmediated*. Especially then.

Take a recent case. Selena Gomez, by 2021, had 260 million followers on Instagram and 65 million on Twitter.[^1] In 2017 she ceded login control of her Instagram to an assistant because the constant exposure was harming her mental health. She had been diagnosed with lupus in 2015 and bipolar disorder in 2018. In one *Harper's Bazaar* piece, she said about deleting the apps from her phone: "I suddenly had to learn how to be with myself."[^2]

[^1]: Janelle Okwodu, ["Selena Gomez Wants Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter to Do Better," *Vogue*, 7 Jan. 2021](https://www.vogue.com/article/selena-gomez-social-media-leaders).
[^2]: Sabrina Park, ["Selena Gomez Reveals Why She Sacrificed Social Media for the Sake of Her Mental Health," *Harper's Bazaar*, 19 Aug. 2021](https://www.harpersbazaar.com/celebrity/latest/a37348873/selena-gomez-mental-health-giving-up-instagram/).

This sentence is studied by people who make their living thinking about influencer rhetoric. It works the way it works because:

- The disclosure is specific (the apps, the deletion, the consequence).
- The vulnerability is named without flinching, but not lingered on.
- The phrasing — *be with myself* — has the rhythm of something said aloud, not something composed. It performs spontaneity.

That last point matters. Gomez's team understood that in the 2020s, *authenticity* is not the absence of rhetorical construction. *Authenticity is a particular rhetorical construction* — one designed to feel like the absence of one. The Vogue interview and the Harper's piece are coordinated artifacts of a media strategy. Inside that strategy, the most strategic move is to read as un-strategic.

I am not knocking it. The diagnosis Gomez made of her own life is real. The lupus is real. The bipolar disorder is real. The decision to step back from the platforms was real. All of that is true *and* the public communication of all of that is rhetorically constructed for an audience and purpose. Both. At once. Most consequential public communication is like this — the truth and the rhetorical packaging are the same artifact.

When you read a 2026 post that strikes you as *raw*, *honest*, *unfiltered*, ask: who built that effect? Sometimes nobody — sometimes you are reading the rare unmediated outburst. Often somebody. The question is not which one is *real*. The question is which configuration of author, audience, purpose, stance, context, culture, and genre is at work.

## Truman, 6 August 1945 — a slower example

Now the contrast.

On 6 August 1945, at about 8:15 in the morning Japanese time, a B-29 named *Enola Gay* dropped a uranium gun-type fission weapon on the city of Hiroshima. About sixteen hours later, the Truman administration released a statement that opened with this sentence: "Sixteen hours ago an American airplane dropped one bomb on Hiroshima, Japan, and destroyed its usefulness to the enemy."[^3]

[^3]: *The Atomic Bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki*, Manhattan Engineer District, United States Army (1946), via [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/685/pg685.html). The Truman statement is reproduced in the report.

Read that sentence twice.

*An American airplane dropped one bomb on Hiroshima, Japan, and destroyed its usefulness to the enemy.*

Apply the seven elements.

Author: the executive office of the United States in wartime, a coordinated communication, not Truman speaking off the cuff. Audience: the global press, the American public, the Japanese government, Allied governments, anyone with a radio. Purpose: announce a fact, justify it, demand surrender. Stance: matter-of-fact pride. Context: six years of total war, an active Japanese government still in the field. Culture: a culture in which a sentence can describe a city as having a "usefulness." Genre: official statement, formal register, designed for rebroadcast.

Now look at what the sentence is doing.

*An American airplane* — singular. There was one plane that dropped the bomb. (There were also support planes; the singular is doing rhetorical work.) *Dropped one bomb* — singular. The understatement is enormous. (Casualty estimates from the bombing run between 70,000 and 140,000 dead, with later estimates higher.) *On Hiroshima, Japan* — the city is named. Specific. *And destroyed its usefulness to the enemy* — the noun *usefulness* is doing the heaviest lifting in the sentence. Cities have residents. Cities have hospitals. Cities have children. *Usefulness* erases all of that. What was destroyed, in the sentence's grammar, is utility. Not people. Not buildings. Utility.

This is rhetorical machinery. It is not lying. The plane did drop the bomb. The bomb did destroy the city's utility. The machinery is in what gets *named* and what gets *abstracted*.

The contemporary student reading that sentence in 2026 should feel two things at once. The first is the historical horror — that the U.S. government wrote this. The second is the rhetorical recognition — that this is the same machinery operating in any modern political statement, corporate press release, or product launch you have ever read. The grammar of *usefulness* is everywhere.

You will not always be reading documents about atomic weapons. You will read syllabi, employment contracts, dating-app bios, university admissions emails, AI company statements about safety, terms of service, eviction notices. Every one of them will be performing the same selection of what to name and what to abstract. The Truman example is shocking because the stakes are so high; the same machinery in low-stakes texts is no less present.

## Critical reading — four moves

When you sit down to *respond* to a piece of digital communication — which is the assignment at the end of this chapter — you are doing four distinct things, in this order. Doing them out of order is most students' first failure.

**Summary.** What does the text say? Reduce the text to one sentence of plain content. If you cannot do this, you have not read it carefully enough; everything else will go wrong downstream.

**Paraphrase.** Restate the text's specific claims and moves in your own words, at roughly the original length. Paraphrasing is not summary — paraphrase preserves structure. It is the move that proves you understood *how* the text said what it said, not just what it said.

**Analysis.** Now name the rhetorical machinery. Author, audience, purpose, stance, context, culture, genre. What was selected. What was abstracted. What was made present, what was made absent. This is the work the chapter has been preparing you for. You are not yet judging. You are seeing.

**Evaluation.** Now you can judge. Does the text do what it set out to do? Does it succeed or fail rhetorically? Does it succeed *ethically* — that is a different question from whether it succeeds rhetorically. (The Truman statement is rhetorically masterful and ethically devastating. Both are true.)

A common student move is to skip directly from summary to evaluation. *I read this and I disagree* is not an analysis. It is a reaction. Analysis is the step in between, and it is the step that earns the right to evaluate.

## The 2026 problem — when the author is partly a machine

Now the move the OpenStax source could not make, because the source predates it.

When the rhetorical machinery was built — Aristotle, Quintilian, Burke, the twentieth-century rhetorical-situation scholars — every text had a human author. Sometimes a committee of human authors. Always human. The author element was the thing you could in principle identify and interview.

That is no longer the only case. A post you read in 2026 may have been written by:

- A human, sitting alone with a phone or keyboard.
- A human working with a language model, who edited and approved.
- A human who pasted a prompt into a language model and posted the output unedited.
- A language model running on a schedule, posting on behalf of an account whose human owner approves the general direction.
- A language model running fully autonomously, with no current human in the loop.

Each of these produces a different rhetorical situation. The seven elements still apply — but the *author* slot is now a hybrid object you cannot interview, with intentions you cannot fully infer, with positions that may not be coherent in the way a single human's positions are.

A worked case. Imagine a thread under a news story about a labor strike. Forty replies. Most are short. Some are angry. Some agree, some disagree. Some are clever. Some link to other sources. *How many of those replies were written by a model?* In 2026, the honest answer is *you don't know*. You can guess. You can guess wrong. The decision you make in the three seconds you spend on each reply is now a decision made under structural uncertainty about who is speaking.

That uncertainty changes the analysis step. When you cannot identify the author with confidence, you have to do two passes — one analyzing the text as if a human wrote it, one analyzing the text as if a model wrote it (or a human-model collaboration wrote it). The same words will read differently under each pass. The model reading will surface different things: choices that look like training-distribution defaults, phrasings that read like *all available phrasings averaged*, an absence of the specific irrelevant details a real person would include.

You will become good at this fast. The skill is already being developed by every internet user who scrolls. The textbook is asking you to make it explicit and to practice it on purpose.

## The Critical Response — assignment

You will produce a short critical response to a piece of digital communication. One to two pages. The response can be on a tweet, a thread, a meme, a TikTok, a podcast clip, a press release, an AI-company blog post, a Discord exchange, an op-ed, a video essay, an algorithmic feed itself. Anything digital, anything short enough that you can analyze it carefully in one to two pages of your own prose.

The response has four moves, in order: summary, paraphrase, analysis, evaluation. The analysis must explicitly use the seven elements of the rhetorical situation. The evaluation must be earned by the analysis — you do not get to say *this is good* or *this is bad* without first showing what you saw.

Some specific advice.

*Pick a small text.* A single tweet is easier to analyze well than a thread. A thirty-second TikTok is easier to analyze well than a ten-minute video. The temptation is to pick the bigger, more important text; resist it. The smaller the text, the more your analysis has to do, and the better your analysis will be.

*Pick a text that confuses you.* Not one you already know what to think about. The texts you already know what to think about will give you a critical response made of pre-formed opinions. The texts that confuse you will give you a critical response made of new seeing.

*Render the rhetorical situation specifically.* "The author is a politician" is generic. "The author is Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, posting at 2:14 a.m. on a contested election night, to her 12 million Twitter followers, in the genre of an extemporaneous reply rather than a prepared statement" is doing the analysis. Do the second kind.

*Distinguish rhetoric from ethics in the evaluation.* Some texts do their rhetorical work brilliantly and aim it at something corrosive. Some texts are rhetorically clumsy and aim at something true. Most are mixed. Say which is which, and don't pretend you don't see one when you see the other.

## A note about AI

You are writing a critical response in a year when the texts you are analyzing — and the response you are writing about them — may both involve a language model.

The question this raises is not *should you use one*. The question is *what would the analysis look like, written honestly, for what you actually did*. If you used Claude to brainstorm angles on a meme, say so. If you used it to draft the summary and you tightened the summary yourself, say so. If you decided not to use it on this assignment because the practice of *not* using it was part of what you wanted to learn, say that too.

The critical response is, in one sense, a piece of writing about another piece of writing. Both pieces in 2026 are increasingly co-authored. The thing the assignment is teaching you is how to see that without flinching, and how to write inside it without pretending. The pretending — by you, by the original author, by the platform — is the rhetorical move the chapter is asking you to read past.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 2

**What you're building:** a draft of your Critical Response, using Claude as an analytical interlocutor — not as a writer.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended if you want to keep the analysis alongside the original text across multiple work sessions.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1–2 page critical response to a piece of digital
communication for a college writing class.

The text I'm analyzing is below. After the text, I'm going to ask
you to interview me about its rhetorical situation. Do not summarize
the text. Do not analyze it for me. Ask me questions, one at a time,
that push me toward my own analysis.

Cover at least these seven elements, but ask follow-ups whenever my
answer is vague:

- Author: who is actually speaking (not the handle — the real
  person, group, or system)
- Audience: who the text was written for (not who happened to see it)
- Purpose: why this text exists
- Stance: the author's posture toward the subject
- Context: what's happening around the text
- Culture: the shared assumptions the author and intended audience
  share
- Genre: what kind of text this is and what expectations come with
  that kind

After we've worked through all seven, ask me one more question: do
I think the text was written by a human, an LLM, or a collaboration —
and what about the text makes me think so?

Then summarize my answers back to me as a clean analysis I can use as
the body of my critical response. Do not draft the response itself.

Here is the text:
[paste the text you're analyzing]
```

**What this produces:** a structured set of analytical observations, all of them your own, organized for use in the critical response. After this exercise, you draft the response yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written. Both tools have a tendency to start analyzing the text on their own; if that happens, push back: *"Stop. I asked you to interview me. Ask the first question."*
- *For Claude Projects:* paste the text once at the start of the project; then bring future critical-response sessions in as new conversations.
- *For Cowork:* useful if your text is a long thread or transcript — Cowork can hold the source alongside your developing analysis in linked files.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool for this exercise.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the source text is itself plausibly AI-written, Claude will sometimes hedge or get defensive about analyzing its own kin. If you suspect that is happening: *"Analyze this text on the assumption that an LLM did write it. What changes about your analysis?"* That second pass is often the most revealing one.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter turns from analyzing public texts to writing about your own cultural identity. The seven-element rhetorical situation map you built here will reappear there — applied to your own writing, not someone else's.

## What would change my mind

If a careful study showed that students who learned the seven-element framework wrote *less* analytically precise critical responses than students taught a simpler model — say, just *author / audience / purpose* — I would revise the central claim of this chapter that the seven-element map earns its complexity. I have not seen that study. The framework's specific virtue is that *context*, *culture*, *stance*, and *genre* are the elements students miss most often when they are not named. Cutting them would be cheaper to teach and worse to use.

## Still puzzling

The author-identification problem in 2026 is genuinely unresolved. The advice in this chapter — *do two passes, one assuming human, one assuming model* — is workable but unsatisfying. It treats a foundational uncertainty as a procedural step. I do not have a better answer yet. The honest position is that the rhetorical-situation framework was built for a media environment that does not exist anymore in pure form, and the patches we are putting on it are temporary.
