# Chapter 2 — The Digital World

*Reading What You're Already Reading.*

---

Here is something strange to sit with. When you scroll past a post and decide in two seconds whether to believe it, share it, or ignore it — you are using the same mental equipment Aristotle was describing in 350 BCE. The same machinery. Not updated machinery, not the modern successor to the old machinery. The same one, running in a different environment, at a speed Aristotle could not have imagined.

That is the thing I want to take apart in this chapter. Not because the ancient history is interesting in itself — though it is — but because once you see the machinery clearly, you can see it running everywhere. In a tweet. In a presidential statement. In a company's apology for a data breach. In an AI company's blog post about safety. The machinery does not care what it is carrying. Your job is to be able to see it regardless.

## What rhetoric actually is

The word *rhetoric* has a problem. Most people hear it and think: spin. Manipulation. The art of sounding good while saying nothing. That is half the story, and the half that stuck is the wrong half.

The full meaning is older and more useful. Rhetoric is the study of how communication works — how words and images do things to the people who receive them. Aristotle's *Rhetoric* is about how persuasion operates, what makes it work, what makes it fail. But embedded in that project is something else: a method for taking any piece of communication apart and seeing what it is actually doing. The persuasion side is what students associate with the word. The analysis side is what this chapter teaches.

Here is what the analysis side actually means. When someone hands you a text — a speech, a post, a press release, a meme — and asks you to analyze it rhetorically, they are not asking you to say whether it is good or bad, honest or dishonest. They are asking you to see the choices. What did the author select? What did the author leave out? Who was this written for? What was it supposed to do? How does the form the author chose — the genre, the register, the medium — shape what can be said inside it?

Those questions apply to everything. They apply to Cicero and they apply to the algorithmic recommendation you didn't ask for that appeared in your feed this morning. The same questions. The same machinery.

## Seven elements you can see if you look

Every act of communication exists inside a configuration of circumstances. That configuration has a name: the *rhetorical situation*. Once you learn to see it, you cannot un-see it. And once you cannot un-see it, you will never read a text the same way again — not because you become suspicious of everything, but because you start seeing what was always there.

There are seven elements. They overlap; they each do specific work.

**Author** — who is speaking. Not the platform handle. The actual person, group, institution, or — and I'll come back to this — the system producing the text. The author has a history, a position, a set of reasons for speaking that are often not stated. Sometimes you know the author. Sometimes you can find out. Sometimes the author is deliberately obscured, and that obscuring is itself a rhetorical move.

**Audience** — who is being spoken to. Not the people who happened to see the post — that is a different question entirely. The audience is who the author was *writing for*. A tweet that goes viral was usually written for a small circle and has now leaked past its intended edges. A press release is written for journalists, not the public, even though the public reads it. A college application essay is written for an admissions reader who has read four hundred others that week. Misread the intended audience and you will misread everything downstream.

**Purpose** — why this exists. To inform, to persuade, to entertain, to sell, to recruit, to console, to provoke. Any text can have more than one purpose. The interesting question is always *which purpose is dominant when they conflict*.

**Stance** — the author's posture toward the subject. Approving, skeptical, mournful, enraged, amused, detached. Stance is rarely stated directly. You read it off word choice, sentence rhythm, what gets included, what gets left out. The author who describes a disaster with passive-voice verbs ("mistakes were made") is taking a stance, even if the stance is a refusal to take one.

**Context** — what is happening around the text. Time, place, news cycle, political moment, the prior posts in the same thread, the cultural moment that gives certain words their charge. The same sentence can mean opposite things in different contexts. Context is not background. Context is half of the meaning.

**Culture** — the shared assumptions, references, and rules the author and audience inhabit. A meme that lands perfectly inside a particular online community lands because that community has spent months building the shared references that make it readable. The same meme posted to strangers looks like noise. Culture is the room the text is being read in. You can only hear the joke if you are already inside the room.

**Genre** — the kind of communication this is. A tweet, a thread, a slide deck, a poem, a press release, a syllabus, a hospital chart, a terms-of-service agreement. Each genre carries expectations the reader brings before reading a single word. The writer can meet those expectations, exceed them, or break them deliberately. Breaking a genre is sometimes the entire point.

These seven elements are a diagnostic, not a checklist. You do not run through them mechanically and fill in blanks. You use them to see what you would otherwise miss. And the one students most consistently miss — in my experience teaching this — is context. They read the text in isolation, as if it arrived from nowhere, existed in a vacuum, and was meant for anyone. It was not. It arrived from somewhere, it exists in a moment, and it was meant for someone specific. The gap between that specific intended reader and you is often where the most interesting things are hiding.

## What "authenticity" actually is — a worked example

Here is a case that makes the machinery visible.

Selena Gomez by 2021 had 260 million followers on Instagram and 65 million on Twitter.[^1] In 2017 she ceded login control of her Instagram to an assistant because the constant exposure was harming her mental health. She had been diagnosed with lupus in 2015 and bipolar disorder in 2018. In one *Harper's Bazaar* piece she described deleting the apps: "I suddenly had to learn how to be with myself."[^2]

[^1]: Janelle Okwodu, ["Selena Gomez Wants Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter to Do Better," *Vogue*, 7 Jan. 2021](https://www.vogue.com/article/selena-gomez-social-media-leaders).
[^2]: Sabrina Park, ["Selena Gomez Reveals Why She Sacrificed Social Media for the Sake of Her Mental Health," *Harper's Bazaar*, 19 Aug. 2021](https://www.harpersbazaar.com/celebrity/latest/a37348873/selena-gomez-mental-health-giving-up-instagram/).

Now apply the seven elements. Author: not just Gomez alone — this is a coordinated output of a media team, an interview process, editorial decisions at *Harper's Bazaar*, and the longer arc of a carefully managed public persona. Audience: primarily fans, but also journalists, potential collaborators, and the general public who would encounter the story through aggregation. Purpose: disclosure, yes, but also reputation management in a moment when mental health discourse had become both more mainstream and more commercially useful. Stance: vulnerable but composed — not collapsed, not guarded. Context: the 2020s, in which stepping back from social media has become a legible and respected public move. Culture: a media environment in which *authenticity* has become perhaps the most valuable currency an influencer can hold. Genre: the celebrity confessional interview, a well-established form with its own conventions.

Now look at that sentence again: *I suddenly had to learn how to be with myself.*

The disclosure is specific enough to feel real. The vulnerability is named without being performed to excess. The phrasing — *be with myself* — has the cadence of something said aloud, not composed. It performs spontaneity.

This is where students sometimes get uncomfortable, and I want to meet that discomfort directly. I am not saying Gomez is lying. The lupus is real. The bipolar disorder is real. The decision to step back was real. All of that is true. *And* the public communication of all of that is rhetorically constructed — shaped for an audience, designed with a purpose, delivered in a genre that rewards exactly this kind of disclosure. Both things are true at once. The truth and the rhetorical packaging are the same artifact.

The word for what Gomez's team understood is this: *authenticity in 2026 is not the absence of rhetorical construction. Authenticity is a particular rhetorical construction — one designed to feel like the absence of one.* The most strategic move, in an environment that rewards rawness, is to read as un-strategic.

When you encounter a post that strikes you as *raw*, *honest*, *unfiltered* — the rhetorical question is not whether it is real. The question is: who built that effect? Sometimes nobody did; you are reading a rare, genuinely unmediated outburst. Often somebody did. The seven elements give you a way to figure out which, or at least to ask the question honestly.

## A slower example — Hiroshima, 1945

Now the contrast case. I want to use this one because the stakes make the machinery impossible to ignore.

On 6 August 1945, at about 8:15 in the morning Japanese time, a B-29 named *Enola Gay* dropped a uranium gun-type fission weapon on the city of Hiroshima. About sixteen hours later, the Truman administration released this sentence: "Sixteen hours ago an American airplane dropped one bomb on Hiroshima, Japan, and destroyed its usefulness to the enemy."[^3]

[^3]: *The Atomic Bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki*, Manhattan Engineer District, United States Army (1946), via [Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/685/pg685.html).

Read that sentence twice.

*An American airplane dropped one bomb on Hiroshima, Japan, and destroyed its usefulness to the enemy.*

Apply the elements. Author: the executive office of the United States in wartime — not Truman speaking off the cuff but a coordinated government communication designed for maximum reach. Audience: global press, the American public, the Japanese government, Allied governments, anyone with a radio. Purpose: announce a fact, justify it, signal capability, demand surrender. Stance: matter-of-fact, restrained — which is itself a posture, a kind of pride that performs confidence. Context: six years of total war, hundreds of thousands of deaths already, an active Japanese government still in the field. Culture: a wartime culture that has built a grammar for describing destruction. Genre: official government statement, formal register, designed for rebroadcast and quotation.

Now look at what the sentence is actually doing.

*An American airplane* — singular. There was one plane that dropped the bomb. There were also support planes and observation planes; the singular is doing work. *Dropped one bomb* — singular. The casualty estimates from Hiroshima run between 70,000 and 140,000 dead, with later estimates going higher. *One bomb* is not a factual error. It is a selection. *On Hiroshima, Japan* — the city is named. Specific. *And destroyed its usefulness to the enemy.*

The noun *usefulness* is carrying the entire moral weight of the sentence. Cities have residents. Cities have hospitals. Cities have children. *Usefulness* erases all of that. What was destroyed, in the sentence's grammar, is a military and industrial utility. Not people. Not buildings. Utility. The sentence is not lying — the plane did drop the bomb, the city's utility was destroyed. The rhetorical machinery is in the choice of what to name and what to abstract into a different kind of noun.

<!-- → [IMAGE: the Truman statement sentence typeset in large display text with annotation callouts on each phrase — "An American airplane" (singular; support planes elided), "one bomb" (singular; scale abstracted), "Hiroshima, Japan" (named specifically), "usefulness to the enemy" (human casualties converted to functional category) — student should be able to see the selection/abstraction pattern visually before reading the prose explanation] -->

I bring this example not because we are studying the history of nuclear weapons. I bring it because this is the same grammar operating in every modern political statement, corporate press release, and product launch you have ever read. The specificity of what gets named, and the abstraction of what gets made into a category — that mechanism is everywhere. The Truman example is shocking because the stakes are so high; the same mechanism in low-stakes texts is no less present.

You will read syllabi, employment contracts, dating-app bios, AI company statements about safety, terms of service, eviction notices, university admissions decisions. Every one of them will be performing the same selection — what to name, what to abstract. The shock of the Hiroshima example is useful precisely because it strips away the comfortable feeling that rhetoric is only a concern when somebody famous is trying to manipulate you. It is always operating. In everything.

## Critical reading — four moves

Here is where this becomes practical.

When you sit down to respond to a piece of digital communication — which is where this chapter is leading — you are doing four distinct things, in this order. Doing them out of order is the most common failure I see.

**Summary.** What does the text say? Reduce it to one sentence of plain content. If you cannot do this, you have not read it carefully enough, and everything that follows will be built on sand.

**Paraphrase.** Restate the text's specific claims and moves in your own words, at roughly the original length. Paraphrase is not summary — paraphrase preserves structure. It is the move that proves you understood *how* the text said what it said, not just what it said. You cannot fake a careful paraphrase. Either you followed the argument or you didn't.

**Analysis.** Now name the machinery. Author, audience, purpose, stance, context, culture, genre. What was selected. What was abstracted. What was made present and what was made absent. This is what the seven elements are for. You are not judging yet. You are seeing.

**Evaluation.** Now you can judge. Does the text do what it set out to do? Does it succeed rhetorically? Does it succeed *ethically*? Those are different questions. The Truman statement is rhetorically masterful and ethically devastating. Both are true. You are allowed — required — to say so.

The move students most reliably make wrong is to skip directly from summary to evaluation: *I read this and I disagree.* That is a reaction, not an analysis. Analysis is the step between, and it is the step that earns the right to evaluate. If you cannot show me what you saw, you have not earned the judgment.

## The 2026 problem — when the author is partly a machine

Here is where the inherited framework meets a genuine problem.

When the rhetorical machinery was built — Aristotle, Cicero, the twentieth-century scholars who formalized the rhetorical situation — every text had a human author. Sometimes a committee of human authors. Always human. The *author* slot was the thing you could in principle identify and interview.

That is no longer the only case.

A post you read in 2026 may have been written by a human sitting alone with a phone. Or a human working with a language model who edited and approved the output. Or a human who pasted a prompt into a model and posted the result unedited. Or a model running on a schedule, posting on behalf of an account whose human owner approves the general direction. Or a model running fully autonomously, with no current human in the loop.

Each of these produces a different rhetorical situation. The seven elements still apply — but the author slot is now a hybrid object you cannot interview, with intentions you cannot fully infer, with a relationship to *meaning* that is genuinely different from a single human author's relationship to meaning. A language model does not have purposes in the way a person has purposes. It has training distributions and prompts and objectives set by the humans who built and deployed it. When you ask *why did the author say this*, the answer to that question is architecturally different for an LLM than for a person.

This changes the analysis step. When you cannot identify the author with confidence, you do two passes. One analyzing the text as if a human wrote it. One analyzing the text as if a model wrote it — or a human-model collaboration. The same words will read differently under each pass. The model-reading surfaces different things: choices that look like training-distribution defaults, phrasings that read like an average of all available phrasings, an absence of the specific irrelevant details a real person would include. (A human writing at 2 a.m. about a bad day might mention what they had for dinner. A model would not.)

You will get good at this fast. The skill is already being developed by anyone who scrolls, even without explicit instruction. What this chapter is asking is that you make it conscious — that you practice it on purpose, and that you say what you see without pretending the uncertainty does not exist.

## The assignment — a critical response

You will write one to two pages of critical analysis on a piece of digital communication. A tweet, a thread, a meme, a TikTok, a press release, an AI company blog post, an op-ed, a Discord exchange, a podcast clip, an algorithmic feed itself. Anything digital, anything short enough that you can analyze it carefully in one to two pages.

The response moves in order: summary, paraphrase, analysis, evaluation. The analysis must explicitly use the seven elements. The evaluation must be earned by the analysis — you cannot say *this is good* or *this is bad* without first showing what you saw.

Pick a small text. A single tweet is harder to analyze than you think, and that difficulty is productive. The smaller the text, the more your analysis has to carry. Pick a text that confuses you — not one you already know what to think about. The texts you already know what to think about will give you an analysis made of pre-formed opinions. The texts that confuse you will give you new seeing.

Render the rhetorical situation specifically. "The author is a politician" is not an analysis. "The author is a senator posting at 11:47 p.m. on a contested vote night, in the genre of an extemporaneous reply rather than a prepared statement, to an audience that includes both constituents and opposing researchers looking for ammunition" — that is analysis. Do the second kind.

Distinguish rhetoric from ethics in the evaluation. Some texts do their rhetorical work brilliantly and aim it at something corrosive. Some texts are rhetorically clumsy and aim at something true. Most are mixed. Say which is which, and do not pretend you don't see one when you see the other.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 2

**What you're building:** a draft of your Critical Response, using Claude as an analytical interlocutor — not as a writer.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended if you want to keep the analysis alongside the original text across multiple work sessions.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1–2 page critical response to a piece of digital
communication for a college writing class.

The text I'm analyzing is below. After the text, I'm going to ask
you to interview me about its rhetorical situation. Do not summarize
the text. Do not analyze it for me. Ask me questions, one at a time,
that push me toward my own analysis.

Cover at least these seven elements, but ask follow-ups whenever my
answer is vague:

- Author: who is actually speaking (not the handle — the real
  person, group, or system)
- Audience: who the text was written for (not who happened to see it)
- Purpose: why this text exists
- Stance: the author's posture toward the subject
- Context: what's happening around the text
- Culture: the shared assumptions the author and intended audience
  share
- Genre: what kind of text this is and what expectations come with
  that kind

After we've worked through all seven, ask me one more question: do
I think the text was written by a human, an LLM, or a collaboration —
and what about the text makes me think so?

Then summarize my answers back to me as a clean analysis I can use as
the body of my critical response. Do not draft the response itself.

Here is the text:
[paste the text you're analyzing]
```

**What this produces:** a structured set of analytical observations, all of them your own, organized for use in the critical response. After this exercise, you draft the response yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written. Both tools have a tendency to start analyzing the text on their own; if that happens, push back: *"Stop. I asked you to interview me. Ask the first question."*
- *For Claude Projects:* paste the text once at the start of the project; then bring future critical-response sessions in as new conversations.
- *For Cowork:* useful if your text is a long thread or transcript — Cowork can hold the source alongside your developing analysis in linked files.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool for this exercise.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the source text is itself plausibly AI-written, Claude will sometimes hedge or get defensive about analyzing its own kin. If you suspect that is happening: *"Analyze this text on the assumption that an LLM did write it. What changes about your analysis?"* That second pass is often the most revealing one.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Cynthia Selfe** was working on computers and composition — making the case that digital writing was a *rhetorical* activity, not a typing activity — decades before anyone called themselves a content creator. Here's a prompt to find out more, and then make it better.

**Run this:**

```
Who was Cynthia Selfe, and how does their work on computers and
composition as a discipline connect to the rhetorical situation of
digital media? Keep it to three paragraphs. End with the single most
surprising thing about their career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"Cynthia Selfe"** on Wikipedia after you run this. See what the model got right, got wrong, or left out.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it to explain Selfe's argument that paying attention to the *interface* is part of paying attention to the rhetoric, with a contemporary example (a TikTok, a Substack, a group chat).
- Ask: "What did Selfe see in 1990s computers and composition that didn't become broadly true until the 2010s?"
- Add the framing: "Answer as if you're writing the introduction to a 2026 anthology of Selfe's foundational essays."

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. Find a press release from any company published in the last thirty days. Identify the seven elements of its rhetorical situation in a single paragraph. Focus on *audience* and *purpose* — who was this actually written for, and what was it designed to do? *(Tests: identifying rhetorical situation elements; distinguishing intended audience from general public.)*

2. Take the following passive-voice sentence and rewrite it in active voice: "Errors were made in the handling of customer data, and steps have been taken to ensure this does not recur." Then answer: what does the passive voice hide that the active voice would expose, and why might the author have chosen it? *(Tests: recognizing stance as a rhetorical choice embedded in grammar.)*

3. Name the genre of each of the following: a university acceptance letter, a CEO's LinkedIn post after a product failure, a Wikipedia article about a living politician, a Terms of Service agreement. For each, name one expectation readers bring to the genre before reading a word. *(Tests: genre recognition and how genre shapes reading.)*

**Application**

4. Find a social media post — from any platform — in which the author presents a personal experience as evidence for a larger claim. Apply all seven elements of the rhetorical situation. Then write two sentences distinguishing what the post *says* (summary) from what it *does* (rhetorical function). *(Tests: applying the full seven-element diagnostic; distinguishing summary from analysis.)*

5. Locate a public apology — from a public figure, company, or institution — issued within the past year. Run the four-move critical reading sequence on it: summary, paraphrase, analysis, evaluation. Your evaluation must distinguish rhetorical success from ethical success. They are not the same question. *(Tests: four-move sequence; rhetoric/ethics distinction.)*

6. Select a meme that circulates within a specific online community you belong to. Explain why it would read as noise to an outsider. Your explanation must use *culture* and *context* from the seven elements — not just describe the meme's content. *(Tests: applying culture and context as analytical categories rather than background decoration.)*

7. Find a text you initially read as authentic, raw, or unfiltered. Apply the Gomez framework: identify the rhetorical construction that produces the effect of authenticity. Name the author, audience, genre, and purpose. Does the construction being visible change how you read the text? *(Tests: applying the authenticity-as-construction argument to a new case.)*

**Synthesis**

8. Choose any political statement from the past five years — a press conference, a prepared speech excerpt, a tweet, a government release. Run the Truman analysis: identify one noun or phrase that abstracts something that could have been named concretely. Explain what the abstraction does rhetorically and what it hides. Then evaluate: does the abstraction constitute dishonesty, or is it a legitimate rhetorical choice? Defend your position. *(Tests: close reading of word choice as rhetorical selection; rhetoric/ethics distinction under pressure.)*

9. Find a piece of digital communication you genuinely cannot determine was written by a human or a model. Run both passes: analyze it once as if a human wrote it, once as if a model did. Write a paragraph on each pass. Then write a third paragraph: what changed between the passes, and what does that change reveal about the text? *(Tests: the two-pass method; awareness of how assumed authorship changes analysis.)*

10. Write a 300-word critical response to a piece of digital communication of your choice. All four moves — summary, paraphrase, analysis, evaluation — must be present and in order. The analysis must name at least five of the seven elements explicitly. The evaluation must earn its judgment from the analysis. *(Tests: full integration of the chapter's analytical and evaluative sequence.)*

**Challenge**

11. The seven-element rhetorical situation framework was developed for human authors. Propose a modification — add an element, split an existing one, redefine one — that would make the framework more useful for analyzing texts of uncertain authorship. Write a one-paragraph defense of your proposed change. *(Open-ended; points toward Chapter 3's treatment of AI authorship and the ongoing problem of attribution.)*

12. Pick an algorithmic feed — your TikTok For You page, your Instagram Explore, your YouTube recommendations, your Twitter/X timeline — and treat the feed itself as a text. Who is the author? What is the audience? What is the purpose? What is the stance? Apply all seven elements, with the understanding that some may require you to argue for an unconventional interpretation of the element. What does the feed *do* rhetorically that a single post cannot? *(Open-ended; tests whether the student can extend the framework beyond the individual text to the system that selects and sequences texts.)*

---



If careful evidence showed that students who learned only the simpler three-element model — *author, audience, purpose* — wrote more analytically precise responses than students taught the seven-element version, I would revise the central claim of this chapter. I have not seen that evidence. The specific virtue of including *context*, *culture*, *stance*, and *genre* is that those are the elements students consistently miss when they are not named. They read the text in isolation. They miss the room. Cutting those four elements would be cheaper to teach and worse to use.

## Still unresolved

The author-identification problem in 2026 is genuinely open. The two-pass approach I've described — analyze once assuming human, once assuming model — is workable but unsatisfying. It treats a foundational uncertainty as a procedural step, which is a patch, not a solution. The rhetorical-situation framework was built for a media environment where the author slot held a person. That is no longer guaranteed. The honest position is that the patches are temporary, the question is live, and the students working through this chapter in 2026 are going to have to help figure it out. I do not have a cleaner answer than that yet.
