# Chapter 12 — The Position Argument

*Defending a Claim a Reader Might Reject.*

A scene from May 22, 1964.

A man in his mid-fifties stands at a podium at the football stadium of the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor. He is the President of the United States. He has been in the office for six months — since the assassination of his predecessor — and he is preparing to give a graduation address that he and his speechwriters know will be quoted for decades.

He opens with humor. He addresses the graduates. He widens his audience by addressing the nation through them. Then he does something the genre of presidential graduation address does not require him to do. He stakes out a position.

> The Great Society rests on abundance and liberty for all. It demands an end to poverty and racial injustice, to which we are totally committed in our time.[^1]

[^1]: Lyndon B. Johnson, ["Remarks at the University of Michigan, May 22, 1964"](https://www.lbjlibrary.org/exhibits/great-society-speech). The full speech is in the Lyndon B. Johnson Presidential Library archives.

The sentences are short. The commitments are specific. The position is contestable — many in the audience and in the country did not believe poverty could be ended, did not believe racial injustice was the federal government's responsibility to address, did not believe *abundance and liberty for all* was a coherent goal. Johnson knew. He stated the position anyway, in language that would not allow him to back down later.

That move — *stating a position on a contested issue with enough specificity that you cannot retreat from it* — is what the position argument is.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to write a position argument of your own. 1,500 to 2,500 words. A debatable issue. A claim you are willing to defend. Evidence that makes the defense credible. Engagement with the strongest objections to your claim. A conclusion that does not soften what you committed to in the introduction.

## What a position argument is

A *position argument* is a written argument that takes a clear stance on a contested issue and defends it with reasoning and evidence. The genre overlaps with the proposal (Chapter 8) and the analytical report (Chapter 10), but it is distinct from each.

The proposal recommends a specific intervention. The position argument can recommend nothing — it can simply argue that *something is the case* or *something should be understood differently*. The proposal's structure is problem-solution. The position argument's structure is claim-defense.

The analytical report lets the evidence accumulate toward a conclusion. The position argument commits to the conclusion at or near the top and then defends it. The analytical report's voice is calibrated to neutrality. The position argument's voice is calibrated to advocacy — measured advocacy, evidence-anchored advocacy, but advocacy.

What separates the position argument from a rant, an opinion piece, or a manifesto:

- *The position is debatable.* If everyone reasonable already agrees, you do not have a position argument. You have a description.
- *The defense rests on evidence and reasoning, not on the writer's authority.* The reader is being asked to follow the case, not to defer to the writer.
- *The strongest objection is engaged, not ignored.* The position argument that pretends opposition doesn't exist is not yet doing the genre's work.
- *The advocacy is restrained enough to be persuasive to a skeptic.* The reader you are writing for is not the one who already agrees with you.

## The debatable issue — and what makes one

The single most consequential decision in a position argument is what to argue. Choose wrong and the rest of the draft is fighting an uphill battle. Choose well and the writing gets easier.

A *debatable issue* is a question whose answer is contested by people who have considered it. Three conditions, all required.

**The issue is contested in the live discourse.** Not in your friend group. In the broader conversation you are writing into — the news media, the academic literature, the policy world, the public. If the issue is not contested in that conversation, you do not have a debatable issue; you have a settled question.

**Reasonable, informed people can disagree.** This is the harder filter. Some issues are *contested* in public discourse because of motivated bad-faith disagreement (e.g., the shape of the Earth) rather than because reasonable people who have looked at the evidence reach different conclusions. The position argument is for the second kind. The first kind belongs in the analytical report — *here is the evidence, here is why the contested status is itself a fact about the politics rather than about the truth.*

**You can hold a position you are willing to defend in public.** The position has to be one you can argue without flinching. Not because every argument has to be aggressive — many of the best position arguments are quiet — but because the genre will ask you, in the objection section, to take on the strongest case against your view. If you secretly aren't sure you believe your own position, that section will collapse.

Examples of debatable issues that work at the student scale:

- *Should universities require all undergraduates to complete at least one course on the history of their own institution?*
- *Is the use of "AI detectors" in college courses doing more harm than the AI-generated student work the detectors are intended to find?*
- *Should the standard college admissions essay be eliminated as part of the application process?*
- *Is the federal student loan system, as currently structured, doing more harm than good to low-income students?*

Each of these has reasonable people on both sides, evidence available, and a specific enough framing to be argued in 1,500 to 2,500 words.

Examples of issues that won't work, and why:

- *Is climate change real?* Not debatable in the strong sense — the scientific consensus is settled. (Debatable issues exist about specific policies, specific responses, specific aspects of the science. The bare question is not one.)
- *Is American society too polarized?* Not specific enough. Too polarized about what, when, by what measure, compared to what?
- *Should everyone be required to vote?* Workable, but very large. Could be narrowed: *Should U.S. federal elections adopt the Australian model of compulsory voting with a small civil penalty for non-participation?*

The pattern: specificity makes a topic argueable. Generality kills it.

## The claim — your position, in a sentence

Once you have the issue, you need to state your position. A *claim* is a declarative sentence that names the issue and commits you to a position on it.

Examples:

- *Universities should not use AI detection software to screen student writing because the detectors' false-positive rates are higher than their accuracy gains and the resulting harms fall disproportionately on non-native English speakers.*
- *Federal student loans for undergraduate education at non-selective four-year colleges produce, on average, worse financial outcomes for low-income students than direct grant aid at equivalent cost to the federal government.*
- *The standard college admissions essay should be eliminated because it advantages students with access to expensive coaching while contributing minimal predictive value to admissions decisions.*

Each of these names the issue, takes a side, and gives a brief gesture at the reason. Each commits the writer to defending a specific claim, not a vague gesture. Each is unambiguously contestable — a reasonable reader could disagree with each one.

The claim should pass three tests.

**Specificity.** The terms in the claim are defined precisely enough that the reader knows exactly what is being argued.

**Defensibility.** You can imagine the evidence that would support the claim. You can also imagine the evidence that would refute it. Both have to be imaginable.

**Stake.** The claim matters — answering the question one way or the other changes something the reader cares about. *Whether the third item on the cafeteria menu Wednesday is a sandwich or a wrap* is debatable. It also doesn't matter. Stakes are required.

## The structure — two organizing options

There are two standard structures for the position argument. They produce different effects.

**Thesis-first.** State the claim in the introduction. Develop the supporting arguments in body paragraphs. Address objections. Close with a restatement that has accumulated weight from the evidence. Most students default to this. It works.

**Delayed-thesis.** Begin with the issue, the stakes, and the strongest claims on each side. Walk the reader through the evidence. Only late in the essay state your own position — at the point where the evidence has accumulated to make the position feel earned. This is harder to do well, but when it works it produces a stronger sense of having thought the question through with the reader.

The delayed-thesis structure is appropriate when:

- Your conclusion is one that would put off a skeptical reader if announced up front.
- The issue is genuinely difficult, and the position you reach is itself a function of careful reasoning the reader needs to follow.
- The audience is academically inclined and used to seeing arguments unfold over many paragraphs.

The thesis-first structure is appropriate when:

- Your audience expects a clear claim early.
- The argument depends on multiple supporting moves that need to be cumulative.
- Time and attention are constrained — most policy writing, most journalism, most professional position writing uses thesis-first by default.

Both are legitimate. Pick the one that fits your particular argument and audience.

## The objection section — the move most student drafts skip

The single most common failure in student position arguments is the absence — or the perfunctory handling — of the objection section.

The objection section is where you name the strongest cases against your claim and address them. *Strongest*, not *easiest*. The reader who is skeptical of your claim is reading specifically to see whether you have engaged with what they would say. If you have not, you have lost them.

What strong objections look like in practice:

For the AI-detection claim above, the strongest objection is not *but cheating is bad*. The strongest objection is *the detectors are imperfect tools but they raise the cost of casual cheating enough that they reduce overall academic dishonesty even with false positives; the policy may produce specific harms but it also produces specific goods that are not being counted in this argument*. That is a real objection. A position argument that does not engage with it will not persuade anyone who already finds it compelling.

For the student-loans claim, the strongest objection is not *people should pay their debts*. It is *the alternative — replacing loans with grants — would either cost more to taxpayers or produce political instability around higher-education funding that has historically led to worse outcomes than the current system*. Engaging with that objection is the work.

Two specific moves that strengthen the objection section.

**Concession before refutation.** Acknowledge the parts of the objection that are correct. *The objection's underlying concern about academic dishonesty is real and worth taking seriously.* The concession does not weaken your position. It signals that you are arguing in good faith and that your eventual refutation has done the work of seeing what is true on the other side.

**Refutation that names specific evidence or reasoning.** *The concern is real, but the specific intervention (AI detectors) does not address it well because [specific evidence]. A better response to the same underlying concern would be [alternative]*. The refutation has to do real work. Hand-waving past the objection signals the writer has not yet earned the position.

## Voice — measured advocacy, not aggression

The position argument can have heat in it. It is a piece of writing in which the writer is committed to a contested view. The reader will be able to tell.

The trap: confusing *heat* with *aggression*. A piece of writing that is aggressive — sarcastic, dismissive, contemptuous toward the opposing side — will fail with exactly the reader you most need to persuade. The reader who already agrees with you will enjoy the aggression. The reader who doesn't will withdraw.

Measured advocacy is the move:

- *State the claim clearly.* Do not hedge it. *I believe* and *I think* are usually unnecessary in a position argument; the whole document is your thinking.
- *Render your evidence specifically.* Statistics with sources. Examples with names. Reasoning with steps.
- *Name the opposing view fairly.* Steel-man, do not straw-man. The position you defeat in the objection section should be the strongest version of the opposing case, not the easiest.
- *Restrain the adjectives.* *Devastating*, *outrageous*, *clearly* — these signal you are reaching for emotional force when you should be reaching for evidence. Cut them.
- *Allow the conclusion its force without overstatement.* The position you have defended is the position you have defended. Do not claim more than the evidence supports.

A small voice note: position arguments benefit from sentence variety. Long sentences for accumulation, short sentences for landing. The rhythm itself does rhetorical work. Read your draft aloud. If the rhythm is uniform, the argument is flat.

## A worked example, condensed

A student writing in 2024 on the AI-detection question — say her name is Maya — produced a position argument with this structure.

*Introduction.* She names the issue: increasing use of AI-detection software in college courses since 2023. She names the position: that the policy is producing more harm than it prevents. She offers a brief gesture at the reason: the detectors' false-positive rates and the disparate impact on non-native English speakers. The introduction takes one paragraph.

*Background.* What AI detectors do. How widely they have been adopted. What the documented false-positive rates are. The first cited evidence appears here.

*First supporting argument.* The false-positive rate is the central problem. Studies cited (Sadasivan et al. 2023 and similar). Specific institutions named where the policy has produced specific student harms. The argument is anchored in numbers and named cases.

*Second supporting argument.* The disparate impact on non-native English speakers. Two cited studies. One specific account, with the student's name and institutional affiliation, of a non-native speaker accused of AI use whose prose had been verifiably their own.

*Third supporting argument.* The detector arms race. As models improve, detectors fail. The history over 2023–2024 shows the detection rate dropping even as the policy persists in institutions.

*Objection section.* Three objections, in order of strength. Cheating is real. Detectors raise the cost of dishonesty even with errors. Institutions need *some* tool. For each, a concession naming what the objection gets right, followed by an evidence-anchored refutation.

*Conclusion.* The case restated. The recommendation implicit — not a proposal, just the position that the policy as currently structured is harming students more than it helps. A final paragraph naming what would change Maya's mind: better detector accuracy combined with documented evidence of reduced cheating that compensated for the false-positive harms.

The argument is doing the genre's work. The position is specific. The evidence is anchored. The objection section engages the strongest cases. The voice is measured. The conclusion lands.

## A note about AI

The position argument is the genre in which the temptation to use a language model badly is highest.

Models are very good at producing prose that *sounds like* an argument. They are less good at producing prose that *is* an argument. The difference is what the genre tests.

Useful moves:

*Stress-testing your claim.* Once you have it, ask the model: *Here is my claim. What are the four strongest objections a hostile reader would make? Make them sharp. Don't soften.* The model will produce sharper objections than you would have generated on your own. You then have to grapple with them in your actual essay.

*Identifying gaps in your evidence.* Paste your draft and ask: *Where in this argument is a claim made without supporting evidence? Where am I asserting rather than demonstrating?* The model catches these reliably.

*Building the steel-manned opposition.* Ask the model to write a one-paragraph version of the strongest opposing position. Read it. Notice what it does that your draft hasn't engaged with.

Damaging moves:

*Drafting the argument.* The model will produce a defensible-looking argument. It will lack the specificity and the genuine commitment that the genre requires. Even careful editing rarely repairs the underlying flatness.

*Generating sources or statistics.* Models will produce confident-sounding citations that may be fabricated. The position argument depends on evidence the reader can verify. Every cite has to be one you read.

*"Improving the flow" of an objection section.* The model will tend to soften the objections — making the opposing case more polite, more reasonable in tone — which is the opposite of what the section needs. Strong objections are sharp. Smoothed objections are weak.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 12

**What you're building:** the analytical scaffold for your position argument — claim, evidence map, objection list, defensible conclusion — before you draft.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word position argument for a college writing
class. The genre requires a debatable claim, evidence-based defense,
and engagement with the strongest objections.

The issue I'm writing on: [name it in one sentence]
My tentative claim: [state it in one sentence]
The sources I have so far (with citations): [paste]

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. Stress-test my claim. Is it specific enough? Is it debatable? Is
   it defensible with the evidence I have? Ask three diagnostic
   questions, one at a time. Push back on vagueness.

2. Construct the three strongest objections a hostile reader would
   raise against my claim. Do not soften them. After listing them,
   tell me which one I am least prepared to address, based on the
   sources I have shared with you.

3. For each of my supporting points, identify the strongest piece of
   evidence in my sources that supports it. If a point has weak or
   no supporting evidence in my sources, flag it — I may need more
   sources before I can defend that point.

4. Once I've responded, propose a one-sentence claim sentence that
   incorporates the sharpening from steps 1–3.

Do not draft the argument. Do not write the introduction. The drafting
is mine to do.
```

**What this produces:** a sharpened claim, a sharper version of three strong objections, an evidence map. After this, you draft the argument yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful for arguments with many sources. Keep them in linked files Cowork can access.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the model constructs objections, it will sometimes hedge or refuse to fully argue the opposing side. *"I'm not sure that objection is really fair to your view..."* Push back: *"Don't worry about fair. Make the strongest case the opposition would make. The fairness is my problem in revision."*

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter steps back from genre-specific writing to examine the reasoning strategies themselves — analogy, cause and effect, classification, comparison, problem-solution, definition — that operate inside every kind of argument, including the one you have just been writing.

## What would change my mind

If a careful comparison of position arguments produced with extensive LLM assistance to position arguments produced without showed that the LLM-assisted versions were rated by readers as *more persuasive on contested issues*, I would revise the chapter's central concern that LLM-drafted position arguments lose the specific commitment the genre requires. The current evidence — admittedly partial — points the other way: position arguments where the writer is visibly invested tend to land harder, and the LLM tends to flatten precisely the investment. But the tools are changing, and the claim should be tested empirically as they do.

## Still puzzling

The line between *measured advocacy* and *flat hedging* is hard to teach. The chapter's advice — *restrain the adjectives, hold the position firmly, render evidence specifically* — produces good drafts when followed. It does not, on its own, produce the rare position arguments that genuinely change a skeptical reader's mind. Some of what makes those arguments work — the writer's specific voice, the particular configuration of stakes and evidence, the kairotic moment — is not teachable in a textbook section. The advice in this chapter is necessary but not sufficient.
