# Chapter 12 — The Position Argument

*Defending a Claim a Reader Might Reject.*

---

On May 22, 1964, Lyndon Johnson stood at the podium in Michigan Stadium at the University of Michigan, six months into a presidency he had not campaigned for, and gave a graduation address that he and his speechwriters knew would be quoted for decades. He opened with humor. He addressed the graduates. He widened his audience to the nation. And then he did something the genre of presidential graduation address does not require: he staked out a position.

> The Great Society rests on abundance and liberty for all. It demands an end to poverty and racial injustice, to which we are totally committed in our time.[^1]

[^1]: Lyndon B. Johnson, ["Remarks at the University of Michigan, May 22, 1964"](https://www.lbjlibrary.org/exhibits/great-society-speech). LBJ Presidential Library archives.

<!-- → [IMAGE: photograph of LBJ at the Michigan Stadium podium, May 22, 1964 — the scale of the audience visible; caption should note this was a commencement address, not a policy speech, which makes the specificity of the commitment unusual for the genre] -->

The sentences are short. The commitments are specific. Many in the audience and in the country did not believe poverty could be ended. Many did not believe racial injustice was the federal government's responsibility to address. Many did not believe *abundance and liberty for all* was a coherent goal, let alone an achievable one. Johnson knew. He stated the position anyway, in language that would not allow him to back down later.

That move — *stating a position on a contested issue with enough specificity that you cannot retreat from it* — is what the position argument is.

---

## What distinguishes this genre from adjacent ones

A position argument is a written argument that takes a clear stance on a contested issue and defends it with reasoning and evidence. The genre has two close neighbors and it is worth being precise about how they differ.

A *proposal* recommends a specific intervention to a specific problem. A position argument can recommend nothing — it can simply argue that something is the case, or that something should be understood differently. The proposal's structure is problem-solution. The position argument's structure is claim-defense.

An *analytical report* lets evidence accumulate toward a conclusion, often held back until the data has been laid out. The position argument commits to the conclusion at or near the top and then defends it. The analytical report's voice is calibrated to apparent neutrality. The position argument's voice is calibrated to advocacy — measured advocacy, evidence-anchored advocacy, but advocacy.

What separates the position argument from a rant, an opinion piece, or a manifesto is precision on four dimensions. The position is genuinely debatable — if everyone reasonable already agrees, you do not have a position argument, you have a description. The defense rests on evidence and reasoning rather than on the writer's authority, so the reader is being asked to follow the case, not to defer. The strongest objection is engaged and addressed, not ignored. And the advocacy is restrained enough to be persuasive to a skeptic — the reader you are writing for is not the one who already agrees with you.

## The debatable issue: what makes one work

The single most consequential decision in a position argument is what to argue. Choose wrong and the rest of the draft is fighting uphill.

Three conditions, all required, for a genuine debatable issue.

The issue is contested in the live discourse. Not in your friend group — in the broader conversation you are writing into. News coverage, academic literature, policy debate, public discussion. If the issue is not contested in that conversation, you have a settled question, and your job is to report the settlement rather than argue it.

Reasonable, informed people can disagree. This is the harder filter. Some issues are contested in public discourse because of motivated bad-faith disagreement — the shape of the Earth, the safety of vaccines — rather than because people who have examined the evidence reach different conclusions. The position argument is built for the second kind. Questions where the controversy is manufactured belong in an analytical report: *here is the evidence, and here is why the contested status is itself a fact about the politics rather than a fact about the truth.*

You can hold a position you are willing to defend publicly, including against its strongest opposition. The genre will require you, in the objection section, to take on the most compelling case against your view. If you secretly aren't sure you believe your own claim, that section will collapse and the reader will feel it.

Specificity is what converts a general topic into a workable issue. *Should universities require undergraduates to complete a course on the history of their own institution?* is workable. *Is AI changing education?* is not — it is too general to argue, because no claim could be specific enough to be falsified. *Is the use of AI detectors in college courses doing more harm than the AI-generated work the detectors are intended to catch?* is workable. *Should we address our polarized politics?* is not. *Should U.S. federal elections adopt compulsory voting with a small civil penalty for non-participation, on the Australian model?* is.

The pattern: specificity makes an issue arguable. Generality kills it.

## The claim: your position, in a sentence

Once you have the issue, you need to state your position. A claim is a declarative sentence that names the issue and commits you to a side.

Here is what a real claim looks like, compared to what students typically write.

The typical version: *AI detection software has become controversial in college courses and raises many important questions about fairness and academic integrity.* That is a topic description. It does not commit to anything and therefore cannot be defended or refuted.

A real claim: *Universities should not use AI detection software to screen student writing because the detectors' false-positive rates exceed their accuracy gains and the resulting harms fall disproportionately on non-native English speakers.*

The difference is commitment. The real claim names the issue, takes a side, and gestures at the reason. It is unambiguously contestable — a reasonable reader could reject each of its three moving parts. And it commits the writer to defending a specific claim, not a vague concern.

Three tests for a real claim.

*Specificity.* The terms are defined precisely enough that the reader knows exactly what is being argued. *Non-native English speakers* is specific; *marginalized groups* is not. *False-positive rates exceed accuracy gains* is falsifiable; *harms students* is not.

*Defensibility.* You can imagine the evidence that would support the claim, and you can imagine the evidence that would refute it. If you cannot imagine what would change your mind, you do not have a claim; you have a conviction. The reader can tell the difference.

*Stake.* The claim matters. Answering the question one way or the other changes something the reader cares about. Stakes are not optional — they are what give the reader a reason to keep reading.

---

## Structure: two options, and when each fits

Two standard structures for the position argument. They produce different effects in the reader.

*Thesis-first* states the claim in the introduction. Supporting arguments develop in body paragraphs. Objections are addressed. The conclusion restates the claim with the weight of the evidence accumulated behind it. Most students default to this structure. It works, and it is the right choice for most arguments.

*Delayed-thesis* begins with the issue, the stakes, and the strongest cases on each side. It walks the reader through the evidence. The writer's own position appears only late, at the point where the evidence has accumulated enough to make the position feel earned. This is harder to do well. When it works, it produces a stronger sense of having thought the question through with the reader, rather than having announced a conclusion and then defended it.

The delayed-thesis structure earns its difficulty when your conclusion would put off a skeptical reader if announced up front, when the issue is genuinely difficult and the position you reach is itself a function of careful reasoning the reader needs to follow, and when your audience is analytically inclined and willing to track an argument over many paragraphs.

The thesis-first structure is right when your audience expects a clear claim early, when the argument depends on multiple supporting moves that need to be cumulative, and when time and attention are constrained — which is most of the time in most of the writing environments a college graduate will enter.

Both are legitimate. The choice is not aesthetic; it follows from who your reader is and how your argument works.

## The objection section: the move most drafts skip

The single most common failure in student position arguments is the absence — or the perfunctory handling — of the objection section.

The objection section is where you name the strongest cases against your claim and address them. *Strongest*, not *easiest to dismiss*. The reader who is skeptical of your position is reading specifically to see whether you have engaged with what they would say. If you have not, you have lost them — not because you are wrong but because you have signaled that you have not done the work of taking the opposition seriously.

What strong objections actually look like: for the AI-detection claim above, the weakest objection is *but cheating is bad*. You can dismiss that in a sentence; it does not engage the substance of the claim. The strongest objection is something like: *even imperfect detectors raise the cost of casual cheating enough that they reduce overall academic dishonesty; the policy produces specific harms to specific populations but also produces specific goods that this argument doesn't count, and the full accounting might favor the policy.* That is a real objection. A position argument that does not engage with it will not persuade anyone who already finds it compelling.

Two moves that strengthen the objection section.

*Concession before refutation.* Acknowledge the parts of the objection that are correct. *The concern about academic dishonesty that motivates the detector policy is real and worth taking seriously.* This does not weaken your position — it signals you are arguing in good faith and that your eventual refutation has already done the work of seeing what is true on the other side. Readers who disagree with you are looking for the concession; when they find it, they keep reading.

*Refutation that names specific evidence.* *The concern is real, but this particular intervention does not address it well, because [evidence]. A better response to the same underlying concern would be [alternative].* The refutation has to do real work. Gesturing past the objection — *while this view has some merit, ultimately my position is correct* — is not a refutation; it is a demonstration that the objection was too strong to answer.

A note on steel-manning vs. straw-manning: the version of the opposition you defeat in the objection section should be the *strongest* version of the opposing case, not the easiest. If the reader recognizes the opposing view you are addressing as a weakened or distorted version of what serious critics actually argue, you have undermined your own credibility. Steel-man the opposition.

## Voice: measured advocacy

The position argument can have heat in it. You are committed to a contested view, and the reader will be able to tell.

The trap is confusing heat with aggression. A piece of writing that is sarcastic, dismissive, or contemptuous toward the opposing side will fail with precisely the reader you most need to persuade. The reader who already agrees with you will enjoy the aggression. The reader who doesn't will withdraw, and they are the only one the position argument is designed to change.

Measured advocacy is the move. State the claim clearly — *I believe* and *I think* are usually unnecessary in a position argument; the whole document is your thinking and the reader knows it. Render evidence specifically — statistics with sources, examples with names, reasoning with steps. Name the opposing view fairly and in its strongest form. Restrain the adjectives. *Devastating*, *outrageous*, *clearly*, *obviously* — these reach for emotional force when the argument should be reaching for evidence. Allow the conclusion its force without overstatement: the position you have defended is the position you have defended; do not claim more than the evidence supports.

A small observation about rhythm. Position arguments benefit from sentence variety — long sentences for accumulation, short sentences for landing. The rhythm itself does rhetorical work. Read the draft aloud. If the rhythm is uniform throughout, the argument is flat.

## The AI problem in this genre

The position argument is the genre in which the temptation to use a language model badly is highest, and the reason is instructive.

Models produce prose that sounds like an argument. They are less reliable at producing prose that *is* an argument. The difference is in the specific commitment the genre requires: a position argument works when the writer is visibly invested in a particular claim, has engaged with the specific evidence bearing on it, and has grappled genuinely with the strongest cases against it. The model tends to flatten exactly the investment that makes a position argument land.

Additionally, the objection section — the hardest section to write — is where the model is most likely to do damage. Models will soften the opposition. They will produce objections that are more polite and more diffuse than the actual strong versions, which is the opposite of what the section needs. The rhetorical effect of engaging a softened objection is that readers who hold the strong version feel their view was not taken seriously.

Where the model is genuinely useful:

Stress-testing the claim before drafting. Once you have a tentative position, ask the model to name four objections a hostile reader would raise. Ask it to make them sharp. The model generates sharper opposition than most writers generate on their own, and the list is useful material for the objection section.

Identifying gaps in evidence. After drafting, paste the argument and ask the model where claims are being made without supporting evidence — where you are asserting rather than demonstrating. Models are accurate at this diagnosis.

Building the steel-manned opposition. Ask the model to write a one-paragraph version of the strongest case against your claim. Read it. Notice what it does that your draft has not yet engaged with.

Where the model does damage:

Drafting any part of the argument, including the introduction. The model will produce a defensible-looking argument that lacks the specific commitment the genre requires. Careful editing rarely repairs the underlying flatness.

Generating sources or statistics. Models produce confident-sounding citations that may be fabricated. The position argument depends on evidence the reader can verify. Every cite has to be one you have read.

Revising the objection section for flow. The model will smooth the objections, making them more polite, which is exactly what you do not want. Strong objections are sharp; smoothed objections are weak.

## What would change my mind

If a careful comparison of position arguments produced with extensive LLM assistance to arguments produced without it showed that the LLM-assisted versions were rated by readers as more persuasive on genuinely contested issues, I would revise the central concern of this chapter that LLM-drafted arguments lose the specific commitment the genre requires. The current evidence — admittedly partial — points the other way: arguments where the writer is visibly invested tend to land harder with skeptical readers, and the model tends to flatten precisely that investment. But the tools are changing fast, and the claim should be tested empirically as they do.

---

## Still puzzling

The line between measured advocacy and flat hedging is hard to teach. The advice in this chapter — restrain the adjectives, hold the position firmly, render evidence specifically — produces good drafts when followed. It does not, on its own, produce the rare position arguments that genuinely change a skeptical reader's mind. Some of what makes those arguments work — the writer's specific voice, the particular configuration of stakes and evidence, the moment in which the argument lands — is not teachable in a textbook section. The advice here is necessary. It is not sufficient.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. *Issue audit.* Choose three topics you could imagine arguing about. For each, apply the three conditions from this chapter: Is it contested in the live discourse? Can reasonable, informed people disagree based on evidence? Can you hold a position you would defend publicly against strong opposition? Classify each topic as workable, unworkable, or workable-with-narrowing. For the workable-with-narrowing cases, write the narrowed version. (Tests: ability to apply the debatability filter before investing in an argument, and to diagnose what makes a topic too general.)

2. *Claim surgery.* Below are three topic-description sentences. Rewrite each as a real claim — one that names a side, commits specifically, and passes the three tests (specificity, defensibility, stake).
   - *Social media has changed how young people communicate.*
   - *The criminal justice system faces many complex challenges.*
   - *College athletes and compensation is a topic of ongoing debate.*
   For each rewrite, identify which of the three tests your new version passes that the original failed. (Tests: the core skill of converting a topic into a claim — the move most position arguments fail at the start.)

3. *Steel-man drill.* Take a claim you already believe strongly — on any issue. Write a one-paragraph argument for the *opposite* position, as if you genuinely held it. Make it as strong as you can. Then identify: what would you have to concede from your original position to answer this paragraph? (Tests: the steel-man move in isolation, before the student has to perform it inside a full argument under deadline pressure.)

**Application**

4. *Objection triage.* For the claim you are developing for your position argument, generate five objections — the five strongest cases a hostile reader would raise. Rank them from hardest to easiest to address. Write one sentence for each explaining why you ranked it where you did. Then draft the concession-before-refutation move for the hardest objection only. (Tests: ability to triage objections by strength rather than convenience, and to perform the concession move on genuinely difficult opposition.)

5. *Structure decision.* Outline the same argument twice — once as thesis-first, once as delayed-thesis. For each outline, write two sentences explaining what a reader experiences at the moment the claim appears. Which structure fits your argument and your likely reader? State the reason, not just the preference. (Tests: ability to make the structure choice analytically rather than by default, and to think about reader experience at specific points in the argument.)

6. *Voice audit.* Take a draft paragraph from your position argument — or write one fresh. Read it aloud. Mark every adjective or adverb that is reaching for emotional force rather than descriptive precision (*devastating*, *clearly*, *obviously*, *deeply troubling*). For each marked word: either replace it with specific evidence that earns the force the word was borrowing, or cut it. Then read the revised paragraph aloud. (Tests: the measured-advocacy discipline applied to actual prose — the most common single failure in student position arguments after the objection section.)

**Synthesis**

7. *The full objection section.* Write the complete objection section for your position argument: at least two objections, each with a named concession and an evidence-anchored refutation. The objections must be steel-manned — the version a serious critic of your position would actually make, not the easiest version you could find. After drafting, read the section aloud and ask: would a reader who holds the opposing view feel their position was taken seriously? If not, revise until they would. (Tests: the hardest move in the chapter — holding the opposing view in good faith while refuting it with evidence, in a complete section rather than a drill.)

8. *What would change your mind.* Write a 150-word paragraph — in the register of the "What would change my mind" sections in this textbook — naming specifically what evidence or argument would cause you to revise or abandon your position argument's central claim. The conditions have to be real: falsifiable, grounded in what you actually know about the evidence, not pro forma. (Tests: whether the student has a genuine claim or a conviction — a claim is revisable in principle; a conviction is not; and readers can tell the difference.)

**Challenge**

9. *Write the argument.* 1,500–2,500 words. A debatable issue you have chosen and narrowed. A claim that passes all three tests. Supporting evidence anchored to sources you have read. An objection section that steel-mans the opposition and responds with specific evidence. A conclusion that does not soften what you committed to in the introduction. Use the LLM exercise to stress-test the claim and map the evidence before drafting. The argument is yours to write.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 12

**What you're building:** the analytical scaffold for your position argument — sharpened claim, evidence map, objection list — before you draft the prose.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word position argument for a college writing
class. The genre requires a debatable claim, evidence-based defense,
and engagement with the strongest objections.

The issue I'm writing on: [name it in one sentence]
My tentative claim: [state it in one sentence]
The sources I have so far (with citations): [paste]

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. Stress-test my claim. Is it specific enough? Is it debatable? Is
   it defensible with the evidence I have? Ask three diagnostic
   questions, one at a time. Push back on vagueness.

2. Construct the three strongest objections a hostile reader would
   raise against my claim. Do not soften them. After listing them,
   tell me which one I am least prepared to address, based on the
   sources I have shared with you.

3. For each of my supporting points, identify the strongest piece of
   evidence in my sources that supports it. If a point has weak or
   no supporting evidence in my sources, flag it — I may need more
   sources before I can defend that point.

4. Once I've responded, propose a one-sentence claim that incorporates
   the sharpening from steps 1–3.

Do not draft the argument. Do not write the introduction. The drafting
is mine to do.
```

**What this produces:** a sharpened claim, a sharper version of three strong objections, an evidence map by supporting point. After this, you draft the argument yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful for arguments with many sources. Keep them in linked files Cowork can access.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the model constructs objections, it will sometimes hedge or refuse to fully argue the opposing side. Push back: *"Don't worry about being fair to my view. Make the strongest case the opposition would make. The fairness is my problem in revision."*

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Stuart Hall** was working on essays that argued positions in the open — "Encoding/Decoding," "The Whites of Their Eyes," "Notes on Deconstructing the Popular" — at the founding moment of cultural studies, decades before most people had heard of the position argument as a teachable genre. Here's a prompt to find out more — and then make it better.

**Run this:**

```
Who was Stuart Hall, and how does their work on encoding/decoding and
the position essay as cultural critique connect to writing a position
argument that engages a real opposition? Keep it to three paragraphs.
End with the single most surprising thing about their career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"Stuart Hall"** on Wikipedia after you run this. See what the model got right, got wrong, or left out.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it to explain Hall's "encoding/decoding" model and how it changed the way scholars think about audience reception of media.
- Ask: "Hall famously wrote, 'There is no escape from politics of representation.' What position argument did that sentence open — and what did it close?"
- Add the framing: "Answer in the structure of a Hall essay — a position taken at the top, opposition acknowledged, refutation that surprises."

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?
