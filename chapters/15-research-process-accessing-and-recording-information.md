# Chapter 15 — The Research Process

*How to Find What You Don't Yet Know You're Looking For.*

---

Here is a scene that will be familiar. You sit down with a topic and a deadline. You open a tab, type some words into a search engine, get back ten million results, click the third or fourth one, read it for five minutes, lose track of what you were looking for, follow a link to something tangentially interesting, lose more time, look at the clock, panic gently, and start again with slightly different keywords.

This is how most students research. It is the process of someone who has not been shown that research is a craft with specific techniques, ethical constraints, and recordable structure. Most of what goes wrong in a research paper was already going wrong in the first two hours of the search, before a single word was written.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to build a research log — a structured record of what you have found, what each source contributes to your argument, and how the sources relate to each other and to the position you are developing. The log is a tool. It exists so that, three weeks into a project, you know exactly what you have read and what you still need to find. Without it, most students lose roughly a third of their early research before drafting and then re-find it under pressure, badly.

## Two kinds of research — and why both matter

There are two broad approaches to academic research, and the strong argumentative research papers usually combine them in some proportion.

Library research is research into what others have already discovered, written, and published. It is the work most students think of when they hear *research*: journal articles, books, news reports, government data, archived documents, well-curated databases. The library is broader than the building — the relevant databases, digital archives, and high-quality freely available material online are all part of it.

Field research is research you conduct yourself by observing, interviewing, surveying, or measuring. It produces primary sources — original data nobody else has collected on your specific question. Field research is harder than library research for most student projects, but it can be the most important contribution of a strong paper because it produces evidence the existing literature does not yet contain.

Most student papers use predominantly library research. Some benefit substantially from a small amount of field work — a few interviews, a short observation, a brief survey — added to the library base. Some topics require more field research than library research. The choice is topic-dependent. The discipline is to make the choice deliberately rather than defaulting to whatever is easiest.

## Library research — the specific moves

A useful sequence, in order.

Generate keywords before you search. List the words and phrases you expect to find in good sources on your topic. Include synonyms. Include words that mean roughly the same thing but get used in different fields — *food sustainability* and *food security* mean slightly different things and the literatures using each term are sometimes disjoint. Finding both literatures requires knowing both terms. The model of generating keywords before searching, rather than discovering them by accident as you search, is the difference between a deliberate search and a random walk.

Use multiple databases. Single-database research produces single-database results. The same query in JSTOR, Google Scholar, Web of Science, and a discipline-specific database will produce overlapping but importantly different result sets. Your institution's library website gives you access to most of these. Use at least three for any research question you are taking seriously. The sources that appear in one but not the others are sometimes the most useful.

Read with two passes. First pass: skim the abstract or executive summary, look at the conclusion, check the bibliography. Decide whether the source is worth reading in full. Second pass: full read of the sources that passed the first filter. Most students try to do both passes at once and end up doing neither well. The first pass takes two minutes. The second pass takes twenty. Do not give twenty minutes to a source that does not deserve them.

Follow the citation trails. Every good academic source has a bibliography. The works it cites are themselves leads — the bibliography of a strong recent article points you to the older work it is engaging with. This *citation tracing* is one of the most productive research techniques and one of the least taught. If you find one strong recent source on your topic, its bibliography is a map of the conversation.

Save everything in a structured way. When you find a source, record the full citation, the URL or DOI, the date you accessed it, your initial credibility assessment, the specific claim or evidence you took from it, and where it fits in your developing argument. This becomes the research log.

## Evaluating sources — what the standard checklist catches and what it misses

A widely taught framework for source evaluation goes by the acronym CRAAP: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose. It is a useful starting checklist.

Currency asks when the source was published or updated. In fields that move quickly — AI, climate, technology — a five-year-old source may be substantially outdated. In other fields — Shakespeare scholarship, ancient history — a fifty-year-old source may still be authoritative. The question is not the date alone; it is whether the date matters for the question you are researching.

Relevance asks whether the source actually addresses your specific question, or whether it is on a related but different topic that you are tempted to treat as if it addressed yours. The temptation is high when a source is high-quality but not quite on point. Resist it. A strong source that does not address your question is not evidence for your argument.

Authority asks who wrote the source and what the publishing venue's reputation is. A peer-reviewed journal, a major newspaper, and a university press each imply different editorial controls. These controls are not guarantees of accuracy, but they are evidence of the scrutiny the work has undergone.

Accuracy asks whether the source's specific claims can be verified against other sources, whether it cites its own evidence, whether the methodology is defensible. A source that makes strong claims without citations is a different kind of evidence than one whose claims are carefully grounded.

Purpose asks why the source was produced. To inform, to persuade, to sell, to advocate. Each purpose carries its own bias. The bias is not disqualifying — a piece written to persuade can still be evidence — but the purpose has to be named and the bias factored in.

CRAAP catches most obvious source-quality problems. It does not catch everything. A few specific things it misses.

Institutional bias of journals. Even peer-reviewed journals have editorial perspectives. *Journal of Health Economics* and *Health Affairs* publish in overlapping but distinct positions on contested policy questions. Reading the journal's editorial board and recent issues reveals the bias that CRAAP does not see.

Author networks. Some scholars cite each other heavily because they are collaborators or friends; their citation network may not represent the field. Reading the bibliographies and the acknowledgements sections reveals this.

The settled framing. Some sources present contested questions as if they are settled. The framing is itself a bias. Reading a source against scholars who would disagree with it reveals what the source is not saying.

## Field research — when to do it and how

Some topics benefit from primary research you conduct yourself. A few specific methods.

Observation means going somewhere — a classroom, a public square, a digital community — and taking detailed notes on what you see. The notes have to distinguish observation (what you saw) from interpretation (what you think it meant). The discipline of separating the two in your notes is what makes observation a research method rather than a personal essay. The two postures are participant observation, where you participate in what you are observing, and non-participant observation, where you watch but do not participate. Participation gives you access to information an outsider cannot reach; non-participation preserves an analytical distance the participant has lost. Neither is inherently superior; the choice depends on the question.

Interviews mean asking open-ended questions of one or more people and recording their answers. The questions should be open-ended where possible — *describe a time when...*, *what was your experience of...* — rather than yes/no. An audio recording with permission, or careful written notes, is the record. Either way, transcribe key passages you might want to quote before you forget the context.

Surveys mean asking a number of people the same set of questions to produce comparable data across respondents. Survey design is its own craft. For a student project, three to ten well-crafted questions administered to a defined population usually beats a longer survey administered to whoever happens to respond. The respondents have to be a defined population for the results to mean anything.

Two requirements for field research at student scale. Get permission. If you are interviewing, observing, or surveying people, ask their permission, tell them what the research is for and how the data will be used, and let them refuse or retract. And know whether your institution requires IRB review before you begin. Most informal student research falls below the threshold, but check. Research involving children, prisoners, vulnerable populations, or potentially distressing topics usually requires it. The check is part of the practice.

## The research log — what it is and why it saves you

The research log is the document that holds your work together. Without it, you are carrying everything in your head, losing some of it, and rebuilding the rest under deadline pressure.

A useful structure has three components per source.

The first is source information: the full citation in your required format, the URL or DOI, the date you accessed it, whether you have the full text or only an abstract, your initial reliability assessment.

The second is specific claims and evidence: what this source actually says that you might use. Direct quotes with page numbers for passages you might cite. Paraphrases with page numbers for claims you might restate in your own voice. Statistics or specific data points with the source's own citations noted.

The third is synthesis notes: where this source fits in the argument you are developing. Which other sources it agrees with, contradicts, or extends. What questions it leaves unanswered. What it makes you think about that you had not been thinking about.

Here is a working example, condensed, for a paper on AI-detection software in college courses:

> **Source:** Sadasivan, Vinu et al. "Can AI-Generated Text Be Reliably Detected?" *arXiv preprint*, May 2023. https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11156. Accessed 12 Oct. 2024. Preprint, not yet peer-reviewed; authors at University of Maryland CS department.
>
> **Claims/Evidence:** Authors test multiple AI-detection systems against multiple LLM outputs and adversarial rewrites. Find that paraphrasing attacks reduce detection accuracy substantially. "Even the best-known detectors of LLM-generated text can have their accuracy reduced... by simple paraphrasing attacks" (p. 2). Detection accuracy drops from 95% to roughly 50% after light paraphrasing.
>
> **Synthesis notes:** Strong evidence for the technical-failure argument against AI detectors. Pairs with Liang et al. 2023 on biased false-positive rates for non-native English speakers. Together: technical evidence that detectors are both inaccurate against motivated cheaters and biased against innocent non-native speakers. This is the empirical spine of the position argument.

That takes more time than dumping URLs into a notes file. It saves time later by a factor that is hard to overstate. The discipline pays off when, three weeks into a project, you can find exactly what you need without re-reading everything from scratch under pressure.

## Plagiarism — the traditional question and the 2026 version

Plagiarism, in the traditional academic sense, is presenting someone else's work as your own. It can be deliberate — copying a paper, paying for a paper — or unintentional — paraphrasing without citing, copying a key phrase without quotation marks, summarizing without attribution. The traditional fix is consistent: cite everything that is not yours. When you summarize a source's ideas, cite. When you paraphrase a passage, cite. When you quote a phrase, use quotation marks and cite. When in doubt, cite.

The 2026 question is what plagiarism means when the source is a language model. The honest answer is that there is not yet a stable convention across institutions. Some treat LLM use as plagiarism unconditionally. Some treat it as plagiarism only when the output is presented as the student's own without disclosure. Some treat it as permissible with disclosure in a footnote or cover note.

The practical discipline, in the absence of a settled convention: be transparent. If you used an LLM to brainstorm, draft, edit, or interrogate your work, say so. The disclosure protects you. It also makes the LLM's contribution part of the work rather than a hidden ingredient, which is the more honest relationship to have with any tool you actually used.

Some specific moves that are clearly problematic: submitting LLM-generated text as if it were your own writing; asking an LLM to paraphrase a source for you and then citing the source as if you had read it; using LLM-generated text as evidence in an argument that requires the evidence to be your own analysis.

Some moves that are usually fine: using an LLM to brainstorm topics or research questions and writing the paper yourself afterward; using an LLM to identify gaps in a draft and revising the gaps yourself; using an LLM as an interrogation partner that asks you questions about your developing argument.

The line is fuzzier than I would like. The discipline is to ask, of each LLM use: would I be comfortable telling my instructor exactly how I used this tool? If yes, probably fine. If no, you have a problem.

## Where the language model helps and where it damages the research process

Library research is the part of the writing workflow where LLMs are most easily integrated and most easily misused, because the appearance of having found sources is easy to produce and the reality of having read them is hard to fake from the outside.

Useful: keyword expansion. The model is good at suggesting search terms you have not thought of. Use the suggestions to populate database searches, then verify the results yourself. The model is generating vocabulary, not retrieving literature.

Useful: source triage. Paste an abstract and ask whether the source is likely to be useful for your research question. The model often gives a reasonable assessment in seconds. This is a time-saving first filter, not a substitute for reading.

Useful: note-taking organization. After you have read a source, dictate or type your notes to the model and ask it to help organize them into the three-component log structure. The thinking is yours; the model formats it.

Useful: citation formatting. The model is reasonably good at converting citations between MLA, APA, and Chicago. Verify against the official style guide before submitting anything.

Damaging: asking the model to find sources. The model will invent plausible citations. Use it for keyword generation, then run the keywords in actual databases. The fabrication problem is not marginal; it is structural. The model does not distinguish between citations it retrieved and citations it generated. You cannot tell from the output which is which.

Damaging: asking the model to read your sources for you. The model can summarize an abstract or article paragraph you paste into the conversation. It cannot read PDFs you have not given it. If you ask the model about a source you have not shared with it, you will get an answer based on training data, which may or may not match the actual source.

Damaging: outsourcing synthesis. The model can produce synthesis-shaped prose without doing the synthesis work. Read every synthesis-like paragraph the model produces against your own developing thinking. If you cannot identify what the synthesis claim is, and why each source belongs in the paragraph, the paragraph is decorative.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 15

**What you're building:** the research log for your current project, structured for use through the whole research process.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat, with [Cowork](https://claude.ai/) for file management if you have many sources.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm conducting research for an academic paper. The topic is [name it]
and my tentative thesis is [state it].

I have read the following sources (citations and notes below).

Do the following, in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. For each source I've listed, suggest the three things that need
   to be in my research log: the source's specific claims I should
   record, the source's specific evidence I should record, and the
   source's position in the larger conversation.

2. Identify gaps in my source list. Where in the literature have I
   not yet looked? Suggest specific keywords or specific scholars I
   should search for. Do not invent citations — only suggest search
   directions.

3. For each source, identify how it relates to the others. Where do
   they reinforce each other? Where do they contradict? Where could
   the synthesis between two sources produce an argument neither
   makes alone?

4. Based on my tentative thesis and the source set, what is the
   single most important piece of evidence I am missing? What
   specific kind of source — primary research, statistical data, an
   opposing scholarly voice — would most strengthen the paper?

Do not draft the paper. Do not produce a thesis. The research is mine.

Here are my sources:
[paste source list with brief notes]
```

**What this produces:** a structured research log, an evidence-gap analysis, and a clear direction for the next round of source-finding. After this, you continue the research yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For Cowork:* if your sources are in separate PDFs, have Cowork read them before the conversation. Cowork can also maintain the running research log as a file you update across sessions.
- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool for the conversation, but useful for managing the file structure of a longer research project.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when you ask the model to suggest gaps in your source list, it will sometimes name specific scholars who do not exist or attribute specific positions to scholars who did not take them. Treat every named scholar as a lead to verify, not a citation to use.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter walks through building an annotated bibliography — turning your research log into the kind of document that demonstrates your research credibility to an academic reader.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Zora Neale Hurston** was working on *Mules and Men* — a field-research project that documented Black Southern folklore on its own terms, decades before participatory anthropology was a recognized method and decades before most people had heard of the research process as a craft. Here's a prompt to find out more, and then make it better.

**Run this:**

```
Who was Zora Neale Hurston, and how does their work on ethnographic
field research and the work of recording a community in its own voice
connect to the research process — accessing and recording information
from real sources? Keep it to three paragraphs. End with the single
most surprising thing about their career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"Zora Neale Hurston"** on Wikipedia after you run this. See what the model got right, got wrong, or left out.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it to describe the specific scene in *Mules and Men* where Hurston enters a Florida turpentine camp, and what she did differently than her Columbia anthropology training would have prescribed.
- Ask: "Hurston's anthropology training came from Franz Boas. What did she keep, and what did she throw out, when she did her own fieldwork?"
- Add the constraint: "Answer using at least one direct quotation from *Mules and Men* — Hurston's voice on the page."

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. Set a timer for two minutes. Using only your memory and your understanding of your research topic, generate a keyword list: write every word, phrase, and synonym you expect to find in strong sources on your topic. Then run three of those keywords in two different databases. Note which results overlap and which are unique to one database. *(Tests: the keyword-generation-before-searching discipline; experiencing the value of using multiple databases.)*

2. Take any source you have already found for any assignment. Run it through all five CRAAP dimensions — Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose — in writing, one sentence per dimension. Then identify which of the three things CRAAP misses might apply to this source: institutional bias, author network effects, or settled framing on a contested question. *(Tests: applying the evaluation framework in full rather than selectively; developing awareness of what CRAAP does not catch.)*

3. Locate the bibliography of any strong academic source you have already found. Identify two works in that bibliography you have not yet read that seem relevant to your research question. This is citation tracing. Record both works with full citations. *(Tests: citation tracing as a deliberate technique rather than an accidental discovery.)*

**Application**

4. Build a research log entry for one source you have already read for your current project. It must have all three components: source information (full citation, URL or DOI, access date, reliability assessment); specific claims and evidence (at least one direct quote with page number, at least one paraphrase); synthesis notes (where this source fits in your developing argument, which other sources it agrees with or contradicts, what question it leaves unanswered). Compare what you had in your notes before this exercise to what you have now. What did the structured format force you to add? *(Tests: full application of the three-component log structure to your own research; developing awareness of what ad-hoc notes typically omit.)*

5. You are planning a short field research component for your paper — one interview, one observation, or a brief survey of five to ten people. Write: the specific question your field research will answer that your library research cannot; the method you will use and why; the permission you will need to obtain; and whether IRB review applies at your institution for this type of project. If you are not using field research, write a paragraph explaining why library research alone is sufficient for your specific question. *(Tests: deliberate choice between library and field research methods; applying the ethical requirements for field research before beginning.)*

6. Write one paragraph of your research paper that synthesizes two sources — using paraphrase for both, not direct quotation. Then identify: the synthesis claim your paragraph is making; whether the claim is supported by both sources or by one more than the other; and what a reader would learn from your paragraph that they would not learn from reading either source alone. If your paragraph does not produce something neither source says alone, revise until it does. *(Tests: paraphrase as the workhorse of research writing; the synthesis discipline applied to original prose.)*

7. Identify one moment in your developing research where you are asserting a claim without yet having evidence to support it. Name the specific kind of source — primary research, statistical data, an opposing scholarly position, a case study — that would provide that evidence. Then run a database search using the most specific keywords you can generate for that gap. Record what you find, even if what you find is that the evidence does not yet exist. *(Tests: identifying evidentiary gaps before drafting; using the gap as a search direction rather than a stopping point.)*

**Synthesis**

8. Build the complete research log for your project as it currently stands — every source you have read, with all three components for each. Then write a one-paragraph synthesis map: which sources reinforce each other, which contradict, which have the potential to produce synthesis arguments none of them makes alone. The synthesis map is not the paper. It is the argument the paper will build from. *(Tests: full integration of the log discipline across a multi-source project; the synthesis map as a pre-draft planning document.)*

9. Write the introduction to your research paper — the section that establishes stakes, names the dual problem or contested terrain, and states your thesis. Cite at least two sources from your log in the introduction itself. After writing it, check each citation against your log: is it doing argumentative work, or is it decorative? If decorative, either revise the introduction or revise your judgment about what the citation is doing. *(Tests: citation discipline under the pressure of actual drafting; distinguishing argumentative citation from ornamental citation in your own prose.)*

**Challenge**

10. Find a source you initially assessed as strong using the CRAAP framework. Now run it against the three CRAAP misses: check the journal's editorial board for institutional perspective, look at the author's acknowledgements and bibliography for network effects, and find one scholar who would disagree with the source's framing of the question. Write a 300-word reassessment. Has your confidence in the source gone up, down, or stayed the same — and why? *(Open-ended; extends the CRAAP-misses analysis into a genuine re-evaluation of a source the student already holds; the quality of the response depends on the specificity of what the student found.)*

If careful tracking of student research projects showed that students who used structured research logs produced papers no stronger than students who used ad-hoc note-taking — that the log discipline was overhead without payoff — I would revise the chapter's central emphasis on the log. The evidence from teaching points the other way: students who keep logs produce stronger papers and lose less work along the way. But the claim should remain testable.

## Still unresolved

The relationship between research process and the actual quality of thinking is more complex than the chapter suggests. Some students with messy, ad-hoc research processes produce wonderful papers because their thinking is strong. Some students with immaculate research logs produce mechanical papers because the log organized data but did not produce insight. The log helps. It does not substitute for thinking. I do not yet know how to teach the difference cleanly, and I am not sure it is teachable in a textbook section at all.
