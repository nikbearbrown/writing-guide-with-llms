# Chapter 15 — The Research Process

*How to Find What You Don't Yet Know You're Looking For.*

A scene that will be familiar.

You sit down with a topic and a deadline. You open a tab, type some words into a search engine, get back ten million results, click the third or fourth one, read it for five minutes, lose track of what you were looking for, follow a link to something tangentially interesting, lose more time, look at the clock, panic gently, and start again with slightly different keywords.

This is most students' research process. It is the process of someone who has not been shown that *research* is a craft with specific techniques, ethical constraints, and recordable structure. The chapter ahead is about converting the ad-hoc search into something deliberate enough to produce a paper.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is to build a *research log* — a structured record of the sources you have gathered, what each one contributes to your argument, and how they relate to each other and to the position you are developing. The log is a tool. It exists so that, three weeks into a research project, you know what you have read and what you still need to find. Without it, the average student loses about a third of their early research before drafting.

## The two kinds of research

There are two broad approaches to academic research, and the strong argumentative research papers usually combine them.

**Library research** is research into what others have already discovered, written, and published. It is the work most students think of when they hear *research*: finding journal articles, books, news reports, government data, archived documents, well-curated websites. The library is broader than the building — the *library* in this sense includes every database and digital archive your institution provides access to, and a substantial amount of high-quality material freely available online.

**Field research** is research you conduct yourself by observing, interviewing, surveying, or measuring. It produces *primary sources* — original data nobody else has collected on this question. Field research is harder than library research for most student projects, but it can be the most important contribution of a strong paper because it produces evidence the existing literature does not yet have.

Most student research projects use predominantly library research. Some projects benefit substantially from a small amount of field research — a few interviews, a short observation, a brief survey — added to the library work. Some topics demand more field research than library research. The choice is topic-dependent.

## Library research — the specific moves

A useful sequence.

**Generate keywords.** Before you search, list the words and phrases you expect to find in good sources on your topic. Include synonyms. Include words that mean roughly the same thing but get used in different fields. *Food sustainability* and *food security* mean slightly different things; both are useful search terms; the literature using one term is sometimes disjoint from the literature using the other.

**Use multiple databases.** Single-database research produces single-database results. The same query in JSTOR, Google Scholar, Web of Science, and a discipline-specific database will produce overlapping but importantly different result sets. Your institution's library website probably gives you access to all of these and others. Use at least three.

**Read what you find with two passes.** First pass: skim the abstract or executive summary, look at the conclusion, check the bibliography. Decide whether the source is worth reading in full. Second pass: full read of the sources that passed the first filter. Most students try to do both passes at once and end up doing neither well.

**Follow the citation trails.** Every good academic source has a bibliography. The works it cites are themselves leads — the bibliography of a strong recent article will point you to the older work that source is engaging with. This *citation tracing* is one of the most productive research techniques and one of the least taught.

**Save everything, in a structured way.** When you find a source, record the full citation, the URL or DOI, the date you accessed it, your initial assessment of its credibility, the specific claim or evidence you took from it, and where it fits in your developing argument. This becomes the research log.

## The CRAAP test — and what it actually catches

A widely taught source-evaluation framework goes by the acronym CRAAP: *Currency*, *Relevance*, *Authority*, *Accuracy*, *Purpose*.

**Currency.** When was the source published or updated? Is it current enough for the question you are researching? In fields that move quickly — AI, climate, technology — a five-year-old source may be substantially outdated. In other fields — Shakespeare scholarship, ancient history — a fifty-year-old source may still be authoritative.

**Relevance.** Does the source actually address your specific question, or is it on a related but different topic that you are tempted to treat as if it addressed your topic? The temptation is high when a source is high-quality but not on point. Resist it.

**Authority.** Who wrote it? What are their credentials? What is the publishing venue's reputation? Is the publisher a peer-reviewed journal, a major newspaper, a university press, a self-published blog? Each implies a different set of editorial controls.

**Accuracy.** Can the source's specific claims be verified against other sources? Does it cite its own evidence? Is the methodology, where relevant, defensible? Does it engage with the strongest counter-positions?

**Purpose.** Why was this source produced? To inform, to persuade, to sell, to advocate, to entertain? Each purpose carries its own bias. The bias is not disqualifying — a piece written to persuade can still be evidence — but the purpose has to be named and the resulting bias factored in.

CRAAP is a starting checklist. It catches most obvious source-quality problems. It does not catch every problem. Some specific things CRAAP misses.

*Institutional bias of journals.* Even peer-reviewed journals have editorial perspectives. The *Journal of Health Economics* and *Health Affairs* publish in overlapping but distinct positions on contested policy questions. CRAAP will not catch the difference. Reading the journal's editorial board and recent issues will.

*Author networks.* Some scholars cite each other heavily because they are friends or co-authors; their citation network may not represent the field. CRAAP does not see this. Reading the bibliographies and the acknowledgements does.

*The "settled" framing.* Some sources present contested questions as settled. The framing itself is a bias. CRAAP does not always catch this; reading the source against scholars who would disagree with it does.

## Field research — when and how

Some topics benefit from primary research you conduct yourself. A few specific methods.

**Observation.** You go somewhere — a classroom, a public square, a digital community — and take detailed notes on what you see. The notes have to distinguish *observation* (what you saw) from *interpretation* (what you think it meant). The discipline of separating the two in your notes is what makes observation a research method rather than a personal essay.

There are two postures: *participant observation*, where you participate in what you are observing (a researcher studying a youth basketball league by helping coach it), and *non-participant observation*, where you watch but do not participate. Each has trade-offs. Participation gives you access to information that an outsider cannot reach; non-participation preserves an analytical distance the participant has lost.

**Interviews.** You ask questions of one or more people and record their answers. The questions should be open-ended where possible — *describe a time when...*, *what was your experience of...* — rather than yes/no. The interview record should be either an audio recording (with permission) or careful written notes. Either way, you will need to transcribe key passages if you want to quote.

**Surveys.** You ask a number of people the same set of questions. Surveys produce comparable data across respondents. They also produce data that is only as good as the questions are clear. Survey design is its own craft. For a student project, three to ten well-crafted questions in a survey administered to a defined population usually beats a longer survey administered to whoever happens to respond.

Two specific notes for field research at the student scale.

*Get permission.* If you are interviewing people, observing them, or surveying them, ask their permission. Tell them what the research is for. Tell them how the data will be used. Let them refuse. Let them retract later if they want.

*Know whether you need IRB approval.* Institutional Review Boards exist to protect human research subjects. Most informal student-paper research falls below the threshold that requires IRB review, but check with your instructor or your institution. If your research involves children, prisoners, vulnerable populations, or potentially distressing topics, IRB review is usually required. The check is part of the research practice.

## The research log

The research log is the document that holds your work together. A simple, useful structure has three columns or sections per source.

**Column 1: Source information.** The full citation in the format you are using (MLA, APA, Chicago — whichever the assignment requires). The URL or DOI. The date you accessed it. Whether you have the full text or only an abstract. Your initial reliability assessment (peer-reviewed journal, major newspaper, etc.).

**Column 2: Specific claims and evidence.** What this source actually says that you might use. Direct quotes (with page numbers) for passages you might cite. Paraphrases (with page numbers) for claims you might restate in your own voice. Statistics or specific data points (with the source's own citations).

**Column 3: Synthesis notes.** Where this source fits in the argument you are developing. Which other sources it agrees with, contradicts, or extends. What questions it leaves unanswered. What it makes you think about that you had not thought about before.

A working example, condensed, for a paper on AI-detection software in college courses.

> **Source:** Sadasivan, Vinu et al. "Can AI-Generated Text Be Reliably Detected?" *arXiv preprint*, May 2023. https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11156. Accessed 12 Oct. 2024. Preprint; not yet peer-reviewed; authors at University of Maryland CS dept.
>
> **Claims/Evidence:** Authors test multiple AI-detection systems against multiple LLM outputs and adversarial rewrites. Find that paraphrasing attacks reduce detection accuracy substantially. "Even the best-known detectors of LLM-generated text can have their accuracy reduced... by simple paraphrasing attacks" (p. 2). Specific numbers: detection accuracy drops from 95% to ~50% after light paraphrasing.
>
> **Synthesis notes:** Strong evidence for the technical-failure case against AI detectors. Pairs with Liang et al. 2023 on biased false-positive rates for non-native English. Together: technical evidence that detectors are both inaccurate against motivated cheaters AND biased against innocent non-native speakers. This is the empirical spine of the position argument.

That format takes more time than dumping URLs into a notes file. It saves time later by miles. The discipline pays off when, three weeks into a project, you can find what you need without re-reading everything.

## The plagiarism question — and what it is becoming in 2026

*Plagiarism*, in the traditional academic sense, is presenting someone else's work as your own. It can be deliberate (copying a paper, paying for a paper, paying someone to write a paper) or unintentional (paraphrasing a source without citing, copying a key phrase without quotation marks, summarizing without attribution).

The traditional fix: cite everything that is not yours. When you are summarizing a source's ideas, cite. When you are paraphrasing a passage, cite. When you are quoting a phrase, use quotation marks and cite. When in doubt, cite.

The 2026 question is what *plagiarism* now means when the source is a language model.

The honest current answer: there is not yet a stable convention. Some institutions treat LLM use as plagiarism unconditionally; some treat it as plagiarism only when the LLM-generated text is presented as the student's own without disclosure; some treat it as permissible with disclosure. The practical advice: *be transparent*. If you used an LLM to brainstorm, draft, edit, or interrogate your work, say so somewhere — in a footnote, a cover note, an acknowledgements section. The disclosure protects you. It also makes the LLM's contribution part of the work rather than a hidden ingredient.

Some specific moves that are clearly problematic:

- Submitting LLM-generated text as if it were your own writing.
- Asking an LLM to paraphrase a source for you, then citing the source as if you had read it.
- Using LLM-generated text as evidence in an argument that requires the evidence to be your own analysis.

Some specific moves that are usually fine:

- Using an LLM to help brainstorm topics, generate research questions, or organize an outline — and writing the paper yourself afterward.
- Using an LLM to identify gaps in your draft and revising the gaps yourself.
- Using an LLM to check your citations against the original sources you have read.
- Using an LLM as an interrogation partner that asks you questions about your work.

The line is fuzzier than I would like. The honest discipline is to ask, of each LLM use: *would I be comfortable telling my instructor exactly how I used the tool*? If yes, you are probably on safe ground. If no, you have a problem.

## A note about AI

The research process is the part of the writing workflow where LLMs are most easily integrated and most easily misused. A small map.

Useful integrations:

*Keyword expansion.* The model is good at suggesting search terms you have not thought of. Use the suggestions to populate database searches, then verify the results.

*Source triage.* Paste an abstract and ask whether the source is likely to be useful for your research question. The model often gives a reasonable assessment in seconds.

*Note-taking partner.* After you have read a source, dictate or type your notes to the model and ask it to help you organize them into the three-column structure. The structure is yours; the model formats it.

*Citation formatting.* The model is reasonably good at converting a citation between MLA, APA, and Chicago. Verify against the official style guide for anything you are submitting.

Damaging integrations:

*Asking the model to find sources.* The model will invent plausible citations. Use the model for keyword generation, then run the keywords in actual databases.

*Asking the model to read your sources for you.* The model can summarize an abstract or article paragraph it has been given. It cannot read PDFs you haven't given it. If you ask the model about a source you haven't shared with it, you will get an answer based on training data, which may or may not match the actual source.

*Outsourcing your synthesis.* The model can produce synthesis-shaped prose without doing the synthesis work. Read every synthesis-like paragraph the model produces against your own developing thinking.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 15

**What you're building:** the research log for your current project, structured for use through the whole research process.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat, with [Cowork](https://claude.ai/) for file management if you have many sources.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm conducting research for an academic paper. The topic is [name it]
and my tentative thesis is [state it].

I have read the following sources (citations and notes below).

Do the following, in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. For each source I've listed, suggest the three things that need
   to be in my research log: the source's specific claims I should
   record, the source's specific evidence I should record, and the
   source's position in the larger conversation.

2. Identify gaps in my source list. Where in the literature have I
   not yet looked? Suggest specific keywords or specific scholars I
   should search for. Do not invent citations — only suggest search
   directions.

3. For each source, identify how it relates to the others. Where do
   they reinforce each other? Where do they contradict? Where could
   the synthesis between two sources produce an argument neither
   makes alone?

4. Based on my tentative thesis and the source set, what is the
   single most important piece of evidence I am missing? What
   specific kind of source — primary research, statistical data, an
   opposing scholarly voice — would most strengthen the paper?

Do not draft the paper. Do not produce a thesis. The research is mine.

Here are my sources:
[paste source list with brief notes]
```

**What this produces:** a structured research log, an evidence-gap analysis, and a clear direction for the next round of source-finding. After this, you continue the research yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For Cowork:* if your sources are in separate PDFs, have Cowork read them before the conversation. Cowork can also maintain the running research log as a file you update across sessions.
- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool for the conversation, but useful for managing the file structure of a longer research project.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when you ask the model to suggest gaps in your source list, it will sometimes name specific scholars who do not exist or attribute specific positions to scholars who did not take them. Treat every named scholar as a lead to verify, not a citation to use.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter walks through the formal process of building an annotated bibliography — turning your research log into the kind of document that demonstrates your research credibility to an academic reader.

## What would change my mind

If careful tracking of student research projects showed that students who used structured research logs produced papers no stronger than students who used ad-hoc note-taking — that the log discipline was overhead without payoff — I would revise the chapter's central emphasis on the log. The evidence from teaching is the other way: students who keep logs produce stronger papers and lose less work along the way. But the claim should remain testable.

## Still puzzling

The relationship between research process and the actual quality of thinking is more complex than the chapter suggests. Some students with messy, ad-hoc research processes produce wonderful papers because their thinking is strong. Some students with immaculate research logs produce mechanical papers because the log organized data but did not produce insight. The log helps; it does not substitute for thinking. I do not yet know how to teach the difference cleanly.
