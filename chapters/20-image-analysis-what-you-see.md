# Chapter 20 — Image Analysis

*Reading What You Cannot Read.*

A specific image to begin.

In 1918, a thirty-five-year-old painter named Charles Demuth, working in a watercolor-and-graphite technique he had been refining for years, produced a painting called *Dancing Sailors*. The painting shows three pairs of sailors dancing, possibly at a bar, possibly in a club, in a configuration of bodies the period would not have publicly acknowledged was happening. The painting hangs now in the Cleveland Museum of Art.[^1]

[^1]: Charles Demuth, *Dancing Sailors* (1918), Cleveland Museum of Art. See the [museum's catalogue entry](https://www.clevelandart.org/art/1956.85) and Jonathan Weinberg's monograph *Speaking for Vice: Homosexuality in the Art of Charles Demuth, Marsden Hartley, and the First American Avant-Garde* (Yale UP, 1993).

The painting does not tell you what it is. The painting *is*. Three pairs of sailors. White uniforms. A muted background. Specific gestures. A specific configuration of weight and gaze. The viewer looks. The viewer infers. The viewer asks: what is this image showing me, what is it doing, and how does it do it?

Those are the questions image analysis asks.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is your own analysis of a specific image. Approximately 1,500 to 2,500 words. The image can be a painting, a photograph, a still from a film, a piece of advertising, a public monument, a meme, an infographic. Anything visual. The question — *what is this image doing and how does it do it* — applies to all of them.

## What image analysis is, and what it isn't

*Image analysis* is the application of the close-reading discipline you developed in Chapter 19 to images instead of text. The skill is real. The vocabulary is different.

What image analysis is not:

- *Describing an image*. Description names what is in the frame. Analysis interprets what the frame is doing.
- *Saying what an image makes you feel*. Personal response is an input to analysis. It is not the analysis itself. The analysis is what *produces* the response, and the analysis is grounded in what is visible in the image.
- *Saying what the artist intended*. As in text analysis, the question is what the image does, not what the maker meant. Intent is sometimes recoverable from context; the image's effects are recoverable from looking.
- *Making the image about you*. Some image-analysis essays slide into autobiography. That can work occasionally — a memoir about an image — but the genre as taught here keeps the image as the primary object.

The genre is older than it might seem. Art historians have been analyzing images for centuries. Film scholars have been analyzing moving images since the early twentieth century. The student version of the genre uses some of the same vocabulary, calibrated to the scale of the assignment.

## What you look at when you look at an image

A vocabulary, calibrated to what an undergraduate analysis can do.

**Point of view.** Where is the maker positioning the viewer? A photograph taken from above implies one relationship; from below, another; eye-level, another. The same scene shot from three different positions tells three different stories. In film, *camera angle* is the technical term — high angle, low angle, level, Dutch tilt. In painting and photography, the maker's chosen viewpoint is doing the same kind of work.

**Composition and arrangement.** Where in the frame are the major elements? A subject placed in the center reads differently than a subject placed at the edge. Heavy mass on one side of the frame creates one kind of balance or imbalance; symmetric arrangement creates another. The classic *rule of thirds* — placing key elements one-third in from the frame edge — produces specific visual effects. Triangular compositions create different visual weight than rectangular or scattered ones.

**Color.** What colors dominate, what colors are absent, what colors are surprising? Color carries cultural meaning, but the meaning varies — red is celebratory in Chinese contexts and mourning-adjacent in some African ones; white is wedding-purity in Western Christian tradition and mourning in Buddhist and Hindu tradition. The analyst names the color choices and considers their effects without assuming universal meaning.

**Light.** Where is light coming from? Hard or soft? Frontal, side, back? Light shapes the emotional register of an image. A photograph with hard frontal flash reads very differently from one with soft side-light from a window. Painters spend careers learning to render light; analysts spend essays interpreting what it does.

**Symbol.** What objects, gestures, or visual configurations carry meaning beyond their literal presence? An open Bible in a portrait is not just a book. A bouquet of white lilies on a casket is not just flowers. The symbols are culturally specific. The analyst names them and interprets them in context.

**Juxtaposition.** What is placed next to what? Two elements placed in proximity produce meaning their separate existence would not. A diptych of two photographs — one of a person; one of a landscape — produces a relation between the two the viewer is asked to construct.

**Line.** Curves, diagonals, horizontals, verticals. Each carries different visual weight. Hard right angles read formally; curves read softly. Diagonal lines create motion; horizontals create stability.

**Texture.** Smooth, rough, scratchy, glossy. The texture of a painted surface is part of its meaning. The texture of a photograph (film grain, smooth digital, blurred motion) is part of its meaning.

These are the elements. They do not have to be addressed exhaustively in every analysis. You pick the ones doing the most work in your specific image and read them closely.

## Description, reflection, analysis — the three moves

A useful distinction.

**Description** names what is in the image. *Three pairs of sailors. White uniforms with dark trim. A muted blue-green background. Each sailor in close physical contact with their partner.* Description is the foundation. Without it, the rest cannot work.

**Reflection** names what the image evokes in the viewer. *The painting produces a sense of intimate informality that feels furtive — like the viewer is seeing something not meant to be openly observed.* Reflection is your honest response. It is real evidence, but it is one viewer's response, and it has to be calibrated against what other viewers might respond.

**Analysis** explains how the image produces its effects. *The arrangement of the sailors — close, in pairs, with weight distributed between them rather than separated — creates the intimacy. The neutral background isolates the pairs as the focal subject. The 1918 historical context, when same-sex public affection was not openly representable, sharpens the sense that the painting is doing something the period would not have permitted in writing.* Analysis is the work the essay is asking you to do.

A common student failure is to confuse the three. The essay that is mostly description is not yet analytical. The essay that is mostly reflection is personal rather than analytical. The strong image analysis moves through description, into reflection, into analysis, with each layer doing its own work.

## Context — what the image cannot tell you alone

An image rarely interprets itself entirely from inside the frame. The historical, cultural, and biographical context shapes what the image means.

A specific case. *Dancing Sailors* read in 1918 was read by viewers who could see the homoerotic register clearly but could not name it openly. The painting's meaning was suspended in the gap between what could be shown and what could not be said. Read in 2024, the painting is read by viewers who can name the register openly. The painting is the same. The meaning is different.

This is not a paradox. It is how images work in time. The image carries its formal features forward; the cultural moment of reception assigns meaning to those features. The analyst writes from a specific moment — your moment, 2026 — and the writing has to acknowledge that your reading is a reading from this moment.

Some specific kinds of context that matter.

**The maker.** Who made the image? When? Under what circumstances? Charles Demuth, in 1918, was a gay man in early-twentieth-century America whose paintings often explored homoerotic themes in coded form. The painting is read differently with that biographical context than without it.

**The medium and circumstances of production.** A painting commissioned by a wealthy patron in the seventeenth century operates under different constraints than a photograph taken by an unknown documentarian in 1937 or a contemporary digital image posted to Instagram. The medium and circumstances of production shape what the image could be.

**The reception history.** How has the image been read over time? *Dancing Sailors* was largely unread by mainstream art history for decades; specific 1990s scholarship (Jonathan Weinberg's *Speaking for Vice*, 1993) recovered the painting's coded register. The reception history is part of the image's life.

**The public situation.** A monument in a public square is doing work different from a painting in a museum. The image's context is partly its physical and institutional location.

For your analysis, the context section does not have to be exhaustive. You name what matters for your reading. The rest goes in your notes.

## A common case — the public monument

Public monuments are interesting because they are visual objects with explicit political functions. A statue in a city square is, almost always, an argument. The argument is in the figure represented, the pedestal, the inscription, the location, the surrounding architecture, the gestures.

A worked case. From 1939 to 2020, the American Museum of Natural History in New York City had on its front entrance plaza an equestrian statue of Theodore Roosevelt, designed by James Earle Fraser. Roosevelt sat mounted on a horse. Walking beside the horse, on either side and below Roosevelt's eye level, were two figures: a Native American man and an African man. Neither was named. Neither was mounted. The composition was a hierarchy in stone.

The statue stood for eighty-one years. It was removed in 2020 — after extensive public debate, formal recommendations from the museum's own commission, and a vote by the museum's board.

An image analysis of this monument could examine: the compositional hierarchy (Roosevelt elevated, the named individual; the unnamed figures subordinate); the choice to render the unnamed figures as racial *types* rather than as portraits; the placement of the statue on the museum's central entrance, signaling the museum's institutional values; the eighty-one year history of its acceptance; the public debate that led to its removal; what the empty plinth that replaced it now means.

The monument as image is doing rhetorical work. The analysis names the work and locates it in time. The removal is itself an image — an absence is also a composed visual fact.

## A worked example, condensed

A student image analysis of *Dancing Sailors*, in roughly the proportions a 1,800-word essay would use.

*Introduction (200 words).* Names the painting, the painter, the year, the museum. Briefly contextualizes Demuth's work in early twentieth-century American art and his sustained engagement with subjects coded as homoerotic. Introduces the analytical question: what does the painting's formal composition do that the verbal culture of 1918 could not yet do?

*Description (300 words).* The painting itself. Three pairs of sailors. White uniforms. The muted blue-green background. The specific arrangement — pair 1, pair 2, pair 3, roughly equally spaced. The hand placement. The angle of the heads. The lack of clearly defined facial features in some figures. The watercolor-and-graphite medium and what it allows.

*Context (200 words).* Demuth's biography. The Provincetown and Paris circles. The presence of homoerotic subject matter in his work without explicit verbal labeling. The 1918 cultural situation — Comstock laws still active, same-sex relations criminalized, no published vocabulary for what the painting shows.

*Analysis (700 words).* The formal moves. The arrangement creates equivalence among the three pairs — none is foregrounded, none is the central pair; the viewer reads them as a series rather than a hierarchy. The intimacy of the pairings is visible in the specific gestures — hand placement, body angle, weight distribution. The neutral background isolates the pairs as the subject; there is no narrative context to dilute the social fact of the dancing. The watercolor medium produces softness — hard edges would have rendered the bodies as separate; the soft edges let them blend into each other.

The painting is doing what the period's verbal culture could not. It is showing same-sex dancing as ordinary, repeated, configured, and worth depicting. The compositional choices make the showing legible without saying what is being shown.

*Engagement with other readings (200 words).* Weinberg's 1993 reading. The earlier critical silence on the painting. Subsequent feminist and queer scholarship. The analyst's reading sits in this lineage.

*Conclusion (150 words).* What the close reading has produced. The painting as an early instance of a visual argument that the language of the period could not yet make. What the painting's reception history teaches about how images outlast their cultural moment.

The structure is teachable. The proportions vary by image. Some images need more description (an abstract painting where the description is doing the work); some need more context (a historically situated photograph); some need more analysis. The default is roughly one third description, one third analysis, one third everything else.

## A note about AI

Image analysis in 2026 has been complicated by the existence of large vision-language models that can themselves analyze images.

Useful:

*Description partner.* Show the image to a multimodal LLM and ask it to describe what it sees in detail. The model will produce a description. Use it as a starting point. Compare the model's description to your own. Where the model saw something you did not, look again. Where you saw something the model did not, your specific perception is doing work.

*Pattern identification.* Ask the model to identify recurring formal elements — repeated colors, repeated shapes, compositional patterns. The model is reasonably good at this.

*Context location.* Ask the model what is known about the image's history, the artist's biography, the critical reception. Verify everything against actual sources before citing.

Damaging:

*Outsourcing the interpretation.* The model will produce an interpretation if asked. The interpretation will average the readings the model has seen. It will not be the reading produced by *your* specific encounter with the image.

*Trusting the model's cultural-symbol claims.* The model will sometimes claim that a color or symbol means a specific thing universally. The claim is almost always too strong; cultural meanings are local and contested. Verify against actual cultural-history scholarship.

*Asking the model to produce the image.* If the assignment is to analyze a specific extant image, the model cannot help by generating one. The image's history, context, and cultural location are part of what the analysis is engaging with.

A specific 2026 caution: image generation tools (DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, others) produce images that *look like* images with the cultural meaning of human-made images, but that do not have the production context that human-made images do. A textual analysis of an LLM-generated image is a different genre than a textual analysis of *Dancing Sailors* — the cultural meaning, the production context, the reception history are all different. If you choose to analyze an LLM-generated image, the analysis has to engage explicitly with that difference.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 20

**What you're building:** the analytical scaffold for your image analysis — description, contextual research, formal patterns, interpretive thesis — before drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. Multimodal capability required — show the image to Claude.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word image analysis for a college writing
class. I'm pasting the image (or providing a link to it) below.

The image is: [name it — title, artist, year if known]

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Describe the image in full detail. Inventory everything visible —
   figures, objects, composition, colors, light, gestures. Do not
   yet interpret. Do not yet contextualize.

2. Identify three or four formal patterns or choices that are
   doing the most visual work in the image — patterns of repetition,
   compositional balance, light, color, or any specific feature.
   For each, quote where in the image it appears.

3. Ask me four questions, one at a time, about the historical or
   cultural context I know or have researched on this image.

4. Based on my answers and your observations, propose a one-sentence
   interpretive claim that names what the image is doing and how
   the formal choices accomplish it.

5. Construct the strongest counter-interpretation. Where could
   another viewer, looking at the same image, reach a different
   reading? What evidence in the image supports that counter-reading?

Do not write the analysis. Do not draft the introduction. The
interpretation is mine.
```

**What this produces:** a thorough description, a formal-pattern inventory, the contextual specifics, a working interpretive claim, and a counter-interpretation. After this, you draft the analysis yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written; both have multimodal capability.
- *For Cowork:* useful if you want to keep the image alongside developing notes.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model will sometimes confidently claim a specific cultural meaning for a color, gesture, or symbol that is more contested than the model lets on. *Where does this meaning come from? Is it universal or specific to a tradition?* The verification is part of the analytical work.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter moves from analyzing a single image to composing multimodal texts that combine images and words. The skills you have practiced in textual and image analysis become the skills you bring to making rather than analyzing.

## What would change my mind

If sustained study showed that students who used multimodal LLMs to assist with image analysis produced no stronger interpretations than students who worked from their own observation alone — that the AI assistance averaged the analysis toward standard readings without producing the close-looking discipline the genre rewards — I would revise the chapter's openness to using the tools. The early evidence is mixed: the tools are useful for description and context, weaker for interpretation. The pedagogy here is to use the tools for their strengths and resist them for the central interpretive work.

## Still puzzling

The cultural meaning of color, symbol, and gesture is more contested than the chapter pretends. The advice I have offered — *name the cultural specificity, do not assume universal meaning* — is right but it is also harder to operationalize than the chapter admits. Students reading non-Western art often default to Western-tradition symbol meanings without realizing they are doing so. The fix is to ground specific claims in cultural-history scholarship, which is harder than asking the LLM and harder than I have made it sound. I do not have a clean teaching solution for this yet.
