# Chapter 20 — Image Analysis

*Reading What You Cannot Read.*

---

In 1918, a thirty-five-year-old painter named Charles Demuth produced a painting called *Dancing Sailors*. The painting shows three pairs of sailors dancing — possibly at a bar, possibly in a club — in a configuration of bodies the period would not have publicly acknowledged was happening. The painting hangs now in the Cleveland Museum of Art.[^1]

[^1]: Charles Demuth, *Dancing Sailors* (1918), Cleveland Museum of Art. See the [museum's catalogue entry](https://www.clevelandart.org/art/1956.85) and Jonathan Weinberg, *Speaking for Vice: Homosexuality in the Art of Charles Demuth, Marsden Hartley, and the First American Avant-Garde* (Yale UP, 1993).

The painting does not tell you what it is. It *is*. Three pairs of sailors. White uniforms. A muted background. Specific gestures. A specific configuration of weight and gaze. The viewer looks. The viewer infers. The viewer asks: what is this image showing me, what is it doing, and how does it do it?

Those are the questions image analysis asks.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is your own analysis of a specific image, 1,500 to 2,500 words. The image can be a painting, a photograph, a still from a film, a piece of advertising, a public monument, a meme, an infographic. Anything visual. The question — *what is this image doing, and how does it do it* — applies to all of them.

## What image analysis is — and what makes it hard

Image analysis is the application of the close-reading discipline to images instead of text. The skill is real. The vocabulary is different.

The hardest part of the genre is not the vocabulary. It is a discipline that description of the image-analysis task usually understates: you have to stay in the analytical layer. This sounds straightforward. It is not.

The essay that is mostly description — naming what is in the frame — is not yet analytical. Description is the foundation; it is not the analysis. The essay that is mostly reflection — what the image makes you feel — is personal rather than analytical. Personal response is real evidence, but it is one viewer's response, and the work of analysis is to explain what *produces* the response, grounded in what is actually visible. The essay that claims to know what the artist intended is another failure mode; the question is what the image does, not what the maker meant.

The discipline is to move through description — what is in the image — into reflection — what it evokes — into analysis — what formal choices produce the effect. Each layer does its own work. The analysis explains the reflection. The description makes the analysis checkable.

## The elements — what to look at when you look

A vocabulary for what you are looking at, calibrated to what an undergraduate analysis can do.

*Point of view* is where the maker positions the viewer. A photograph taken from above implies one relationship; from below, another; eye-level, another. The same scene from three different angles tells three different stories. In film this is camera angle; in painting and still photography the maker's chosen viewpoint is doing the same work.

*Composition and arrangement* is where in the frame the major elements sit. A subject placed in the center reads differently than a subject placed at the edge. Heavy mass on one side creates imbalance; symmetric arrangement creates something else. The classical rule of thirds places key elements one-third in from the frame edge; the violation of that rule places them somewhere else, and the violation is itself a choice.

*Color* — what colors dominate, what are absent, what are surprising. Color carries cultural meaning, but the meaning varies. Red is celebratory in Chinese contexts; mourning-adjacent in others. White is wedding-purity in Western Christian tradition; mourning in Buddhist and Hindu tradition. The analyst names the color choices and considers their effects without assuming universal meaning. The assumption of universality is the error.

*Light* — where it comes from, whether it is hard or soft, frontal or side or back. Hard frontal flash reads very differently from soft side-light through a window. Painters spend careers learning to render light; analysts spend essays interpreting what it does.

*Symbol* — objects, gestures, or configurations that carry meaning beyond their literal presence. An open Bible in a portrait is not just a book. A bouquet of white lilies at a funeral is not just flowers. The symbols are culturally specific. The analyst names them in context.

*Juxtaposition* — what is placed next to what. Two elements placed in proximity produce meaning their separate existence does not. A diptych of a person and a landscape produces a relation between them the viewer is asked to construct.

*Line* — curves, diagonals, horizontals, verticals. Hard right angles read formally; curves read softly. Diagonal lines create motion; horizontals create stability.

*Texture* — smooth, rough, scratchy, glossy. The texture of a painted surface is part of its meaning. The texture of a photograph — film grain, smooth digital, blurred motion — is part of its meaning.

These elements do not all need to appear in every analysis. You identify the ones doing the most work in your specific image and read those closely. The ones doing the least work in a given image are not worth extended attention.

## Context — what the image cannot tell you alone

An image rarely interprets itself entirely from inside the frame. The historical, cultural, and biographical context shapes what the image means.

A specific case. *Dancing Sailors* read in 1918 was read by viewers who could see the homoerotic register clearly but could not name it openly. The painting's meaning was suspended in the gap between what could be shown and what could not be said. Read in 2026, the painting is read by viewers who can name the register without hesitation. The painting is the same. The reading is different.

This is not a paradox. It is how images work in time. The image carries its formal features forward; the cultural moment of reception assigns meaning to those features. The analyst writes from a specific moment — this moment, your moment — and the analysis has to acknowledge that the reading is a reading from here, not from everywhere.

Some specific contexts that matter.

Who made the image. Charles Demuth, in 1918, was a gay man in early-twentieth-century America whose paintings often explored homoerotic themes in coded form. The painting is read differently with that biographical context than without it. Not differently because the context changes what is in the frame, but because the context clarifies what the formal choices were doing.

The medium and circumstances of production. A painting commissioned by a wealthy patron in the seventeenth century operates under different constraints than a photograph taken by an unknown documentarian in 1937 or an Instagram post in 2024. The medium and circumstances shape what the image could be and what it could not.

The reception history. How has the image been read over time? *Dancing Sailors* was largely unread by mainstream art history for decades. Jonathan Weinberg's 1993 scholarship, *Speaking for Vice*, recovered the painting's coded register. The critical silence before 1993 is itself evidence — the silence was what the period could do with what Demuth showed. The reception history is part of the image's life.

The physical and institutional location. A monument in a public square is doing work different from a painting in a museum. The museum wall and the public plaza are different arguments. The image's context is partly where it sits.

## A specific case — the public monument

Public monuments are interesting for image analysis because they are visual objects with explicit political functions. A statue in a city square is almost always an argument. The argument is in the figure represented, the pedestal, the inscription, the location, the surrounding architecture, the gestures.

From 1939 to 2020, the American Museum of Natural History in New York City had at its front entrance an equestrian statue of Theodore Roosevelt, designed by James Earle Fraser. Roosevelt sat mounted on a horse. Walking beside the horse, on either side and below Roosevelt's eye level, were two figures: a Native American man and an African man. Neither was named. Neither was mounted. The composition was a hierarchy in bronze.

The statue stood for eighty-one years. It was removed in 2020 after public debate, formal recommendations from the museum's own commission, and a board vote.

An image analysis of this monument has specific things to engage. The compositional hierarchy — Roosevelt elevated and named, the other figures subordinate and anonymous — is doing specific rhetorical work. The choice to render the unnamed figures as racial types rather than as portraits is a specific formal decision. The placement at the museum's main entrance signals what the institution considered its front face. The eighty-one-year history of acceptance is context. The public debate that led to removal is context. The empty plinth that now stands there is itself an image — an absence is also a composed visual fact.

What is this monument doing? The analysis names the work. The formal analysis names how the composition does it. The context places it in time.

## A worked example, condensed

A student image analysis of *Dancing Sailors*, in roughly the proportions a 1,800-word essay would use.

*Introduction* (roughly 200 words): names the painting, the painter, the year, the museum. Briefly contextualizes Demuth's work in early-twentieth-century American art and his sustained engagement with subjects coded as homoerotic. Introduces the analytical question: what does the painting's formal composition do that the verbal culture of 1918 could not yet do openly?

*Description* (roughly 300 words): the painting itself. Three pairs of sailors. White uniforms with dark trim. The muted blue-green background. The specific arrangement — three pairs, roughly equally spaced across the frame. The hand placement. The angle of the heads. The softened facial features in some figures. The watercolor-and-graphite medium and what it allows.

*Context* (roughly 200 words): Demuth's biography. The Provincetown and Paris circles. The sustained presence of homoerotic subject matter in his work without explicit verbal labeling. The 1918 cultural situation — same-sex relations criminalized, no published vocabulary for what the painting depicts.

*Analysis* (roughly 700 words): the formal moves. The arrangement creates equivalence among the three pairs — none is foregrounded, none is the central pair; the viewer reads them as a series rather than a hierarchy. The intimacy of the pairings is visible in specific gestures: hand placement, body angle, the distribution of weight between partners rather than held separately. The neutral background isolates the pairs as the subject; there is no narrative context to dilute the social fact of the dancing. The watercolor medium produces softness — hard edges would have rendered the bodies as separate objects; the soft edges let them blend at contact points.

The painting is doing what the period's verbal culture could not. It is showing same-sex dancing as ordinary, repeated, configured, and worth depicting. The compositional choices make the showing legible without saying what is being shown.

*Engagement with other readings* (roughly 200 words): Weinberg's 1993 reading. The earlier critical silence. Subsequent queer and feminist scholarship. The analyst's reading sits in this lineage — after it, informed by it, but not identical to it.

*Conclusion* (roughly 150 words): what the close reading has produced. The painting as an early instance of a visual argument that the language of the period could not yet make. What the painting's reception history teaches about how images outlast their cultural moment.

The proportions vary by image. Some images need more description — an abstract painting where the description carries the analytical burden. Some need more context — a historically situated documentary photograph. Some need more analysis. The default is roughly one third description and context, one third analysis, one third the rest. The one non-negotiable is that the analysis section earns what it claims.

## The 2026 complication — image generation and what it changes

Image analysis in 2026 has been complicated by the existence of tools that generate images.

DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, and similar tools produce images that look like photographs or paintings with the cultural weight of human-made images — but without the production context that gives human-made images their analytical richness. A generated image has no maker with a biography. It has no reception history. It has no commission context, no cultural moment that shaped what it could and could not show. The formal elements are present; the contextual substrate is absent or radically different.

An analysis of an LLM-generated image is not the same genre as an analysis of *Dancing Sailors*. If you choose to analyze a generated image, the analysis has to engage explicitly with what that difference means — not because generated images are unworthy of analysis, but because the analysis is of a different object, with different contextual coordinates.

A related complication: multimodal LLMs can themselves analyze images, and they can produce image-analysis prose on request. The prose will average the readings the model has seen. It will not be the reading produced by *your* specific encounter with the image. There are useful things to do with these tools in the service of image analysis; generating the interpretation is not one of them.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 20

**What you're building:** the analytical scaffold for your image analysis — description, contextual research, formal patterns, interpretive thesis — before drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. Multimodal capability required — show the image to Claude.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 1,500–2,500 word image analysis for a college writing
class. I'm pasting the image (or providing a link to it) below.

The image is: [name it — title, artist, year if known]

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my response
between steps.

1. Describe the image in full detail. Inventory everything visible —
   figures, objects, composition, colors, light, gestures. Do not
   yet interpret. Do not yet contextualize.

2. Identify three or four formal patterns or choices that are
   doing the most visual work in the image — patterns of repetition,
   compositional balance, light, color, or any specific feature.
   For each, quote where in the image it appears.

3. Ask me four questions, one at a time, about the historical or
   cultural context I know or have researched on this image.

4. Based on my answers and your observations, propose a one-sentence
   interpretive claim that names what the image is doing and how
   the formal choices accomplish it.

5. Construct the strongest counter-interpretation. Where could
   another viewer, looking at the same image, reach a different
   reading? What evidence in the image supports that counter-reading?

Do not write the analysis. Do not draft the introduction. The
interpretation is mine.
```

**What this produces:** a thorough description, a formal-pattern inventory, the contextual specifics, a working interpretive claim, and a counter-interpretation. After this, you draft the analysis yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written; both have multimodal capability.
- *For Cowork:* useful if you want to keep the image alongside developing notes.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** the model will sometimes confidently claim a specific cultural meaning for a color, gesture, or symbol that is more contested than the model lets on. *Where does this meaning come from? Is it universal or specific to a tradition?* The verification is part of the analytical work.

**Preview of next chapter:** the next chapter moves from analyzing a single image to composing multimodal texts that combine images and words. The skills practiced here in analysis become the skills you bring to making rather than interpreting.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **bell hooks** was working on *Black Looks: Race and Representation* — essays that taught a generation of writers how to take a film, an ad, or a magazine cover apart at the seams and show what the image was *doing* — decades before most people had heard of image analysis as a genre. Here's a prompt to find out more, and then make it better.

**Run this:**

```
Who was bell hooks, and how does their work on the oppositional gaze
and visual cultural criticism connect to image analysis as a writing
genre? Keep it to three paragraphs. End with the single most
surprising thing about their career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"bell hooks"** on Wikipedia after you run this. See what the model got right, got wrong, or left out.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it to explain hooks's concept of the "oppositional gaze" — what kind of looking it is, where it comes from, and how a writer practices it on the page.
- Ask: "hooks chose to write her name in lowercase. What was the argument behind that choice — and how does that argument bear on how she analyzed images of Black women?"
- Add the framing: "Answer as if you're hooks herself, writing the opening paragraph of an essay on a single image from this week's news."

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. Find any image — a painting, photograph, advertisement, or screenshot — and write two versions of a single observation about it: Version A as description (what is in the image); Version B as analysis (what the formal choice is doing). The observation must be about the same specific detail in both versions. Label which is which and write one sentence explaining what changed between them. *(Tests: the description/analysis distinction as a concrete writing practice before applying it to a full essay.)*

2. Take any image and identify which two of the eight elements — point of view, composition, color, light, symbol, juxtaposition, line, texture — are doing the most work. For each, write one sentence naming where in the image it appears and one sentence naming what it accomplishes. Do not analyze all eight. *(Tests: selective application of the element vocabulary — identifying the elements doing the most work rather than running through a checklist exhaustively.)*

3. Find the same image read by two different critics, viewers, or commentators — two reviews of a film still, two analyses of a photograph, two assessments of a monument, two interpretations of an ad. Name where their readings agree and where they diverge. What is the evidence each reading draws on to support the divergence? *(Tests: the counter-interpretation discipline — recognizing that the same image can sustain multiple readings before writing your own.)*

**Application**

4. Write the description section of your image analysis — 200–300 words, naming everything visible in the frame without yet interpreting. When you are done, go through every sentence and mark it: D (description — what you saw) or I (interpretation — what you inferred). If any sentence is I, revise it into D. After the revision, note what you had to cut or defer to the analysis section. *(Tests: description as a discipline separate from interpretation; developing awareness of how readily inference enters what we think we are only seeing.)*

5. Research the context of your chosen image: the maker's biography, the medium and circumstances of production, the reception history, the physical or institutional location. Write a 150–200 word context section. After writing it, ask: which piece of context is doing the most work for your interpretation — and would your reading change substantially if that context were unknown? *(Tests: context as analytical evidence rather than background information; understanding which contextual facts are load-bearing for the reading.)*

6. Write a one-sentence interpretive claim for your image: what the image is doing and how the formal choices accomplish it. Then write the strongest counter-interpretation you can — where could another viewer reach a different reading using evidence from the same image? Write two to three sentences developing the counter-reading, citing specific visual evidence. *(Tests: the interpretive thesis and counter-interpretation moves before drafting the full analysis.)*

7. The Roosevelt monument analysis in this chapter names several things simultaneously: compositional hierarchy, the decision to use racial types rather than portraits, the institutional placement, the eighty-one-year acceptance history, and the empty plinth as its own image. Take any public monument, statue, or publicly installed work you can visit or find detailed photographs of. Apply the same analytical moves: name the compositional hierarchy (if any), the formal decisions about representation, the institutional placement, and the reception history. Write 300–400 words. *(Tests: applying the chapter's monument-analysis moves to a new case; extending the formal analysis into political reading.)*

**Synthesis**

8. Write the analysis section of your image analysis — approximately 600–700 words. It must: name at least three formal elements by the chapter's vocabulary; explain what each element is doing (not just where it appears); connect the formal analysis to a specific interpretive claim about what the image is doing as a whole; and acknowledge at least one place where another viewer could reasonably read the same element differently. After writing, identify the single sentence that is doing the most analytical work. *(Tests: full application of the formal vocabulary in original analytical prose; the interpretive claim integrated with specific formal evidence.)*

9. Assemble the complete image analysis: introduction, description, context, analysis, engagement with other readings, conclusion. The introduction must name the analytical question. The conclusion must say what the close reading produced — not a restatement of the introduction but a claim that the analysis has now earned. After assembling, check every claim in the analysis section against the description: can each analytical claim be traced to something actually visible in the image? If not, revise until it can. *(Tests: full integration of the genre's structure in original prose; the discipline of grounding every interpretive claim in visible evidence.)*

**Challenge**

10. Find an image generated by a text-to-image AI tool (DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, or similar). Write 300–400 words: what can and cannot be said about this image using the chapter's analytical vocabulary? Where does the absence of a maker's biography, a reception history, and a production context change what the analysis can claim? What is the analysis of an AI-generated image actually analyzing — the image itself, the training data it reflects, the prompt that produced it, or something else? There is no single correct answer; the quality of the response depends on the honesty of the confrontation with the difference. *(Open-ended; forces the student to test the chapter's 2026 complication against a real case; develops awareness of what the analytical vocabulary assumes about its objects.)*

---



If sustained study showed that students who used multimodal LLMs to assist with image analysis produced no stronger interpretations than students who worked from their own observation alone — that the AI assistance averaged the analysis toward standard readings without producing the close-looking discipline the genre rewards — I would revise the chapter's openness to using the tools. The early evidence is mixed: the tools are useful for description and context inventory, weaker for interpretation. The pedagogy here is to use them for their strengths and resist them for the central interpretive work.

## Still unresolved

The cultural meaning of color, symbol, and gesture is more contested than this chapter admits. The advice I have offered — name the cultural specificity, do not assume universal meaning — is correct. It is also harder to operationalize than it sounds. Students analyzing non-Western images often default to Western-tradition symbol meanings without noticing they are doing so. The fix is to ground specific claims in actual cultural-history scholarship, which is harder than asking the model and harder than I have made it sound. I do not have a clean teaching solution for this yet.
