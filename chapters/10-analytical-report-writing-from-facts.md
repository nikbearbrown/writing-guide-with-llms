# Chapter 10 — The Analytical Report

*Letting the Facts Argue.*

---

In January 2018, a woman named Luciana Borio sat down to write a report. She was the director of medical and biodefense preparedness at the U.S. National Security Council. She had spent her career thinking about how the United States would respond when, not if, the next major pandemic arrived. The report she wrote that month examined the country's existing readiness. It identified gaps. It named specific weaknesses in the federal response infrastructure. Every claim in it was supported by evidence.

Two years later, in the first months of 2020, most of what Borio's report had identified turned out to be the exact infrastructure that failed. By the end of that year, the United States had recorded more COVID-19 deaths than any other country in absolute terms — this despite having one of the most sophisticated medical systems in the world, spending more per capita on health care than any other nation, and having, two years earlier, a document that specified exactly what would break.[^1]

[^1]: Trevor Garcia's student analytical report, anthologized in the OpenStax *Writing Guide with Handbook*, aggregates comparative pandemic figures from the *Wall Street Journal*, *Washington Post*, Worldometer, and Just Security.

The document failed not because it was wrong. It failed because the people who needed to act on it didn't, and the people who needed to fund the fixes didn't, and the gaps it named remained unfilled for two years until the gaps became the story.

Here is the puzzle I want you to sit with: Borio's report was right. It was specific. It was supported. It was verifiable. And it was ignored. So what is the point of an analytical report?

I will give you the answer now, and then spend the chapter explaining why the answer is not as discouraging as it sounds. The point of an analytical report is that it creates an object — a specific, checkable, revisable record of what the evidence said at a particular moment — that exists independently of whether anyone acts on it in the short term. The analytical report did not prevent the pandemic failure. But it meant that the failure had a before. It meant that when the question *did anyone see this coming?* was asked, the answer was not silence. It meant that the gap between what was known and what was done was legible, and legibility is the first condition of accountability.

That is what the genre is for. Let the facts argue. Then let the argument stand in the record.

---

## What the genre is doing

I need to be precise about the mechanism, because the phrase *let the facts argue* sounds simple and is not.

Facts do not argue. Facts sit there. The argument is made by a person who assembles facts, organizes them into a structure, draws inferences from the structure, and renders the whole thing in prose clear enough that a reader can follow the reasoning and check it. The analytical report is the genre that names this process as the primary job. Everything else — the voice, the structure, the citation style — is in service of one goal: making the inferential path from evidence to conclusion visible and reproducible.

This is different from what other genres do with facts. An op-ed uses facts as support for a stance the writer already holds. A personal essay uses facts as texture in a first-person narrative. A biography uses facts to reconstruct a life. The analytical report uses facts as the *primary material of argument*. The conclusion is supposed to emerge from the analysis, not precede it.

I will not pretend this is always what happens in practice. Writers are not blank slates. You will come to your topic with a tentative view, and that view will shape what you search for and what you notice. That is not cheating. It is how reasoning works. The discipline the genre asks of you is not *have no prior view* — that is impossible. The discipline is *let the evidence revise the prior view, and show the reader the revision*. If you think U.S. pandemic preparedness failed for reason X, and then your evidence shows reasons Y and Z are also important, the report has to reflect the complication. The evidence does not serve your thesis. Your thesis serves the evidence.

---

## The fact-opinion problem, and why it doesn't solve cleanly

Most analytical report instruction begins with the distinction between fact and opinion, and the distinction is useful enough that I will state it before I explain why it is insufficient.

A fact is a statement whose truth can be verified by checking external evidence — observation, measurement, document. An opinion is a statement of belief or value that depends on the believer's framework for its truth. *The Hubble Space Telescope was launched on April 24, 1990* is a fact. *The Hubble was the most important astronomical instrument of the twentieth century* is an opinion — it depends on what you mean by *most important* and which other instruments you compare it against.

The problem is that almost no non-trivial claim in an analytical report is purely one or the other. Consider: *The U.S. response to COVID-19 was slower than India's in the early months of 2020.* Is this fact or opinion? It depends on what *response* means, what *slower* means, what *early months* means, and how you measure each. Once those terms are specified, the claim becomes verifiable. Before they are, it is contestable.

This is the situation the analytical report lives in. Most of the claims that matter — the claims that do real analytical work — are interpretations of evidence. They are anchored in fact, but they require judgment to make. The genre's solution is not to eliminate interpretation. It is to *specify the terms* clearly enough that a reader can check the work.

This is the precise move: every time you make a claim that is doing analytical work, ask whether you have defined your terms specifically enough that a hostile reader could verify the claim against the same evidence. If yes, you have an interpretation. If no, you have an opinion in fact-clothing. The difference is not always large. The discipline of finding it is.

## The three biases you cannot eliminate, only compensate for

Three biases operate on every analytical report. On the writer, on the sources, on the reader. They cannot be eliminated. The discipline is to know they are there.

The first is confirmation bias: the tendency to seek and weight information that confirms what you already believe. This operates most heavily in the topic-selection and source-selection phases. Without noticing it, you will find sources that agree with your tentative thesis. The fix is not to avoid having a tentative thesis. The fix is to deliberately seek sources that disagree. The strongest analytical reports make this visible — they cite the best opposing source and engage with it, not because they had to, but because doing so makes the eventual conclusion harder to dismiss.

The second is source bias: the position from which a source produces its information. A pharmaceutical company's report on its own drug carries a different kind of bias than the same company's marketing department carries. A government agency's data on its own performance has a different quality than an opposition-party analyst's report on the same question. The bias is not a disqualifier. Biased sources still produce verifiable information. The bias has to be named in the report so the reader can weight the evidence accordingly.

The third is what psychologists call the false consensus effect: the tendency to assume that other people share your views more widely than they actually do. In an analytical report, this shows up when the writer assumes the reader will find a particular claim obvious, when in fact the claim is contested in ways the writer has not noticed. The fix is to imagine a specific hostile reader — not a generic skeptic, but a person who would actively disagree with the report's emerging thesis — and write toward what would persuade that person. Not what would persuade you. You are already persuaded.

## Why topics are always too big, and how to fix them

There is a nearly universal failure mode in analytical reports, and it happens before any drafting begins. The topic is too big.

*The U.S. response to COVID-19* is too big. The student report that works narrows it to: *the U.S. response in 2020, focusing on three specific causes — reductions in expert personnel, equipment shortages, and policy decisions that delayed action*. The narrowing is what made the report writable. Three causes, each with specific evidence, each carrying a specific inference. Together, they add up to a conclusion the evidence can support.

*Climate change* is too big. *The carbon footprint of a specific manufacturing process in a specific industry* is workable. *Social media's effect on teenagers* is too big. *The relationship between one platform's algorithmic recommendation patterns and a specific measurable mental-health indicator in a specific age group over a specific period* is workable.

The diagnostic test is this. Try to finish two sentences:

*This report analyzes [specific subject] by examining [three to five specific dimensions].*

*The evidence suggests [tentative direction of conclusion].*

If you cannot finish both sentences cleanly, the topic is still too big. The inability to finish the sentence is the information. It is telling you that you do not yet know what you are analyzing. Do not draft. Narrow first.

The narrowing discipline is uncomfortable because it feels like giving up. You had a large, important topic, and now you have a smaller one. The trade is that the smaller topic is actually writable, actually arguable, actually verifiable. A report that examines three specific causes with specific evidence will teach a reader more than a report that gestures at a large subject with surface-level coverage of twelve different angles. Depth beats breadth in this genre. Every time.

## What the structure is doing

The analytical report has five parts, and each is doing a specific job.

The introduction names the subject, establishes why it is worth analyzing, and states what the report will examine. It sometimes holds the full thesis — the direction the evidence points — until after the analysis. That is a legitimate choice. The introduction's job is to make the reader willing to follow you through the analysis, not to preempt the analysis. If you announce the conclusion before earning it, you have written an op-ed with footnotes.

The background gives the reader what they need to know before the analysis can begin — history, prior research, definitions of terms that will recur. The discipline here is ruthless: if a background section does not load directly into the analysis that follows, it is filler. Cut it.

The analysis is the body. Each section develops one cause, one effect, one comparison, one sub-problem. Each section makes a claim, presents evidence, and draws an inference. The inferences accumulate across sections. By the end of the analysis, you have built something: a body of evidence organized so that a specific conclusion follows from it.

The synthesis pulls the analysis together. What does the evidence, collectively, suggest? Where is there ambiguity? Where does it converge? The full thesis lands here if it has been held back. If it was stated in the introduction, the synthesis restates it with the full evidential structure behind it. The reader can now see both the claim and the scaffolding that holds it up.

The conclusion states what follows from the analysis — implications for further research, for policy, for understanding — briefly, without recapping. The conclusion is not a summary. It is where the analysis has brought you.

One structural discipline that the source text does not state directly but that I find essential: every section of the analysis should be able to answer the question *so what?* A section that names a cause and presents evidence that the cause exists, but does not tell the reader what follows from the cause, is incomplete. The *so what* is the inference, and the inference is what makes the section do analytical work rather than just informational work.

## The voice problem

The analytical report is conventionally written in a *neutral* or *objective* tone, and that phrase is misleading enough that I want to be direct about what it actually means.

Neutral does not mean without a voice. You are not impersonating a machine. Neutral means calibrated to be trustworthy to a reader who does not yet share your tentative conclusion. The voice is still yours. The calibration is the discipline.

Specific moves. Avoid first-person assertion where it adds nothing — *I think* and *I believe* are usually unnecessary in an analytical report, because the entire report is your thinking; adding the tag adds nothing except an implication that the claim is personal rather than evidential. Use first person when it describes something you specifically did: *I interviewed*, *I observed*, *I measured*. Those are useful.

Restrain adjectives. *Devastating*, *shocking*, *outrageous* are signals that you are reaching for emotional force instead of evidentiary force. Cut them. The facts, properly assembled, carry more force than your adjectives do, and the adjectives make a reader trust you less.

Hedge precisely. *Suggests*, *indicates*, *is consistent with*, *does not yet establish* — these verbs are the genre's grammar for calibrated uncertainty. Their opposites — *proves*, *demonstrates beyond doubt*, *makes clear* — are rarely warranted, and when they are not warranted and you use them anyway, you have borrowed credibility you have not earned. The precision of your hedging is part of the argument.

Cite specifically. *According to a 2020 Pew Research Center survey, 64 percent of American adults report...* lands. *Studies show* does not. *Studies show* is the verbal equivalent of a citation you forgot to look up.

Define every contested term before you use it analytically. *Recession*, *misinformation*, *bias*, *progress* mean different things in different contexts. If your report hinges on one of these terms, define the meaning the report is using before the analysis begins. If you do not define the term, you will use it in two different senses in two different paragraphs without noticing, and a hostile reader will notice.

The voice that emerges from these moves is not flat. It is the voice of a careful person doing careful work, legible to the reader as careful. That is the goal. You are not hiding. You are earning trust by demonstrating that you have done the work and that you know the limits of what the work shows.

## What makes a source reliable and what makes it not

The analytical report requires sources, and not all sources are equal in what they establish. Some specifics.

The strongest sources are peer-reviewed academic publications, major government data sources with documented methodologies, reporting from outlets with editorial standards and fact-checking infrastructure, and reports from established research organizations. The political position of the source organization matters and should be named in your report — Brookings and AEI produce serious work and hold different political orientations, and a reader weighing your evidence needs to know that.

Weaker but sometimes usable: industry trade publications, reporting from outlets that cover a specific sector, Substack newsletters or blogs by named experts with documented credentials. Use these with citation of the credential, not just the publication.

Not usable as primary evidence: anonymous online sources, social media posts unless the post itself is the subject of analysis, material from organizations with explicit advocacy positions except as evidence of what the organization argues.

One category that requires special attention in 2026: language model outputs. Models generate plausible-sounding citations that may not exist. Every citation in your report must be one you have read. The model's output is a lead to verify, not a source to cite.

---

## A note about AI

Three specific places where the model is genuinely useful in analytical report work, and three where it does damage.

Useful: stress-testing your interpretation — paste an analytical paragraph and ask for the strongest alternative interpretation a reader who disagreed with your conclusion would offer of the same evidence; catching invisible assumptions — ask the model where your argument is relying on premises you have not explicitly defended; source-checking your paraphrase — paste a source passage alongside your paraphrase of it and ask whether you have imported any claim the source does not actually make.

Damage: generating sources (the citations will sound plausible and may not exist, and every citation must be one you have read), drafting the analysis (the model produces paragraphs with the texture of reasoning without doing the inferential work — *suggests* appears where it should, but the inference was not earned from your evidence), and polishing the prose (the single most reliable way to introduce epistemic confidence the original sentence did not carry — *suggests* quietly becomes *shows*, and the calibration you built in is quietly removed).

The rule that covers all three: the model can help you think about work you have done. It cannot do the analytical work the genre requires, and when it simulates doing it, the result passes a surface reading and fails a close one.

## Exercises

The chapter's inferred learning objectives: (1) explain the mechanism of the analytical report — what *let the facts argue* actually means, and how the inferential path from evidence to conclusion is made visible and reproducible; (2) apply the fact-opinion distinction and the term-specification discipline to contested analytical claims; (3) identify and compensate for confirmation bias, source bias, and false consensus effect in a developing report; (4) narrow an over-broad topic using the two-sentence diagnostic until it is writable, arguable, and verifiable; (5) evaluate the structural function of each of the five report sections — including the *so what* discipline in the analysis sections; (6) apply the voice and citation calibration moves — hedging precisely, restraining adjectives, citing specifically — to weak analytical prose.

---

**Warm-up**

*1. Fact, opinion, or interpretation?* The chapter argues that most consequential claims in an analytical report are neither pure facts nor pure opinions, but interpretations of evidence — claims anchored in fact that require term-specification to become verifiable. Classify each of the following five claims as fact, opinion, or interpretation. For each claim you classify as interpretation, name the term that would need to be specified for the claim to become verifiable: (a) *The United States spends more per capita on health care than any other high-income country.* (b) *The U.S. health care system is inefficient.* (c) *Urban areas have higher rates of pedestrian fatalities per capita than rural areas.* (d) *Remote work has reduced employee productivity.* (e) *Social media companies are not doing enough to address misinformation.*
*(Tests objective 2 — the fact-opinion-interpretation distinction and term specification.)*
*Difficulty: Low.*

*2. The two-sentence diagnostic.* The chapter gives a two-sentence test for whether a topic is narrow enough to be writable. Apply it to each of the following five topics. For any topic that fails the test, rewrite it as a narrowed topic that passes: (a) *Climate change.* (b) *The opioid crisis.* (c) *The relationship between minimum wage legislation and teenage employment rates in states that passed $15 minimum wage laws between 2018 and 2023.* (d) *Immigration policy.* (e) *The effect of remote learning on math achievement gaps among low-income middle school students during 2020–2022.*
*(Tests objective 4 — topic narrowing using the two-sentence diagnostic.)*
*Difficulty: Low.*

---

**Application**

*3. Source bias audit.* Find three sources on a contested empirical question — pick any question where institutional position could influence the data presented: pharmaceutical regulation, energy policy, gun violence, labor economics, anything. For each source, identify: (a) the type of source; (b) the institutional or political position; and (c) one specific claim the source makes that a reader should weight differently because of that position. Write 200–300 words.
*(Tests objective 3 — identifying and naming source bias so the reader can weight evidence.)*
*Difficulty: Medium.*

*4. The so-what audit.* The chapter identifies a discipline for analysis sections: every section must answer *so what* — the inference from the evidence, not just the evidence itself. Take any two paragraphs from a published analytical piece, a student report, or a news article that presents evidence. For each paragraph, write one sentence that names what the *so what* is (or should be), and one sentence explaining whether the paragraph delivers it or leaves it implicit.
*(Tests objective 5 — the structural function of inference in analysis sections.)*
*Difficulty: Medium.*

*5. Rewrite for calibration.* The chapter names five voice and citation moves: avoid unnecessary first-person assertion, restrain adjectives, hedge precisely, cite specifically, define contested terms. Take the following passage and rewrite it applying all five moves. Do not change the substance of the claims — only the calibration and specificity:

> *Studies show that social media is devastating for teenagers. I think the evidence clearly demonstrates that platforms like Instagram and TikTok are making young people more anxious and depressed. The shocking rise in mental health problems among teens is obviously connected to screen time, and it is outrageous that companies haven't done more to fix it.*

Write the revised passage (approximately the same length) and then write 100 words identifying which specific moves you applied and where.
*(Tests objective 6 — applying the voice calibration moves to weak analytical prose.)*
*Difficulty: Medium.*

---

**Synthesis**

*6. Confirmation bias by design.* The chapter says the fix for confirmation bias is to deliberately seek sources that disagree with your tentative thesis and engage with them visibly. Choose a topic you have a prior view on — something you already believe. Identify two sources that support your view and two that complicate or contradict it. Then write a 300-word analytical paragraph on one dimension of the topic that gives the complicating evidence its proper weight — without abandoning your tentative thesis, but without suppressing the complication either. After the paragraph, write 100 words reflecting on what you had to do to give the complicating evidence its fair weight.
*(Tests objectives 1, 2, and 3 together — mechanism, interpretation discipline, and bias compensation.)*
*Difficulty: Medium-high.*

*7. The five-section audit.* Take a published analytical report, a government policy brief, or a student report of at least 1,500 words. Map it to the chapter's five sections: introduction, background, analysis, synthesis, conclusion. For each section, write two sentences: one describing what the section does in this specific report, and one evaluating whether it does its job as the chapter defines it. Then identify the one section where the report is weakest, and write 150 words explaining what it would need to do differently.
*(Tests objectives 1 and 5 — mechanism and structural function across all five sections.)*
*Difficulty: High.*

---

**Challenge**

*8. The legibility argument.* The chapter opens with a claim that may be the hardest one it makes: that the value of an analytical report is not contingent on anyone acting on it, because the report creates a legible record that makes the gap between knowledge and action accountable. Evaluate this claim in 500–700 words. You may agree, disagree, or qualify. Your argument must engage with at least one case where an analytical report was ignored and one case where it was not — using real, verifiable examples — and must address the obvious counterargument: that a report nobody acts on is just a document, and that legibility without consequence is not accountability but its appearance.
*(Open-ended. Tests all six objectives and requires the student to argue against the chapter's own framing.)*
*Difficulty: High.*

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 10

**What you're building:** a sharpened thesis and an evidence-organized outline for your analytical report, before you begin drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended if you want to keep your developing research notes alongside the analysis.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 2,000–3,500 word analytical report for a college writing
class. The genre uses fact-based analysis to examine causes, effects,
comparisons, or problems and possible solutions.

The subject I'm analyzing is: [name in one sentence]

The dimensions I'm planning to examine are: [list 3–5]

The sources I've gathered (with full citations) are pasted below.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. Read my source list. For each source, identify what kind of source
   it is and where it sits politically or institutionally — what its
   position is, in a sentence. Flag any source whose position I
   should explicitly name in my report.

2. Based on my subject and my dimensions, ask me four diagnostic
   questions about whether the dimensions are sharp enough. Where am
   I being vague? Where could a hostile reader make my categories
   collapse? Where am I assuming a comparison is fair without
   defending it?

3. Once I've answered, propose a tentative thesis sentence — one
   sentence — that names the subject, the dimensions analyzed, and
   the direction the evidence points. Do NOT overstate the certainty.

4. For each of my dimensions, identify the strongest piece of
   evidence in my source list that supports the tentative thesis and
   the strongest piece that complicates it. If a dimension has only
   supporting evidence and no complicating evidence, flag it — that
   may mean I need more sources before I can write that section
   credibly.

Do not draft the report. Do not write the introduction. The drafting
is mine.

Here are my sources:
[paste source list]
```

**What this produces:** a position on each of your sources, a sharpened set of dimensions, a tentative thesis, and an evidence map. After this, you draft the report yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

For ChatGPT or Gemini, the prompt works as written. For Cowork: useful if your sources are PDFs or long articles you have downloaded — tell Cowork to read the files before starting the conversation. For Claude Code: not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the model identifies a source's political position, it will sometimes be overconfident — describing the source as more biased or less biased than it actually is. Verify by reading the source's editorial pages or about page. The model's first guess at institutional position is reasonable but not authoritative.
