# Chapter 10 — The Analytical Report

*Letting the Facts Argue.*

A short scene from January 2018.

A woman named Luciana Borio sat down to write a report. She was the director of medical and biodefense preparedness at the U.S. National Security Council. She had spent her career thinking about how the United States would respond when, not if, the next major pandemic arrived. The report she wrote that month examined the country's existing readiness. It identified gaps. It named specific weaknesses in the federal response infrastructure. It was not a speculative document. Every claim in it was supported by evidence.

Two years later — in the first months of 2020 — most of what Borio's report had identified turned out to be the exact infrastructure that failed. By the end of that year, the United States had recorded more deaths from COVID-19 than any other country, in absolute terms. By the end of 2021, the number of U.S. deaths was approximately 20 million confirmed cases worth of consequence, against an Indian total of confirmed cases roughly half that — despite India having more than four times the population.[^1]

[^1]: Trevor Garcia's student analytical report in the OpenStax *Writing Guide with Handbook* aggregates these comparative figures from contemporary sources including the *Wall Street Journal*, *Washington Post*, Worldometer, and Just Security. The 20-million-cases figure tracks confirmed reported cases through late 2020.

I am not telling this story to argue about the pandemic. I am telling it because the analytical report is the genre that produced Borio's 2018 document. And the analytical report that one student wrote about the comparative U.S. response — Trevor Garcia, in the OpenStax source for this chapter — is a model of what the genre can do at the student scale.

The genre's central move: *let the facts argue*. The writer assembles evidence, organizes it, draws conclusions from it, and renders the argument visible to a reader who can then evaluate whether the conclusions follow. The writer's stake — what they want to be true — is set aside. The facts get to speak.

The assignment at the end of this chapter is your own analytical report. Approximately 2,000 to 3,500 words. A topic of your choice. Three to five reliable sources. A thesis earned by the evidence, not announced before it.

## What an analytical report is, and what it isn't

The analytical report is a non-fiction genre that examines a subject by analyzing causes, effects, comparisons, contrasts, or problems and their possible solutions — using facts as evidence, organized into a coherent argument that a reader can follow and verify.

What it is not:

- *An op-ed*. The op-ed argues from a stance the writer announces. The analytical report lets the stance emerge from the analysis.
- *A research paper*. The research paper is a wider category — analytical reports are a kind of research paper, but not the only kind. A literature review or a position paper is also a research paper. The analytical report is specifically the one that uses fact-based analysis as its primary tool.
- *A summary*. The analytical report contains summary but is not one. It moves beyond summary to interpretation, while keeping the interpretation anchored in the summarized evidence.
- *A personal essay*. The first-person presence of the writer is restrained. The writer's experience is not the evidence. External, verifiable evidence is the evidence.

The genre exists in many forms outside academia. Investigative journalism is a long-form cousin. Policy reports — like Borio's 2018 document — are analytical reports written for government. Industry analyst reports are analytical reports written for business. White papers, technical reports, after-action reviews. The student version of the genre is the practice ground for all of these.

## Fact and opinion — a working distinction that won't hold up cleanly

The chapter has to make a distinction the rest of the chapter will keep undermining. The distinction is the one between *fact* and *opinion*. Let me state the clean version first, then the complications.

The clean version. A *fact* is a statement whose truth can be verified by checking external evidence — observation, measurement, document. An *opinion* is a statement of belief or value that depends on the believer's framework for its truth.

Examples on the clean side. *The Hubble Space Telescope was launched on April 24, 1990.* Fact. Verifiable. *The Hubble was the most important astronomical instrument of the twentieth century.* Opinion. Depends on what you mean by *most important* and how you weight scientific against cultural impact and which other instruments you compare it against.

Now the complication. Almost no non-trivial statement is purely fact or purely opinion. Most statements you will encounter in an analytical report are *interpretations of evidence* — claims that are anchored in fact but that require judgment to make. *The U.S. response to COVID-19 was slower than India's in the early months of 2020.* Is this fact or opinion? It depends on what *response* means, what *slower* means, what *early months* means, and how you measure each. Once those terms are specified, the claim becomes verifiable. Before they are, it is contestable.

The analytical report lives in this contested space. The writer's job is not to pretend the contest is resolved. The writer's job is to *specify the terms* clearly enough that a reader can check the work.

A practical move: every time you make a claim in your report that is doing analytical work, ask yourself whether you have defined the terms specifically enough that a hostile reader could verify your claim against the same evidence. If yes, you have an interpretation. If no, you have an opinion in fact-clothing.

## Bias — the writer's, the source's, the reader's

Three kinds of bias matter for the analytical report. All three operate on the writer, on the sources, and on the reader.

**Confirmation bias.** The tendency to seek and weight information that confirms what you already believe. This operates on the writer most heavily in the topic-selection and source-selection phases. You will, without noticing, tend to find sources that agree with your tentative thesis. The fix is to deliberately seek and engage with sources that disagree. The strongest analytical reports do this visibly — they cite the best opposing source and engage with it, not because the writer needed to, but because doing so makes the eventual conclusion stronger.

**Source bias.** The position from which a source produces its information. A pharmaceutical company's report on its own drug carries a kind of bias the company's marketing department's report does not. A government agency's data on its own performance has a kind of bias an opposition-party analyst's report does not. The bias is not a deal-breaker — biased sources still produce information that can be cited carefully. The bias has to be *named* in the report so the reader can weight it.

**False consensus effect.** The tendency to assume that other people share your views more than they actually do. This shows up in the analytical report when the writer assumes the reader will find a particular claim obvious, when in fact the claim is contested in ways the writer has not noticed. The fix is to imagine, specifically, a hostile reader — not a generic skeptic, but a person who would actively disagree with the report's emerging thesis — and write toward what would persuade *them*, not what would persuade you.

These three biases will not be eliminated from your report. They will be present. The discipline is to know they are there and to compensate for them deliberately.

## Choosing a topic — narrow until it can be carried

Most student analytical reports fail in the topic-selection phase, before any drafting begins. The failure is uniform: the topic is too big.

*The U.S. response to COVID-19* is too big. Trevor Garcia's report works because he narrowed it: *the U.S. response in 2020, focusing on three specific causes — reductions in expert personnel, equipment shortages, and policy decisions*. The narrowing made the report writable.

*Climate change* is too big. *The carbon footprint of one specific manufacturing process in one specific industry* is writable.

*Social media's effect on teenagers* is too big. *The relationship between TikTok algorithmic recommendation patterns and a specific measurable mental-health indicator in adolescents during 2020–2023* is writable.

The narrowing rule: if you cannot list the three to five specific causes, effects, comparisons, or sub-problems your report will analyze, your topic is still too big.

A useful diagnostic. Try to finish this sentence in two sentences:

*This report analyzes [specific subject] by examining [three to five specific dimensions]. The evidence shows [tentative direction of conclusion].*

If you cannot finish the sentence cleanly, narrow further before you start.

## Sources — what counts and what doesn't

For an analytical report, three to five reliable sources is the floor. Reliable means *verifiable and credible to a knowledgeable reader*. Some specifics.

Strongest:

- Peer-reviewed academic publications (journals, edited books from academic publishers).
- Major government data sources (Census Bureau, CDC, Bureau of Labor Statistics, equivalent agencies in other countries).
- Reporting from publications with editorial standards and fact-checking infrastructure (*The New York Times*, *Washington Post*, *The Guardian*, *Atlantic*, *Wall Street Journal*, *Bloomberg*, *Reuters*, *Associated Press*, *Financial Times*, etc.) — but check for the specific reporter's track record and the publication's politics on your specific topic.
- Reports from established research organizations — Pew Research, RAND, Brookings, AEI, regional Federal Reserve Banks — with the political position of the organization noted in your report.

Weaker but sometimes usable with care:

- Industry-specific trade publications, when reporting on that industry.
- Substack newsletters and blogs by named experts with established credentials. Cite the credential.
- Wikipedia — never as a primary source. Useful for locating primary sources via its citations.

Not usable as primary evidence:

- Social media posts unless the post itself is the subject of analysis.
- Anonymous online sources.
- Material from organizations with explicit advocacy positions, except as evidence of what the organization argues.
- LLM outputs. Models generate plausible-sounding citations that may not exist. Treat any source the model produces as a lead to verify, not a citation to use.

## Structure — what an analytical report contains

Five sections, roughly. Specifics vary by topic.

**Introduction.** Names the subject. Establishes that the subject is worth analyzing. Briefly states what the report will examine. Often ends with a thesis sentence — though the thesis may be tentative at this point in the report; analytical reports sometimes hold their full conclusion until after the analysis.

**Background.** Whatever the reader needs to know about the subject before the analysis can begin. The history. The relevant prior research. The terms that will recur in the report's analysis, defined. Keep it tight. Background that does not load directly into the analysis is filler.

**Analysis.** The body of the report. Organized around the dimensions you committed to in the topic-selection phase — three to five sections, each developing one cause, one effect, one comparison, one sub-problem. Each section makes a claim, presents evidence, and draws an inference. The inferences accumulate.

**Synthesis.** Pulls the analysis back together. What does the evidence, collectively, suggest? Where does it leave ambiguity? Where does it converge? The thesis lands here if it has been held back; if it was stated in the introduction, the synthesis re-states it with the evidence behind it.

**Conclusion.** What follows from the analysis. Implications for further research, for policy, for understanding. Brief. Not a recap. The place the analysis has brought the reader.

## The tone problem

Analytical reports are conventionally written in what the genre calls a *neutral* or *objective* tone. The phrase is misleading.

*Neutral* does not mean *without a voice*. It means *with a voice calibrated to be trustworthy to a reader who does not yet share your tentative conclusion*. Your voice is still there. You are not impersonating a robot.

The specific moves that make the voice neutral:

- Third-person where first-person would distract. *I think* and *I believe* are usually unnecessary in an analytical report — the entire report is your thinking; saying so adds nothing. *I observed*, *I measured*, *I interviewed* — these are different; they describe what you specifically did and are useful.
- Restraint with adjectives. *Devastating*, *shocking*, *outrageous* are signals that the writer is reaching for emotional force rather than evidentiary force. Cut them. The facts, properly assembled, will carry the force without your help.
- Hedge precisely. *Suggests*, *indicates*, *consistent with*, *does not yet establish* — these verbs are the genre's grammar for calibrated uncertainty. The opposite — *proves*, *demonstrates beyond doubt*, *makes clear* — is rarely warranted in an analytical report. Reach for these only when the evidence really does meet that bar, which is less often than first-draft prose tends to assume.
- Cite specifically. *According to a 2020 Pew Research study, 64 percent of...* lands. *Studies show* does not.
- Define every contested term before using it. *Recession*, *misinformation*, *bias*, *progress* — these mean different things in different contexts. Specify the meaning your report is using before relying on it.

The voice that emerges from these moves is not flat. It is the voice of a careful person doing careful work, visible to the reader as careful.

## A note about AI

Three specific moves where the language model is useful in analytical report work, and three where it does damage.

Useful:

*Source-checking your evidence.* Once you have your sources, paste a specific passage from your report and the source you are drawing it from, and ask: *Does my paraphrase accurately represent what this source said? Have I imported any claim the source doesn't actually make?* The model is reasonably good at catching the small distortions that creep in when you summarize.

*Stress-testing your interpretation.* Paste one of your analytical paragraphs and ask: *What alternative interpretation would a reader who disagreed with my conclusion offer of this same evidence?* The model produces the alternative interpretation. You then have to grapple with it in your report or in your thinking.

*Catching your own bias.* Ask: *Looking at my report's argument, what are the assumptions I am making that I have not explicitly defended? Where would a reader who didn't share those assumptions push back?* The model is often good at finding the assumptions invisible to the writer.

Damage:

*Generating sources.* Models will produce confident-sounding citations that may not exist. Every citation must be one you have read. No exceptions.

*Drafting the analysis.* The model will produce paragraphs that have the texture of analysis without doing the inferential work. The paragraph will name a cause, then assert an effect, with prose that reads logical and is in fact filler. Your analysis has to be your reasoning on your sources. The model's analysis is on its training data.

*"Polishing" the prose. The model will tighten sentences in ways that often introduce confidence the original sentence did not carry. *Suggests* becomes *shows*. *Consistent with* becomes *demonstrates*. The calibration of certainty is part of the genre. Read every polished suggestion against the original specifically for shifts in epistemic confidence.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 10

**What you're building:** a sharpened thesis and an evidence-organized outline for your analytical report, before you begin drafting.

**Tool:** [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai/) chat. A Claude Project recommended if you want to keep your developing research notes alongside the analysis.

**The prompt:**

```
I'm writing a 2,000–3,500 word analytical report for a college writing
class. The genre uses fact-based analysis to examine causes, effects,
comparisons, or problems and possible solutions.

The subject I'm analyzing is: [name in one sentence]

The dimensions I'm planning to examine are: [list 3–5]

The sources I've gathered (with full citations) are pasted below.

Do the following in order, one step at a time, waiting for my
response between steps.

1. Read my source list. For each source, identify what kind of source
   it is and where it sits politically or institutionally — what its
   position is, in a sentence. Flag any source whose position I
   should explicitly name in my report.

2. Based on my subject and my dimensions, ask me four diagnostic
   questions about whether the dimensions are sharp enough. Where am
   I being vague? Where could a hostile reader make my categories
   collapse? Where am I assuming a comparison is fair without
   defending it?

3. Once I've answered, propose a tentative thesis sentence — one
   sentence — that names the subject, the dimensions analyzed, and
   the direction the evidence points. Do NOT overstate the
   certainty.

4. For each of my dimensions, identify the strongest piece of
   evidence in my source list that supports the tentative thesis and
   the strongest piece that complicates it. If a dimension has only
   supporting evidence and no complicating evidence, flag it — that
   may mean I need more sources before I can write that section
   credibly.

Do not draft the report. Do not write the introduction. The drafting
is mine.

Here are my sources:
[paste source list]
```

**What this produces:** a position on each of your sources, a sharpened set of dimensions, a tentative thesis, and an evidence map. After this, you draft the report yourself.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For ChatGPT or Gemini:* works as written.
- *For Cowork:* useful if your sources are PDFs or long articles you have downloaded. Tell Cowork to read the files before starting the conversation.
- *For Claude Code:* not the right tool.

**The instructive failure to expect:** when the model identifies a source's political position, it will sometimes be overconfident — describing the source as more biased or less biased than it actually is. Verify by reading the source's editorial pages or about page. The model's first guess at institutional position is reasonable but not authoritative.

## What would change my mind

If the rise of plausible-sounding LLM-generated text undermined the analytical-report genre's foundational bargain — that the writer has done the work and the reader can verify the citations — to the point where even careful student reports were no longer trusted by educated readers, I would revise the chapter's central claim that the genre can be taught at the student level. The early evidence is that the bargain is still intact when the work is genuinely done, and that careful citation practice protects the writer from the worst of the LLM-credibility collapse. But that is an empirical claim that the next several years will test.

## Still puzzling

The fact-vs-opinion distinction is more useful as a pedagogical scaffold than as a robust philosophical position, and most analytical writing operates in a zone where the two categories are leaking into each other. The advice in this chapter — *specify your terms so the claim becomes verifiable* — is the best workable version I have found, but it does not resolve the underlying problem that most consequential claims about complex systems are interpretations all the way down. I do not know how to teach this without either oversimplifying or losing the student.
