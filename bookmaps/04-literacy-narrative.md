### m00030 — Reaching Across the Field: *Educated* (712w)

**Contributed:** Tara Westover's biography — Idaho upbringing, religious-text-only literacy, junkyard literacies, brother Tyler's departure, ACT prep, BYU, family rupture, three PhDs.

**Used in:** §5 (Westover as the "slow rotation" hinge moment, third of three case studies).

The rewrite uses her specifically as the case where the hinge moment is *distributed in time* rather than compressed to a scene — a structurally different lesson than Douglass or Keller. The Westover treatment in the rewrite is shorter than the source, because the structural point can be made in fewer words.

**Cut:** "Discussion Questions" placeholders (broken in source — `[#](#)` markers only).

### m00031 — Glance at Genre: storytelling elements (843w)

**Contributed:** The five narrative elements — plot, characters, setting, problem/resolution, story arc; "showing not telling"; sensory detail, dialogue, action, engaging language; the Malala Yousafzai eighteen-word opening as compression example; Gottschall's *The Storytelling Animal* (2012).

**Used in:** §3 (what makes a story a story) and §4 (scene vs. summary).

**Reshaped:** The source presents the five elements as a list. The rewrite presents each element as *doing a specific job* — plot is "the chain of becauses"; setting is "where, when, and in what culture"; etc. The Malala sentence is used as a worked example of all five elements compressed into one sentence — a stronger pedagogical move than the source's tabular treatment.

**Cut:** The tone/diction/varied-constructions subsection (m00031 §3) was folded into the §3 paragraph on the five elements rather than treated as a separate bullet list.

### m00032 — Annotated Sample Reading: Frederick Douglass (2,583w)

**Contributed:** Douglass biography; the Mr. Auld scene from chapters 6–7 of the 1845 *Narrative*; the "inch will take an ell" phrasing; the Philpot Street bread-for-letters tactic; Mrs. Auld's transformation under the influence of slaveholding.

**Used in:** §1 reference (Douglass's overhearing), §3 (Douglass dialogue as a five-element example), §5 (full Douglass case study).

**Reshaped:** The source presents the Douglass excerpts with line-by-line teaching annotations interleaved. The rewrite extracts the most pedagogically active phrases (the "inch / ell" passage, the moment of overhearing) and reframes them as a single hinge moment in the case-study format. The annotations are not preserved verbatim; their pedagogical content is absorbed into the chapter's own analytical voice.

**Cut:** The Kennedy interview content (sourcing on the slur's etymology) was preserved as a contextual parenthetical rather than the full source treatment, with an explicit note that the language is used because Douglass is mirroring the rhetoric of slavery itself.

**Critical preservation:** The Douglass passage's racial slur is preserved in its historically situated form (the source's treatment). The note around it follows the source's guidance carefully.

### m00033 — Summary of Assignment + writing process + Helen Keller (7,468w — the biggest module)

**Contributed:**

- The four-stage writing process (prewriting, drafting, peer review, revision).
- The full Helen Keller passage from *The Story of My Life* (1903) — porch, honeysuckle, Sullivan's arrival, doll-breaking, well-house, "w-a-t-e-r."
- The seven-element rhetorical-situation map (author / message / audience / purpose / means / context / culture).
- The story-arc structure (beginning / rising action / climax / falling action / resolution).
- The General → Sensory diction example table (girl → tall, dark-haired Sasha).
- The "Another Lens 1 / 2" alternative assignments (interview-based, DALN-based).
- The Douglass excerpt analysis paired with Keller's.
- The Writing Strategies table — reflective diction, figurative language, sensory language, allusion, shift in perception, theme.
- The Peer Review chart.
- The Adding/Deleting Information guidance.

**Used in:** §1 (the cold open is the Keller well-house scene), §3 (Keller as element example), §4 (Keller as the canonical scene-to-summary ratio), §5 (Keller's hinge moment), §6 (the assignment, rhetorical situation map, drafting strategy, peer review).

**Reshaped:** This is the module that needed the most reshaping. The source compresses many pedagogical moves into one ~7,500-word module. The rewrite breaks them into separate concept sections and rebuilds the rhetorical-situation map as a seven-element list rather than the source's seven-row table.

The Writing Strategies table (figurative language, sensory language, allusion, shift in perception, theme) was largely cut from the rewrite. Reasoning: the table reads as a checklist of techniques to apply, which the rewrite resists — techniques should serve the hinge moment, not be applied for their own sake. The voice in the rewrite teaches *scene vs. summary* and *the object-anchor technique* as the through-line; specific techniques (metaphor, allusion) are mentioned in passing where they earn it.

The Peer Review chart was compressed to a single paragraph in §6 — students will get the chart back in a more genre-appropriate place when the workshop assembles printables.

**Cut:**

- The full Peer Review chart (deferred to ancillary materials).
- The Writing Strategies table (techniques absorbed into the chapter's main voice).
- "Another Lens 1" (the interview-based alternative) — preserved as a one-line option in §6 but not expanded.
- All `[#](#)` broken cross-references.

## Hard-rule audit

- **No fabricated sources or citations.** All citations point to verifiable primary sources (Project Gutenberg, Library of Congress, UNESCO, NCTE, Random House, Aunt Lute Books).
- **Primary sources where possible.** Keller via Project Gutenberg, Douglass via NPS, Anzaldúa via cited publisher, Westover via cited publisher, Yousafzai via cited publisher.
- **Contestable factual claims linked.** UNESCO definition, NCTE definition, Douglass biographical detail, Keller text, and the DALN are all linked to primary or canonical sources.
- **Stripped jargon or taught it.** *Idiolect*, *literacy*, *rhetorical situation*, *hinge moment*, *scene/summary*. Each defined on first use with etymology or plain-English gloss.
- **Calibrated uncertainty.** The "What would change my mind" names the specific evidence that would revise the central claim. "Still puzzling" names what remains unresolved.
- **No expertise overreach.** No claims requiring composition-theory domain knowledge that exceeds the source's own treatment.
- **Showed the work.** Scene-vs-summary distinction is *demonstrated* with the failed "I have always been a reader" paragraph and its rewrite, not just asserted.
- **Method-on-method.** The chapter is reflexive — its claim that every literacy narrative has a hinge is explicitly opened to falsification.
- **Analogies earned.** The ship-in-fog metaphor is preserved because Keller herself used it; no new decorative metaphors invoked.
- **Benchmarks contextualized.** No benchmark or capability claims (n/a for this chapter).
